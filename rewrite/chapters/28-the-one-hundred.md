# Chapter 28: The One Hundred

Hyfe did not recruit. He asked.

He sat in the roofless great hall of the Winter Palace with the sky above him and Rodin on the rafter — the single crossbeam that spanned the hall's open ceiling, a structural concession to the ravens that nested there in winter — and he asked. Not for soldiers. Not for warriors. Not for the particular brand of disciplined violence that the Dhukkha Khan's empire manufactured the way other civilizations manufactured cloth.

"I am going into the Crater," he said. "I am looking for the heart of this planet. I need people who are willing to walk into a place they will not understand and who will not bring weapons because weapons are a way of refusing to understand."

The seekers listened. Snow fell through the open ceiling and melted on the basalt floor. Rodin watched from the rafter, black eyes holding their impossible depth.

"This is not an expedition. It is not a military operation. It is a pilgrimage. I cannot tell you what we will find. I cannot tell you that we will survive. I can tell you that the thing at the bottom of the Crater is the thing this planet was built to protect, and that the time for protection has ended and the time for meeting has begun."

He paused. The snow fell.

"One hundred. That is all I need. One hundred people who are willing to be changed."

---

They volunteered in ones and twos. Not immediately — Hyfe had not expected immediately. The decision to walk into the Crater was not the kind of decision that responded to rhetoric. It responded to silence. It responded to the three a.m. reckoning when the body was honest and the mind had run out of reasons to stay safe.

The monk came first. Taras — the one who had been expelled for asking questions his order could not answer. He found Hyfe in the great hall at dawn, before the others woke, and said, "I've been looking for the thing your Crater is hiding since before I knew it was hiding anything." And Hyfe said, "Good." And that was the conversation.

The physician came next. Dr. Serei — the one who had resigned from the Khan's medical corps. She brought a surgical kit and a knowledge of the Outsidelands' biological anomalies that was worth more than any weapon. She did not explain why she came. She did not need to. The look on her face when she set her kit on the great hall's floor was the look of a woman who had spent years treating the consequences of violence and had decided to walk toward the cause.

The cartographer. The deserters. A structural engineer from the border town of Keth who had spent her career building fortifications and wanted, for once, to build something that was not designed to keep people out. A pair of twins from the eastern province who communicated in a private language and who claimed — the court would have dismissed this; Hyfe did not — to have dreamed of the Crater's interior, of bioluminescent veins in the rock, of a warmth that pulsed with a rhythm they could feel in their chests.

They came from the edges. From the places the empire did not monitor because the empire did not value them. They were not heroes. They were not exceptional. They were the people who had looked at the Dhukkha Khan's world and found it insufficient — not morally, not politically, but ontologically. The world was not enough. The explanations were not enough. The story the empire told about itself — that power was the purpose and pain was the fuel and the Khan's body was the body of the state — was a story that left something out, and the something it left out was the thing they had come to find.

One hundred. It took twenty-three days.

On the twenty-fourth day, the messenger arrived from Shakya.

---

The message was diplomatic in form and revolutionary in content. The Shakyan Oracle — or rather, the Oracle's successor; the old Oracle had died in the winter, the cables beneath her chair going dark one by one like lights extinguished in sequence — had proposed an alliance. Not military. Not economic. A marriage.

Hyfe read the message in the great hall. Rodin perched on his wrist, an arrangement the raven preferred when Hyfe was seated — the shoulder for standing, the wrist for stillness. The bird's weight was precise and constant, like the weight of a thought you have chosen to carry.

The message named the bride. Anais Jian. Third daughter of the Jian lineage. Astromancer. Oracle-designate. She was going blind.

The message did not state this directly. It stated that Anais Jian possessed abilities relevant to the Crater expedition. It stated that her astromantic sight — the capacity to read the planet's electromagnetic field through its light — would be essential to navigating the Crater's interior, where conventional instruments failed and conventional senses deceived. It stated that the marriage would unite Shakya and the Khanate of Rime in a formal alliance that would provide political cover for the expedition.

What it did not state, and what Hyfe read between the lines with the precision of a man who had spent seven years reading the Outsidelands' silences: Anais was dying. Not of disease. Of sight. The astromantic gift consumed the eyes that channeled it, and Anais had channeled it with an intensity that had accelerated the consumption, and the Oracle's council wanted her away from the Astromancery before the gift consumed her entirely. The Crater expedition was a way to use the sight before it was gone. The marriage was a way to make the using look like statecraft.

Hyfe set the message down. He looked at Rodin. The raven looked back.

"She sees things," Hyfe said. "So do you."

The raven blinked. In the black eye, briefly, the image of a woman — dark-haired, pale-skinned, her eyes open and focused on something that was not in the room, not in the building, not on the visible spectrum. The image held for a breath. Then it was gone and the eye was an eye and the raven was a raven and Hyfe sat in the great hall with a diplomatic message in his hand and the snow falling through the open ceiling.

He wrote his acceptance in four words: *I will meet her.*

---

She arrived on the winter solstice, when the twin suns barely cleared the southern horizon and the daylight lasted three hours and the plateau was a study in blue and black — blue ice, black rock, the blue-black sky that never fully committed to day or night. She traveled with a small retinue: two Shakyan guards, an automaton that served as translator and attendant, and her sister.

Aharah came because Aharah always came. She stood behind Anais the way a wall stands behind a city — not for decoration but for the specific purpose of ensuring that whatever approached from that direction would have to go through her first. She wore her desert wrappings even in the cold, the sand-colored cloth incongruous against the ice, the gem-key at her wrist pulsing its blue-white pulse. She did not trust Hyfe. She did not trust the Winter Palace or the Khanate of Rime or any son of the Dhukkha Khan, living or dead or claiming to be either. She had come because Anais had asked, and Aharah's refusal had never survived Anais's asking.

Hyfe met them at the palace gate. No ceremony. No retinue of his own. He stood alone in the cold with Rodin on his shoulder and he watched them approach across the plateau — three figures and a bronze shape, small against the ice, moving toward him through the blue light of a sun that had barely risen.

Anais walked at the front. She walked without hesitation, without the careful, testing gait of a person navigating uncertain terrain. She walked as if she could see the ground perfectly, every crack and ice sheet and frost-heaved stone, and perhaps she could — not with her eyes, which were dimming, the retinal tissue thinning from the Astromancery's demands, but with the other sight, the electromagnetic one, the sense that read the planet's field the way a bat reads echoes.

She stopped three paces from Hyfe. She looked at him. The looking was not casual. It was the focused, entire attention of a person who had limited vision remaining and chose to spend it deliberately, the way a soldier spends ammunition when the supply cannot be replenished.

"You're not Apollyon," she said.

The plateau was silent. Aharah's hand moved to the caster at her hip. The guards shifted. The automaton's bronze eyes tracked the exchange.

Hyfe did not move. Rodin did not move.

"No," he said.

"You're his brother. The one who was eaten." A pause. She tilted her head — the gesture of someone listening to a frequency no one else could hear. "The one who chose not to break."

Hyfe looked at her. He looked at her the way he looked at everything — steadily, without urgency, with the monastic patience of a man who had learned that the important things arrived on their own schedule. And what he saw, looking at Anais Jian, was a recognition so immediate it bypassed the process of recognition entirely. He did not identify what he saw. He simply saw it.

Two people. Both broken. Both rebuilt. Both operating on a frequency the world around them could not receive.

"Yes," he said.

Anais extended her hand. Not as greeting — as test. Her fingers were cold. The sunskin on them was pale, barely visible, the faintest gold. Hyfe took the hand. His grip was warm. The contrast — her cold, his warmth — held for a moment, a small fact of temperature that meant nothing and meant everything.

"I can feel it," she said. "The Crater. From here, I can feel it. A heartbeat. Electromagnetic. Deep." She paused. "It's been calling me since I was eleven."

"I know," Hyfe said. "It's been calling me since I was nine."

Aharah stood behind them and watched and said nothing. The wind moved across the plateau. The blue light shifted. The raven watched from Hyfe's shoulder with its impossible eyes, and in those eyes, briefly, the reflection of a thing that had not happened yet: two figures at the mouth of the Crater, walking in.

---

The wedding was held in the roofless great hall on the third day after Anais's arrival.

Snow fell throughout the ceremony. Not a storm — a quiet fall, the flakes large and slow, descending through the open ceiling with the patience of things that have no urgency. They landed on the basalt floor and melted. They landed on the shoulders of the seekers who lined the hall's perimeter and did not melt, because the seekers were cold, because the hall was cold, because the Winter Palace existed in a state of permanent cold that was not discomfort but condition.

Anais wore white. Not the elaborate white of a Shakyan ceremonial gown — the simple white of undyed linen, a tunic that fell to her knees, belted with a cord. No veil. She had been offered one and declined. "I can barely see," she said. "Covering my eyes would be redundant." The statement was delivered without self-pity, without humor, in the flat, precise register of a person stating a measurement.

Hyfe wore dark robes. The grey of the Outsidelands, deepened to near-black by the winter light. His hands were bare. The chimera scars on his fingers were visible — white ridges against the brown skin, the particular scarring of tissue that had been damaged by something neither purely chemical nor purely mechanical.

The ceremony was Shakyan in form, abbreviated. A monk — Taras, the expelled one, who had retained his ordination if not his order's approval — spoke the words. The words were old. They predated the Oracle, predated the Temple, predated perhaps the terraforming itself, belonging to a stratum of language so deep it might have been loaded onto the Pacemaker's cultural database from a world that no longer existed. The words said: *two become one, and the one walks forward.* They did not say love. They did not say forever. They said forward, and the direction was the vow.

Hyfe and Anais stood facing each other. Between them, the snow fell. Above them, the sky — grey, low, the clouds moving slowly, as if the weather were observing the ceremony with the same quiet attention the seekers brought to everything in the Winter Palace.

Rodin sat on the rafter. The raven had not descended for the ceremony, had not taken its customary position on Hyfe's shoulder, and the absence was noted by those who knew the bird's habits. It sat above them, black against the grey sky, its eyes moving between the two figures below with the attention of something that was not merely watching but recording.

Hyfe took Anais's hands. Cold and warm. Pale and brown. The Shakyan monk's words hung in the air like the snow — suspended, turning, settling.

"I see you," Anais said. It was not part of the ceremony. It was the thing beneath the ceremony — the private sentence that the public words were built to carry.

"I know," Hyfe said. "What do you see?"

"A man who was emptied and chose to fill himself with something other than what emptied him."

Hyfe was quiet. The snow fell between them. Rodin watched from the rafter.

"And I see a woman," he said, "who is giving away her eyes so the rest of us can find our way."

Anais smiled. The smile was small and precise and it did not reach her eyes, because her eyes were busy — receiving, processing, reading the electromagnetic field that hummed through the Winter Palace's stone and the plateau's rock and the Crater's depths beneath both. The smile reached somewhere else. Somewhere deeper.

Taras spoke the final words. The ceremony ended. No applause, no celebration, no feast — the Winter Palace did not accommodate excess. The seekers returned to their work. The snow continued. Hyfe and Anais stood in the great hall for a moment longer, hands joined, the silence between them not empty but complete, the way a finished sentence is complete, requiring nothing added.

From the hall's western entrance, Aharah watched. She leaned against the stone doorframe with her arms crossed and her face held in the careful neutrality she adopted when she did not trust her expressions to behave. She did not approve. Not of Hyfe — Hyfe, against every expectation, she almost trusted, because the calm in him was real and she had learned to distinguish real calm from performed calm in the way a soldier distinguishes live rounds from blanks. What she did not approve of was the direction. The Crater. The expedition. The fact that her youngest sister — blind, brilliant, irreplaceable — was about to walk into the most dangerous place on the planet beside a man who was technically a dead person using his dead brother's name.

But Anais had chosen this. Anais chose everything with a clarity that made argument feel like wind pushing against a cliff. And Aharah had promised, in a garden beneath a fig tree in a city that had burned, that they would fight together. Together meant following even when the path went underground.

She turned from the doorframe and walked back through the corridor. Her footsteps were sharp on the stone. The gem-key pulsed at her wrist. The cold pressed against her skin and the sunskin responded, a faint gold warmth, the body's ancient negotiation with whatever light was available.

---

In the weeks that followed, Anais's vision continued to dim.

She told Hyfe on a night when the aurora blazed above the plateau — a phenomenon caused by the Pacemaker's buried systems interacting with Hassan's magnetic field, a curtain of green and violet light that moved across the sky with the slow deliberation of something alive. They sat on the palace's northern wall, legs dangling over the edge, the Crater's darkness below them and the aurora's light above, and Anais said: "I'm going blind."

She said it the way she said everything — precisely, without ornamentation, a datum delivered to a person she trusted to receive it without distortion.

Hyfe was quiet. The aurora shifted. The green deepened.

"What do you see instead?" he asked.

Anais paused. The pause was not hesitation. It was the pause of a person selecting from a vocabulary that did not yet contain the word she needed, improvising with the closest available materials.

"More," she said.

Hyfe waited. The aurora moved.

"The visible spectrum is a sliver. You know this. Light is a narrow band — a crack in a door. What the astromancy is doing is opening the door. The visible light goes because the crack is widening. I'm losing the sliver but gaining the room." She turned to him. Her eyes, in the aurora's green light, were luminous and damaged, the retinal tissue thinning to translucence, the irises holding a depth that was not natural but earned. "I can feel the Crater from here. Not see it — feel it. A pulse. Electromagnetic. The frequency of the Pacemaker's heartbeat. It's been beating since before anyone on this planet was born, and I can feel each beat the way you feel your own heart. Not because I'm listening. Because I'm becoming the thing that listens."

Hyfe looked at her. The aurora painted them both in moving light. Rodin sat between them on the wall, the raven's feathers catching the green and violet in iridescent patterns that shifted as the light shifted.

"The One Hundred will need a navigator," Hyfe said.

"I know."

"Someone who can read the Crater's interior without instruments. Without sight."

"I know."

"You're that person."

"I've always been that person. I was born for this the way you were broken for it. Different damage, same direction."

The aurora faded. The sky darkened. The Crater below them breathed its silver plumes. They sat on the wall in the cold and the quiet and between them, on the stone, Rodin's talons clicked once — a small, precise sound, like a period at the end of a sentence.

---

The One Hundred gathered at the plateau's edge on the morning of the spring equinox.

The twin suns rose together — a rare alignment, Aleph and Beth sharing the horizon, their combined light turning the ice field gold. The Winter Palace stood behind them, austere and beautiful against the black rock, its roofless great hall open to the sky like a mouth in mid-sentence. Before them: the descent. The slope from the plateau into the Outsidelands' outer margin, where the permafrost gave way to the mutated terrain of the Crater's influence zone. Beyond that: the Crater itself.

They carried minimal supplies. Water. Rations for thirty days. Medical kits — Dr. Serei had assembled these personally, calibrating each one for the Outsidelands' particular biological hazards. No weapons. Hyfe had been clear. The seekers had accepted this the way they accepted everything in the Winter Palace — through a silence that was not submission but recognition. They were not going to fight. They were going to meet.

Hyfe stood at the line's front. Beside him, Anais. She wore the same white linen from the wedding, layered now against the cold, her hair bound, her eyes open and focused on something beyond the visible horizon. The gem-key that Aharah had once carried — passed to Anais in a private ceremony that the novel did not witness — pulsed at her wrist. It pulsed in time with the Crater's heartbeat. It had been pulsing in that rhythm since the moment Anais set foot on the plateau, as if the device had recognized its destination.

Rodin circled above. The raven had taken flight at dawn and had not returned to Hyfe's shoulder, choosing instead to wheel in wide, slow arcs over the assembled company, black wings catching the twin suns' gold light, the blue-green iridescence of its feathers painting shifting patterns against the sky. From above, the One Hundred would have looked like a congregation. From below, like a procession. From the raven's eye — which saw, perhaps, from all directions at once — they looked like what they were: a hundred people standing at the edge of a descent, having chosen to go down.

Hyfe did not speak. He had said everything that needed saying in the great hall, twenty-three days ago, when he asked for one hundred people willing to be changed. The time for speaking had passed. The time for walking had arrived.

He took the first step. Anais took it beside him. Behind them, one by one, the seekers followed — the monk, the physician, the cartographer, the deserters, the engineer, the twins, and all the others who had come to the Winter Palace seeking something the empire could not provide and who had found, in Hyfe's calm and Anais's sight, a direction.

They walked toward the Crater.

Behind them, the Winter Palace. Its stone walls caught the equinox light and held it. Its roofless great hall stood open to the sky. The snow had melted in the night and the basalt floor was dark and wet and clean, a black mirror reflecting the clouds.

Ahead: the Outsidelands. The mutated terrain. The chimeras. The microwave anomalies. The Crater's depth. The heartbeat in the planet's core.

Rodin circled above them, and the raven's shadow crossed and recrossed the line of walkers, and the shadow was small and the walkers were small and the Crater ahead was vast, and the procession moved forward into the space between the known and the unknown with the steady, unhurried gait of people who had stopped running from the question and had chosen, instead, to walk toward the answer.

The twin suns rose. The ice turned gold. The Winter Palace fell behind them. And the Crater opened its mouth and waited.
