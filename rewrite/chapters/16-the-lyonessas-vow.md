# Chapter 16: The Lyonessa's Vow

The night before the ordeal, Aharah did not sleep.

She lay in her bed until the household sounds died — Auntie's joints clicking down the hall in her evening rounds, the splash of water as Anais washed her face, the measured silence from Nala's room that was not sleep but the absence of anything that could be overheard. When the third bell sounded from the Temple spire and the sentries changed their positions on the wall, Aharah rose.

She dressed in the dark. The training wrap, torn and rewrapped so many times it had taken the shape of her body. Sandals laced tight. She left her hair unbound. She took nothing else.

The hallway was stone and silence. She moved through it the way she moved through everything — low, fast, following the wall with her fingertips. Past the automaton at the eastern door, its bronze face catching the moonlight in a green flash. Past the courtyard where the fountain sang its eternal idiot phrase. Past the kitchen where Auntie sat powered down in her chair, her chest rising and falling in an imitation of breath that no one had programmed but that she had learned, somehow, from watching the family sleep.

The city streets were empty. The cypress trees stood like sentinels, their shadows straight and black in the moonlight. The air smelled of night-blooming jasmine and the faint mineral tang of the underground springs. In the distance, the Maya barrier shimmered — restless, alive, a wall of light that looked like heat and functioned like forgetting.

Aharah walked toward the Temple.

She did not know why. That was a lie. She knew why. She had known for three days, since the morning Nala announced her selection, since she watched Nala's hands shake around a water skin in the training yard. The knowledge sat in her chest like a stone — heavy, precise, immovable. If Nala entered the ordeal chamber, the hala in her marrow would react to the gene-treatment. The Lyonessa sequence would find the poison and the poison would find the sequence and her sister's body would become a war fought on a cellular level. No healer in Maya could stop it. No automaton had the protocol.

Nala would die.

And Nala knew. Aharah was certain of it — had seen the knowledge in Nala's posture, in the military straightness of her spine that was not discipline but armor, in the way she ate her meals as if each one were practice for eating, in the flatness of her voice when she spoke the formal words: *I am honored by the Temple's selection.* Nala knew and she would do it anyway, because the ordeal was duty, and duty was the structure that held Nala together, and without it she was just a girl with poison in her bones and a dead mother and a father who had loved her mother second.

Aharah reached the Temple steps. She sat.

The Temple rose above her: tier upon tier of pale sandstone, each level narrower than the last, carved with script so old the glyphs had smoothed to suggestions. At its apex, the gem pulsed — slow, rhythmic, blue-white — like a heart made of light. The ordeal chamber was deep inside, below ground level, accessible only through the Hall of Names where every Lyonessa who had ever lived was inscribed on the walls in letters of hammered gold. Aharah had been inside once, as a child, holding Auntie's hand. She remembered the weight of those names pressing down.

She sat on the steps and she thought about what she was going to do.

It was wrong. She knew it was wrong the way she knew the wall was forty cubits high — as a fact of physics, a measurement that existed independent of her feelings about it. The ordeal was Nala's. The selection was Nala's. To take it from her was theft, and worse than theft — it was the erasure of Nala's choice, even if that choice was to die. People had the right to their own destruction. Aharah believed this. She believed it firmly and completely and it made no difference whatsoever because her sister's hands shook and her sister would die and Aharah could not sit in the dark and let it happen.

She was twelve years old and she was not yet fluent in the language of moral complexity, but she understood the grammar: some choices could not be made correctly. You made them anyway. You carried what followed.

The voice came from the Temple.

Not from the steps or the walls or the gem at the apex. From inside — deeper, lower, as if the building itself were speaking through its stone. Not words, exactly. A pressure. A direction. Like a hand placed on the back of her head, gently turning her gaze.

*The girl with the hala will not survive.*

Aharah's breath stopped.

*The sequence will find the poison. The poison will answer. She will die on the table with the gene half-written, and what comes out will be neither Lyonessa nor human. It will suffer. It will last three days.*

"Who are you?" Aharah whispered.

The voice did not answer the question. The voice was not interested in questions.

*You carry no hala. Your marrow is clean. Your sunskin will metabolize the treatment faster than standard — there will be more pain, but you will survive. The gene will take.*

"I'm twelve."

*The gene does not count years.*

"The Oracle will—"

*The Oracle will do what the Oracle does. This is not about the Oracle.*

The voice stopped. The pressure lifted. Aharah sat on the Temple steps with the jasmine-scented air pressing against her skin and the twin moons carving shadows on the stone and the knowledge that she had been spoken to by something she could not name, something that lived in the Temple or under it or through it, something that was not God and not Mater and not any of the powers the Hazzan invoked in his morning prayers.

She sat for a long time.

Then she stood and went to find Nala's badge.

---

The badge was in Nala's room, on the table beside her bed. A disc of black stone with the Temple seal pressed into its face: the Lyonessa glyph, the gene spiral, the name NAHLAH JIAN in script so small it could only be read by touch. Nala slept on her back with her arms at her sides, as if even in sleep she maintained formation. Her face was still. Her hands, resting on the blanket, trembled.

Aharah took the badge. She placed her thumb over Nala's name. The stone was warm.

She left through the window.

---

The Hall of Names was not guarded. It did not need to be. The door responded to the badge — a click, a hum, the stone sliding sideways on mechanisms that no one living understood. Inside, the golden names covered every surface: walls, ceiling, the pillars that held the weight of the Temple above. Thousands of names. Centuries of women who had entered this hall whole and walked out changed.

Or had not walked out at all.

Aharah passed through the hall and descended. The stairs were cut into the bedrock — narrow, spiraling, lit by strips of cold light embedded in the walls that activated at her presence and faded behind her. The air grew cooler. The stone grew damp. She could hear something below — not the voice again, but a hum. Mechanical. Ancient. The sound of machines still performing their function in the dark, faithfully, without maintenance, without operators, without anyone to tell them that the civilization they served had forgotten how to speak their language.

The ordeal chamber was a room of white stone.

Circular. Low-ceilinged. In its center, a table — not metal, not stone, something between, warm to the touch. Around the table, mechanisms she could not name: arms of jointed bronze, lenses that tracked her movement with a faint whirr, reservoirs of pale liquid connected by tubing that pulsed with its own rhythm. The gem-key sat in a recess in the table's surface — small, no larger than a thumbnail, glowing the same blue-white as the gem atop the Temple.

Aharah placed the badge on the table. The machines hummed louder. The bronze arms adjusted, recalibrated. A panel of light appeared in the air before her — script she could mostly read, old formal Shakyan, the language of the Temple rites:

*CANDIDATE: NAHLAH JIAN*
*LINEAGE: CONFIRMED*
*ORDEAL: LYONESSA SEQUENCE IV*
*PROCEED?*

Aharah pressed her hand to the panel. The script flickered. Changed.

*BIOMETRIC MISMATCH. CANDIDATE IS NOT NAHLAH JIAN.*

A pause. The machines quieted. Then:

*CANDIDATE: AHARAH JIAN*
*LINEAGE: CONFIRMED*
*HALA MARKERS: NONE*
*AGE: TWELVE STANDARD*
*WARNING: CANDIDATE IS BELOW RECOMMENDED THRESHOLD*
*PROCEED?*

She lay down on the table. She pressed her hand flat against the panel.

*PROCEEDING.*

What followed was not describable in the language Aharah possessed. Later she would try. She would fail. The closest she came was this: imagine the moment after a fall, when the body has struck the ground but the pain has not yet arrived — that held breath, that silence — and then stretch it across hours. The gene-treatment entered her bloodstream and began to read her. Not her thoughts, not her memories, but her architecture — the cellular scaffolding, the bone lattice, the protein chains that made her body a body. It read her the way a mason reads a wall: looking for load-bearing structures, for fault lines, for the places where new weight could be hung without collapse.

The pain came in waves. Not sharp — deep. The kind of pain that has geography: plains of white heat across her ribs, mountain ridges of pressure along her spine, and at the center of it, in the marrow of her bones, a rewriting. The old script being overwritten with new. She felt the Lyonessa gene threading itself into her — not replacing what was there but braiding with it, a second strand wound through the first, doubling the weave.

Her sunskin blazed. The photosynthetic pigment, activated by the treatment's energy, lit her body from within — gold so bright the white stone walls turned amber, gold that poured from her skin like liquid, gold that the machines tracked with their whirring lenses as if recording something they had not seen before. She burned and she did not burn up.

The gem-key rose from its recess. It moved through the air — slowly, deliberately, as if considering — and pressed itself against the inside of her left wrist. The pain of its insertion was a single clean note in the symphony of agony: a sharp, pure thing, almost welcome for its clarity. She felt it settle against the bone, felt the skin close over it, felt the faint blue-white pulse begin.

The vow wrote itself on the air before her. Old script. Older than the Hall of Names, older than the Temple, perhaps older than Maya itself. She read it through the haze of pain:

*YOU ARE LYONESSA.*
*YOU ARE BOUND.*
*YOU WILL NOT RETURN UNTIL THE TASK IS COMPLETE.*
*THIS IS THE PRICE. THIS IS THE GIFT.*
*THEY ARE THE SAME.*

She tried to speak. Her voice was ash. She nodded.

The machines withdrew. The light dimmed. Aharah lay on the table in the ordeal chamber, twelve years old, her body rewritten, her sunskin fading from blaze to glow, the gem-key pulsing in her wrist like a second heartbeat.

She did not move for a long time.

---

Dawn came to Maya the way it always came — the twin suns rising in sequence, Aleph first, a white blade along the eastern ridge, then Beth, smaller, redder, following like a wound. The light struck the Temple spire and split into colors. The fountains caught the gold. The cypress trees threw their shadows westward. The city woke.

Nala woke. She reached for the badge. The table was empty.

For a long moment she stood in her room with her hand on the place where the badge had been, and her face was a mask that no expression could penetrate. Then she went to the window and looked down at the Temple steps, and she saw Aharah climbing them — slowly, favoring her left side, her training wrap dark with sweat, her sunskin dim and mottled where the treatment had scorched the pigment into new patterns. The gem-key pulsed at her wrist.

Nala did not scream. She did not weep. She did not go to the Temple steps and take her sister by the shoulders and shake her until the theft reversed itself. She stood at the window and she looked down and her face did the thing that Anais would later describe as volcanic and silent — a catastrophe contained, magma held beneath the surface by an act of will so total it deformed the landscape above it.

She said nothing.

She went to the washbasin. She took the blade she used for her practice sword's maintenance — short, sharp, functional. She gathered her hair in one hand. She cut it at the jaw. The black weight of it fell into the basin like a living thing surrendering. She looked at herself in the mirror: shorn, stripped, unfamiliar.

Then she dressed. Military blacks. Boots instead of sandals. She walked out of the Jian household without speaking to anyone — past Auntie, who stopped mid-motion and said "Child?" to her retreating back; past Anais, who stood in the hallway with her hands over her mouth; past the automaton at the eastern door, which tracked her passage with its bronze eyes and did nothing.

She walked to the military quarter. She enlisted.

---

The schism split Maya like a blade through fruit.

By midday, the city knew. The Hazzan's middle granddaughter had stolen the Lyonessa ordeal from her elder sister. She had entered the Temple under a false badge. She had undergone the treatment at twelve — younger than any Lyonessa in the Hall of Names. She was alive. She was changed. She was wrong.

Half of Maya called her hero. They gathered in the fountain square and spoke her name with a reverence that Aharah, lying in Auntie's care with her body aching in places she hadn't known existed, could not hear and would not have wanted. *She saved her sister,* they said. *She saw the hala and she acted. She is what a Lyonessa should be — not obedient but brave. Not chosen but choosing.*

The other half called her thief. They gathered at the Temple steps and burned incense and spoke the formal prayers of expiation, and the Oracle — the Temple's keeper, the interpreter of Mater's will, a woman so old her sunskin had faded to a pale umber — came to the Jian household in person.

"The ordeal was not yours to take," the Oracle said.

Aharah was sitting up in bed. Her body hurt in a way she had not known hurt could be — not the sharp hurt of scraped knees or the dull hurt of exhaustion but a structural hurt, a deep-bone ache, as if her skeleton were settling into a new shape and every adjustment cost. The gem-key at her wrist pulsed steadily. She could feel it communicating with something — the Temple, the machines, the network of systems that undergirded Maya's technology like roots beneath a forest floor.

"No," Aharah said. "It wasn't."

"You have committed a crime against the Temple. Against tradition. Against your sister."

"Yes."

"You do not deny it."

"There's nothing to deny. I did it."

The Oracle studied her. The old woman's eyes were the color of sand after rain — pale, translucent, as if the pigment had worn away and left only the seeing. "The punishment for ordeal theft is exile," she said. "The vow you took binds you. You cannot return to Maya until your task is complete."

"What task?"

"The Tagata."

The word landed in the room like a stone in water. Auntie, standing in the corner, went still. Her bronze face — that green, oxidized, endlessly expressive face — registered something Anais, watching from the doorway, had never seen on it before. Fear.

"The Tagata is a myth," Auntie said carefully.

"The Tagata is a state," the Oracle corrected. "A being. An enlightenment. Mater fears it. The Temple seeks it. You—" she turned back to Aharah "—will find it."

"And if I don't?"

"Then you don't return."

The Oracle left. The room was quiet. Aharah looked at the gem-key in her wrist: blue-white, steady, a pulse that would accompany her for the rest of her life. She was twelve years old and she had been given an impossible task by a woman who wanted her gone, and the task was dressed in the language of honor but it was exile, plain and clean.

She understood this. She did not pretend otherwise.

"Auntie," she said. "Where do I start?"

Auntie's chest rose and fell in her imitation of breath. Her joints clicked. "The Vermillion Desert," she said, and the fear in her bronze face deepened. "The Tagata Rock. It is — or it was — the last place the Tagata was seen. Forty-four days' walk from Maya, through the red sands."

"Then I walk."

---

She left at dawn the next day.

The household came to see her off: Auntie, who pressed a canteen of water into her hands and held her face between her green-bronze palms and said nothing for a long time; the Hazzan, who stood at the gate with his hands clasped behind his back and his face arranged in the expression of formal sorrow that protocol demanded and that might, Aharah thought, contain something real beneath it; the cousins, the servants, the neighbors who lined the street.

Nala did not come.

Aharah walked the stone road toward the Maya barrier. Her body ached. The sunskin pulsed faintly in the morning light — altered now, the patterns disrupted by the ordeal, darker in some places and absent in others, like a map that had been redrawn. The gem-key hummed at her wrist. She carried a sword on her back — one of the old ones, good steel, balanced for a hand smaller than hers. A caster on her forearm: dark, dormant, its power cell at forty percent and no way to recharge it. Water for eight days. Dried meat and bread for four.

The Maya barrier shimmered before her. She could see through it — the desert beyond, red and vast and patient. She had never left Maya. She had never left the only world she knew.

She stepped through the shimmer. It parted for her — the blood-key in her marrow singing to the barrier's frequency — and for a moment she stood in both places at once: the city behind, the desert ahead, the shimmer between like a membrane of light.

"Aharah."

She turned. Anais stood just inside the barrier. Small. Eight years old. Her hands at her sides, her face doing the thing that Anais's face did when she was holding something too large for her body — the eyes wide, the jaw tight, the effort of containment visible in every line.

"I sent that dream to you," Anais said. "The one about the burning city." Her voice was steady. It should not have been. "It's coming."

Aharah looked at her sister. The youngest. The strange one. The one who dreamed of blue planets and burning cities and who spoke of these things as if reporting the weather.

"I know," Aharah said.

She turned. She walked into the desert. The Maya closed behind her like water closing over a stone, and the City of Maya vanished — replaced, for anyone watching from outside, by ruins. Collapsed walls. Dead wells. Sand reclaiming stone.

Anais stood inside the shimmer and watched her sister disappear.
