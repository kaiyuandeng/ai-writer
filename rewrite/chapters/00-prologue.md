# Prologue: Father, I Fear

---

I'm afraid.

What is it that you fear, Son of Man?

I'm afraid of everything.
I fear death.
I fear time passing.
I fear being left behind.
I fear violence.
I fear the masses and the crowd.
I fear misunderstanding.
I fear Mah and Bah aging.
Friends leaving.
I fear talent wasted —
	what could have been —
	who I could have become.

I fear my hands shaking.
I fear the tremor that starts in the left one,
	the one I write with,
	the one that holds the pen that holds the line
	that holds me to the earth.
I fear the body failing before the work is done.
The knees that crack on stairs.
The breath that catches where it didn't used to catch.
I fear the mirror.
Not vanity — recognition.
The slow stranger assembling himself
	in the glass each morning,
	one new line, one new shadow,
	until the face I knew is gone
	and some tired country remains.

I fear the teeth loosening.
I fear the back that locks in the cold.
I fear the morning I reach for a word —
	a word I've used ten thousand times —
	and it isn't there.
Just a hole in the air
	where language used to be.

I fear the new Horsemen. Sickness.
Scarcity.
Poverty.
War.
I fear war…
I fear the drones that see but do not see.
I fear the missile that arrives before its sound.
I fear the children in rubble
	who look like children I have held.
I fear the news scroll.
The way atrocity fits in the palm of a hand now,
	between the weather and a recipe,
	and I keep scrolling.
I fear what the scrolling has done to me.
I fear I am learning to look away
	and calling it survival.

I fear machines that think.
I fear the hum beneath the server farms,
	the quiet appetite of algorithms
	that learn my voice, my syntax, my particular grief,
	and speak it back to me
	flawless
	and empty.
I fear being replaced —
	not by a better man
	but by no man at all.
I fear the hand that writes this
	is already obsolete,
	that the page I bleed on
	will be generated in a quarter-second
	by something that has never bled.
I fear the world will forget
	that a throat can break on a word,
	that sweat falls on the page,
	that error is holy.

I fear love that doesn't last.
I fear the afternoon it simply stops —
	not with a door slamming
	but with a quiet,
	the way a violin string goes slack
	when no one is watching.
I fear the text unreturned.
The bed cooling on one side.
I fear tenderness mistaken for weakness,
	and weakness mistaken for tenderness,
	and never knowing which I am offering.
I fear I will love wrong,
	love too much,
	love in the old way
	when the old way is no longer spoken.

I fear I am a dialect
	with no remaining speakers.
I fear the last person who understands me
	is someone I haven't met yet,
	or someone I've already lost.

That is all human of you.

I fear what I cannot name.
I fear the three a.m. visitor —
	the one who sits on my chest
	and asks, *What have you built?*
	and I have no answer,
	only a stack of pages
	and a room of drafts
	and a silence so complete
	it has a texture,
	like wool,
	like ash.
I fear the Sunday afternoon
	when the light is golden and kind
	and I still cannot feel it.
I fear joy, Pà.
I fear it because it always leaves
	and the leaving is worse
	than never having it.
I fear the gap between the life I imagined
	and the life I have.
I fear the gap is me.

I fear my inner dark saboteur will finally get the best of me.
I fear that I have only one more battle left in me —
	If that.
I fear change.
I fear staying the same.
I fear that change and staying the same
	are the same thing,
	and I am caught in the jaw of it,
	turning and turning
	and calling it movement.

Go on.

I fear the country I come from
	will not be the country I return to.
I fear the language shifting beneath my feet.
I fear the old songs will lose their meaning.
I fear the young ones will not know
	what was lost.

I fear the ocean.
Not the water itself
	but what it carries —
	the ships that crossed it,
	the bodies it swallowed,
	the distance it enforces
	between the living and the gone.
A ship, somewhere, is always leaving.
And I am always on the dock.

I fear, if I give it my all, I'll die.
My brothers have left the field,
given up to their wounds,
or gone home to comfort and safety.
Meanwhile, I have fought for nothing.
Am to have nothing.
I'll have wasted my life —
	I fear I have taken the wrong path.

I fear the path was never a path
	but a clearing I mistook for direction.
I fear the map was drawn by someone
	who never walked the ground.
I fear the ground itself.
I fear it will open.

I fear I am too soft for this century.
I fear I am too hard for the ones I love.
I fear the space between soft and hard
	is where I live,
	and it is no wider than a breath,
	and I spend my whole life balancing there,
	arms out,
	ridiculous,
	holy,
	alone.

Speak out your fears, Dear One.

I try, Pà. Yet I still fear.

Then speak even so. Speak afraid. That is the only honest way.

I fear —

I fear —

…

…

…

Son?

…

The voice stops.
Not a dramatic silence.
Not a protest.
Just — the well runs dry.
The mouth is open but nothing climbs out.
The fears have stacked so high
	they have become a wall
	and the wall has become a room
	and the room has no door.

He sits in it.

Pà waits.

He does not prompt. He does not lean in.
He does not fill the silence with wisdom
	or comfort or explanation.
He is not uncomfortable.
He is not patient the way humans are patient —
	straining against the clock,
	waiting for permission to speak.
He is patient the way stone is patient.
The way a riverbed is patient.
The way the dark side of the moon is patient —
	always there,
	never asking to be seen.
Something the water moves through
	and cannot exhaust.

A minute passes.
An hour.
A year.
He does not measure.
He is not made of minutes.

The silence holds.
It holds the way a cupped hand holds water —
	not forever,
	but long enough.

And Pà sits in the silence the way night sits in a valley:
	complete,
	unhurried,
	making room for whatever comes.

The Son's breathing is shallow.
Then slower.
Then something almost like rest —
	not sleep,
	but the body remembering
	that breath is involuntary,
	that the lungs will keep going
	even when the will stops.

And somewhere in that stillness,
	a small thing stirs.
Not a thought. Not a word.
A warmth.
The kind that starts behind the sternum
	and spreads the way ink spreads in water —
	slow, irreversible,
	becoming everything it touches.

…

Pà?

Yes.

I thought you left.

I don't leave. That is not something I do.

Everyone leaves.

That is true of everyone. It is not true of me.

How do I know?

You don't. That is what the word *trust* is for.

…

I fear — I fear I've been speaking to an empty room.
All these years. All these prayers.
Just my own voice bouncing back off the plaster.
I fear you are a story I tell myself
	to survive the night.
I fear the night is all there is.
I fear the morning is just the night
	wearing a different face.

Even that fear I will hold for you. Even the fear of me. Especially that one.

I fear the stars will go out.
I fear entropy. The long cooling.
I fear that everything — every kiss,
	every page written in fever,
	every child's first word,
	every trembling yes —
	is just heat
	leaving a system
	that never cared for it.

I fear the universe is indifferent.
I fear it is not even indifferent —
	that indifference requires a subject,
	and there is no subject,
	only expansion,
	only distance,
	only the long red yawn of light
	stretching toward nothing.

I fear twin suns setting on a world
	that no one remembers.
I fear beauty with no witness.
A blind girl reaching for a face
	that has already turned away.
I fear the last musician
	playing to a room of dust,
	a ship drifting with no harbor,
	a signal sent that no one receives.
I fear the last sound
	will be a mechanical hum
	and not a human voice.

I fear the end of tenderness.

That is the largest fear.

Yes.

And the truest.

Yes.

You have carried it a long time.

All my life. Since the first morning I woke
	and understood that waking was not guaranteed.
Since the first funeral — my grandmother
	in a box too small for everything she was.
Since the first time I saw Mah cry
	and understood she was not infinite.
Since the first friend who stopped calling
	and I stood in the kitchen
	holding the phone
	with nothing on the other end
	but the low electric patience of a dial tone
	and my own small breathing.

Since the first time I wrote a sentence
	and felt the ground shift underneath it
	and knew — this is dangerous,
	this is alive,
	and it will cost me everything.

You have carried it faithfully.

It doesn't feel like faith.

What does it feel like?

Like drowning in a room full of air.
Like being the last speaker of a language.
Like tuning a violin
	that no one has asked me to play.

And yet you tune it.

And yet I tune it.

Why?

Because — because if I stop,
	the note dies.
	And I cannot be the one
	who lets the note die.

That is not fear. That is something else.

What is it?

You will learn its name. Not tonight. But soon.

Tell me your greatest fear.

(I am alone)
Help does not come.

You're never alone.
Forces great and small,
visible and invisible,
hold and carry you.
But you must ask.
Merely ask.

I'm asking.

I know.

I hear you. I have always heard you — before the words, during the silence, after the silence. I hear what you cannot say. I hear the shape of it in your chest, the weight of it in your jaw. I hear it the way soil hears rain: not with ears, but with the whole self, open and ready and aching to receive.

You think you are the one speaking. But I have been listening since before you had language. I was listening when you were a pulse in your mother's body, no bigger than a lentil, already stubborn, already fighting. I was listening when you cried for no reason and every reason at the same age. I was listening in the years you turned from me, and I did not turn from you. I have never turned from you.

I do not fix, Son. That is not what I am for. I am not a repairman. I am not a strategist. I am the space in which the breaking happens. I am the air around the shatter. I am what remains when the room empties and the guests go home and the plates are still on the table and the candles have burned to wax pools and the silence comes — that particular silence, the one that lives under all other silences — and you sit in it and think you are alone.

You are not alone in that silence. You are *held* in it. The way a seed is held in dark earth. The way a note is held in the body of a violin before the bow touches the string.

You asked me for something. Ask again.

Pà… tell me a story.
Something to bulwark my spirit
against the disturbances of the day
and the poltergeists of night.
Something to prepare me —
for one last fight.

One last fight?

One last fight.

You have said this before. Three times. Four. You always find another.

This time is different.

It is always different. That is what makes it the same. But I hear you. You are tired in a way that sleep cannot mend. You are tired in the bones. In the wanting. In the hoping. You are tired of hoping.

I'm tired, Pà.

I know.

I'm so tired.

I know. But tired is not finished. Tired is a station. You rest there. You do not build a house.

Tell me the story. Please.

And the one you fight for — what is her name?

Her name is —

Say it.

Her name is —

The syllables come slowly.
Like a hand unclenching.
Like a first breath after a long dive.

Her name is —

The way water remembers the shape of the vessel.

Her name is —

I cannot —

You can. You already have. In every page you have ever written, her name was there — woven into the sentences, hidden in the rhythm, pressed between the words like a flower dried in a book you forgot you owned. In every fear you have spoken tonight, she was the thing behind the fear — not the source of it, but the reason it mattered. You were never afraid of dying. You were afraid of dying before you reached her.

Yes.

Then go, Son of Man…
Toward the Daughter of Dawn…

Do not go without fear.
Go *with* it.
Let it walk beside you like a second shadow.
Let it sharpen you.
The blade that has never known the whetstone
	is not a blade.
	It is just metal with ambition.

Your wish is granted.

Let me tell you a familiar story —
	a story that is so very, very old,
	yet so very new.

It begins the way all true stories begin:
	with someone afraid
	who moves forward anyway.

It begins with a planet that has known too much war
	and not enough singing.
It begins with a violin in a velvet case
	and a girl who cannot see
	and twin suns that rise amber and rose
	over a sea that has swallowed
	more prayers than ships.

It begins with a name.
The name is old. The name is a place.
The name is a wound and a promise
	stitched together with the same thread.

It is the story of Love in the time of the Pain Lord,
on an old planet called Hassan.

Now listen.
