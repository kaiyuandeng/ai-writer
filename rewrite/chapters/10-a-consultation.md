# Chapter 10: A Consultation

The hovercraft held a thousand feet above the desert floor, engines so quiet the cabin might have been a room suspended in nothing. Below, the Mojave stretched black and featureless under a moon that had not yet set. Ahead, Las Vegas burned on the horizon — not with fire but with the particular radiance of a city that had learned to make light do the work of gravity, holding its architecture aloft on columns of photon and plasma. The floating hotels turned slowly above the old Strip like lanterns hung from an invisible ceiling.

Elvira sat with a blanket drawn to her chin. The fabric was cashmere — she could tell from the weight, the way it held warmth without trapping heat. Expensive. Everything about Nolen Dawn was expensive in that way: the cost absorbed into the material so thoroughly you forgot someone had paid. She watched the desert through the curved glass. Nothing moved out there. The sand had been scoured by wind into long parallel ridges that caught the moonlight and threw it back in thin silver lines, like the strings of an instrument no one was playing.

Nolen Dawn sat across from her.

He was a large man. He had been larger once — she could see it in the way the suit jacket hung from his shoulders, the fabric remembering a frame that had since contracted. His hands rested on his knees. The knuckles were thick, gnarled, the joints swollen with bone spurs that caught the cabin's dim light like flint. His right hand tremored. The left was still, but the stillness had cost him something — she could see it in the tendons of his wrist, the white-knuckled effort of holding steady. He breathed through his nose. Each exhale fogged faintly in the cooled air.

He had not spoken since they lifted off.

The silence between them was not empty. It was the silence of two people who know the same thing and have agreed, without discussing it, not to say it yet. Elvira had been in this hovercraft for forty minutes. She had been out of the room where they'd kept her for less than six hours. Her wrists still showed the marks — faint pressure lines, already fading, where the restraints had been. She had not asked where they were going. He had not offered.

Las Vegas grew in the glass. The Sentinel Tower rose from the city's center like a needle threaded with light — a single spire, two kilometers tall, broadcasting the regulatory frequencies that kept the city's neural grid in alignment. Around its base, the Pit spread in concentric rings: the sunken entertainment district, the casinos and flesh parlors and combat arenas that had been driven underground when the floating hotels claimed the sky. From this altitude, the Pit was a dark circle in the bright sprawl — a pupil in a luminous eye.

Nolen reached into his breast pocket.

He pulled out a deck of cards. Small. Old. The backs were deep indigo, the color uneven, darker at the edges where handling had pressed oil into the paper over years. He held them in both hands. His tremoring right steadied against the left, and for a moment the two hands together were still.

"I learned from your mother," he said.

His voice was low. Not quiet — it occupied the lower registers the way a cello occupies a room, filling the space below the other sounds. But the volume was turned down. As though the words were not for the cabin but for the six inches between his mouth and the cards.

Elvira said nothing. She watched his hands.

He shuffled. Not the mechanical riffle of a dealer — a slow, imprecise motion, the halves of the deck sliding against each other with the soft friction of old paper. He cut once. Placed the bottom half on top. His thumb found the first card and held it.

"Three cards," he said. "Stage, Garden, Tower." He looked at her. His eyes were dark, recessed beneath a heavy brow, the whites gone yellowish with age. "That's how she taught me."

"I know," Elvira said.

Something crossed his face. A flinch that wasn't quite a flinch — the micro-contraction of a man who has heard a voice that sounds exactly like another voice and cannot, for a fraction of a second, tell the difference.

He laid the first card face-up on the seat between them.

---

The card was hand-painted. She could see the brushstrokes — fine lines of pigment laid over a gesso ground that had cracked with age into a web of tiny fractures, like a country seen from orbit. The image: a young man on a pale horse. He wore armor of a dull silver, winged at the shoulders — not the wings of war but of passage, of movement, as though the armor itself wanted to be elsewhere. The visor was open. The face beneath was smooth, the eyes looking forward with the particular focus of someone who has not yet learned that the horizon recedes.

In his right hand, held at the level of his chest, a golden cup. He carried it the way one carries something borrowed — carefully, with the awareness that it must be returned.

Behind him, a barren desert. Ahead, low hills.

"The Knight of Cups," Nolen said.

He did not explain the card. He looked at it the way one looks at a photograph from a time when the face in the photograph was still your own.

"There were three of us," he said. "In Hong Kong. When we were young." He said *young* the way someone says the name of a country they can never return to. "Your mother. Tian Lee. And me."

Elvira pulled the blanket tighter. Not from cold.

"We were —" He stopped. His right hand had begun to tremor again. He pressed it flat against his knee. "Inseparable. That's the word people use. It's wrong. We were separable. Everything is. But we chose not to be. Every morning, the choice. Every morning, the three of us."

The Knight stared up from the seat between them. The golden cup. The pale horse. The hills that rose without arrival.

"Tian was the brave one," Nolen said. "Lola was the smart one." He paused. "I was the one who left."

Elvira's hands were still beneath the blanket. Her face gave nothing.

"What happened?" she asked.

Nolen's jaw worked. The sound of old calcium against old calcium.

"We grew up," he said. And he said it the way the Knight held the cup: as something that must be given back, something too heavy to carry alone.

He laid the second card.

---

The card was inverted. He did not correct it.

The image, upside-down: a king seated on a block of grey stone, surrounded by water on all sides. His robes were blue — a deep, saturated blue that bled at the edges into the ocean around him, so the boundary between garment and water was unclear. He wore a crown of gold set with three tulips in dark velvet. In his left hand, a scepter. In his right, the same golden cup — no longer held at the chest but extended in the air, arm rigid, as though offering it to no one.

His face was blank. The eyes had been painted without pupils — two rounds of grey-blue that looked everywhere and at nothing. His shoes were made from the skins of scaly things.

Inverted, the water was above him. The ocean hung from the sky. The king sat on his stone island and the weight of everything that should have been below pressed down from above. A ship drifted in the distance, keeled over, going nowhere.

"The King," Nolen said.

He did not say *of Cups*. He did not need to.

Elvira studied the card. The inverted ocean. The rigid arm. The tulips on the crown, which upside-down looked less like flowers and more like drops of blood falling upward.

"It's you," she said.

"It's me now." He touched the edge of the card with one thick finger. The paper dimpled under the pressure. "The Knight becomes the King. That's supposed to be the good version. The grown-up. The one who's learned to hold everything without spilling."

He took his finger away.

"Reversed, it means the cup is full and you're still pouring. Grief, money, empire — it all runs over the edges and you can't —"

He stopped.

His breathing had changed. Shorter. The fog from his exhales came faster, smaller puffs that dissolved immediately in the cooled air. His right hand had left his knee and was gripping the armrest. The knuckles were white. The bone spurs pressed against the leather.

Elvira waited. She did not reach for him. She did not look away. She sat with her blanket and her silence and she let him be a man whose cup was running over in a cabin a thousand feet above a desert that did not care.

"Your mother —" he started.

He could not finish.

The cabin hummed. The city grew. The second card lay upside-down between them, a king drowning in his own sky.

---

He laid the third card with a sound like a door closing.

The Tower.

The image was violent. A tall structure — stone, golden-domed — struck by a bolt of white lightning that cracked the dome like an egg. The lightning did not come from a cloud. It came from somewhere above the frame of the card, from a place the painter had chosen not to include, as though the source of destruction was too large to depict.

Two figures fell from the tower. One face-up, arms spread, mouth open in a scream the paint could not sound. One face-down, curled inward, already surrendered. They fell through air that was not air but a field of small flames, each one shaped like a teardrop, and the teardrops fell with them, or the teardrops were the falling, or the figures and the flames were the same thing expressed in two languages.

At the base of the tower, rubble. The golden dome cracked on the ground. The stones scattered in a pattern that might have been random or might have been a word in a script older than alphabet.

Nolen said nothing.

Elvira knew the Tower. Not from study — from her body. The room where they'd kept her. The bolt cutters on the door. The first breath of outside air in weeks, cold and tasting of char. And before that — before her, before the body she wore: the original Elvira, dead before she turned two. The cloning. The growth. The decanting. Opening your eyes for the first time inside a shape that is both yours and someone else's. The Tower was all of it — every catastrophe that had struck Nolen Dawn's house, every bolt from above the frame.

"When did she die?" Elvira asked.

Nolen understood she did not mean the original Elvira. She meant Lola.

"Eleven years ago," he said. "In Chengdu. With Tian." He said the city and the man's name with the same flatness, as though both were coordinates on a map he no longer traveled.

"And Tian?"

"After."

The word *after* held everything and gave nothing. Elvira let it stand.

The three cards lay between them on the dark leather seat. A young man on a horse. An old king drowning in the sky. A tower splitting apart. Three stations of a life laid out in six inches of painted paper.

Las Vegas was close now. The floating hotels had resolved into distinct shapes — the Xanadu, glass and gold, rotating on its axis so slowly the motion was visible only in the way reflections shifted across its surface. The Meridian, an inverted pyramid of black marble, hung above the old Flamingo site like a fist in permanent suspension. Below them, in the Pit, colored light seeped upward through grates and vents — the underground city bleeding its nightlife into the atmosphere.

Nolen stared at the three cards. His hands had stopped tremoring. Both of them. They lay in his lap, palms up, emptied.

---

Elvira moved.

She reached across the space between them and took the deck from beside his thigh where he'd set it. He did not stop her. She held the cards in her left hand. They were warm from his body heat, from the pocket where they'd rested against his chest. She could feel the wear — the softened edges, the slight bow in the middle of the deck from being held too often in the same grip.

She turned the top card.

The Empress.

The image was lush where the others had been stark. A woman seated in a field of ripe grain, the stalks heavy and golden around her, bending toward her as though she were the source of their gravity. She wore a white gown that gathered beneath her in folds so abundant they merged with the earth — cloth and soil becoming one material. On her head, a crown of twelve stars. In her right hand, a scepter. On a stone beside her, the symbol of Venus, carved or grown, it was impossible to tell.

Her face was full. Not beautiful the way fashion is beautiful — beautiful the way a field is beautiful at the end of a season, when everything is at its heaviest and closest to falling. Her eyes were dark. They looked out from the card with an expression that was not maternal exactly, but *present* — the look of someone who has seen every version of you and has not turned away.

Elvira held the card up.

"What does this one mean?" she asked.

Nolen looked at the Empress and his face changed. Not dramatically. A settling. The corners of his mouth softened. His eyelids lowered a fraction. His entire face gave up the effort of being a face and became, for a moment, just the terrain of a man who had loved a woman and lost her and was looking at a painted image that held her likeness in the shape of a cheekbone, the fall of a gaze, the gravity of a body around which everything bent.

He could not answer.

Elvira watched him not answer. She watched the silence fill the space where the answer should have been, and she understood the silence better than any answer could have made her understand. The Empress was Lola. The Empress was her mother — the original's mother, the woman whose DNA ran through the body Elvira inhabited like a river through a landscape it had not chosen.

She set the card beside the other three.

Four cards now. A knight, a king, a tower, an empress. A life, told in pictures.

---

The hovercraft banked. Las Vegas tilted in the glass — a slow rotation, the city swinging from left to right as they entered the descent corridor. The Sentinel Tower loomed ahead, its broadcast frequencies vibrating the cabin walls at a pitch just above silence. The windows of the Xanadu caught the moonlight and threw it back in fractured planes.

Nolen was looking at her.

Not at the city. Not at the cards. At her. At the face that was not his daughter's face and was exactly his daughter's face. At the eyes that held intelligence he recognized and distance he did not. At the girl wrapped in a blanket that cost more than some people earned in a year, who had been grown in a tank to replace a child he had held as she stopped breathing, who had been kidnapped and recovered and was sitting here now, alive, a copy so precise it was its own form of cruelty.

"I thought you had died," he said. "My little Evie."

The words came out without architecture. No gravel baritone deployed for effect. Just a man's voice, stripped to the frequency of its actual grief, cracking on the name the way a voice cracks on any word it has said too many times in the dark.

He was not talking to her. She knew that.

He was talking to the first one. The original. The daughter whose cells had been harvested and cultured and grown into the body sitting across from him now. He was looking at Elvira and seeing through her to the child she had been made to replace, and the distance between those two people — the one who was here and the one who was gone — was a distance no technology could close.

Elvira knew.

She let him.

She sat in the blanket and let him look at her and see someone else, because she understood — not from data, not from briefing, but from the particular intelligence of a body that knows it is a copy — that this was the only kindness available. To be the screen on which his grief projected. To hold still while a father looked through you for the ghost of his child.

The cabin was quiet. The city lights moved in the glass. Somewhere below, in the Pit, a million people were spending money and losing it and calling the loss entertainment. Up here, in the cashmere silence, a man was dissolving.

"Are you afraid?" Elvira asked.

Nolen shook his head.

"It's not fear," he said.

She looked at the three cards on the seat. The Knight. The King. The Tower. Three stages of a man dismantled by time and the absence of the people who made time bearable.

"Then it's the Tower," she said.

---

The hovercraft descended.

Las Vegas rose around them the way water rises around a stone — slowly, then all at once, the buildings climbing in the glass until the sky was replaced by architecture. The floating hotels passed above, their undersides vast and dark, the anti-grav arrays humming at frequencies that rattled the bones. The mid-stacks swallowed them — tier upon tier of residential platforms, laundry lines and window gardens and the blue flicker of a thousand screens. Then the lower stacks. The market level. Vendors' lights strung between buildings in sagging catenaries of red and gold. The smell of cooking oil and ozone bled through the cabin seals.

They descended through it all. Past the transit arteries where automated pods streaked in green and white blurs. Past the service corridors where the city's plumbing and wiring ran exposed like the veins and nerves of a body with its skin removed. The Pit opened below — a dark mouth, lit from within, the entertainment district sprawling in its permanent twilight.

The hovercraft settled onto a private platform. High up. The penthouse level of a tower that bore no name, only a frequency signature the city's grid recognized as *do not approach*. The engines cycled down. The turbine note dropped from hum to whisper to silence.

The cabin was still.

Nolen gathered the cards. He did not shuffle them back into the deck. He stacked them — Knight, King, Tower, Empress — in the order they had been laid, and slid them into his breast pocket, over his heart, where they had been before. He moved slowly. The way a man moves when the evening has cost him something and the muscles have not yet agreed to continue.

He looked at Elvira.

She looked back.

Between them, the space where the cards had been was empty — a rectangle of dark leather, warm from where the paper had rested. Elvira pulled the blanket from her shoulders and folded it. The act was precise, deliberate. She set it on the seat beside her.

She was eleven years old. She looked forty.

Nolen reached for her hand.

It was not a grab. Not a claim. It was the slowest gesture he had made all night — his thick, gnarled fingers extending across the space between the seats with the care of a man reaching for something that might not be there when his hand arrives. The bone spurs. The tremor. The knuckles that had built an empire and broken men and held a dying infant and had never, in all the decades since, learned how to be gentle enough for what they wanted to hold.

Elvira watched the hand come.

She could have pulled away. She could have turned to the window. She could have wrapped herself back in the blanket and the silence and the identity of a girl who owed this man nothing — who was not his daughter, who had not asked to be made, who had been poured into a mold shaped by someone else's grief.

She let him take it.

His hand closed around hers. The tremor ran through his fingers into hers and she felt it — the vibration of a body that had carried too much for too long and could not put it down. His grip was gentle in the way that only violent hands can be gentle: with effort, with the full conscious weight of choosing not to crush.

She did not squeeze back. She did not pull away.

She sat with her hand in his hand — her father's hand, the word she had never used, the word that was both a lie and the truest thing available — and she let the contact be what it was. Insufficient. Irreplaceable. The warmth of one body passing to another in a cabin suspended above a city that never stopped performing its own aliveness.

The door opened. The platform light fell across them. Las Vegas hummed its vast electric hymn. Somewhere a siren. Somewhere music. Somewhere the machinery of a civilization that had learned to float its pleasures above its miseries and call the arrangement progress.

Nolen Dawn stood. He did not release her hand.

Elvira stood with him.

They walked to the open door. The night air hit — desert cold, cut with the electric tang of the city's neural grid, the smell of a world built on top of another world built on top of sand. The platform stretched ahead, dark stone, polished, reflecting the floating hotels above like a lake reflecting stars.

She looked up at him. He looked down at her.

The face. The same face. The daughter who was not the daughter. The father who was the only father she had.

"Father," she said.

Just the word. No question mark. No warmth beyond what the word itself carried. A designation and an acceptance and a door opened exactly wide enough for one person to pass through, and she was passing, and he was on the other side, and the word hung between them in the cold air like a card laid face-up — legible, undeniable, meaning everything it meant and nothing more.

Nolen Dawn's eyes filled.

He did not wipe them.

They stepped off the hovercraft and into the city, hand in hand, a man and a girl who was not his girl, walking forward into the bright and the noise and the night. Four painted cards pressed against his chest like a letter he would never send. The word *Father* dissolving into the hum of Las Vegas the way all true things dissolve — not into nothing, but into everything around them, becoming so diffuse they can no longer be separated from the air.
