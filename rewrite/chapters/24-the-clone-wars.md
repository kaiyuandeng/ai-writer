# Chapter 24: The Clone Wars

The turret sat at the lip of the Hupasa Valley like a tooth in a broken jaw.

It was a squat cylinder of poured stone and welded alloy, its roof bristling with sensor arrays and the twin barrels of a pulse cannon that had not been fired in six days and that Lyon kept clean anyway, running a rag along the barrel housings with the meditative attention of a man who had found in maintenance the only reliable form of prayer. Below the turret, the valley fell away — two kilometers of terraced slope descending into a basin that the maps called the Hupasa Floor and that the soldiers called the Sink, because things that went down into it did not reliably come back up.

Hyfe sat on the turret's observation ledge with his legs hanging over the drop. His raven Rodin perched on the railing beside him, black feathers ruffled against the wind that came up the valley at this hour — cold, mineral, carrying the smell of ozone and something else, something the veterans called the Taste, which was the atmospheric residue of microwave radiation and which sat on the tongue like copper and dread.

"Another day," Lyon said, from inside the turret.

Hyfe did not respond. He was watching the valley.

"Another day," Lyon repeated, louder, "another mushroom cloud. Another sixteen hours of staring at geological nothing through a visor that gives me a headache. Another meal of reconstituted protein that tastes like the concept of food remembered by someone who never ate. Another—"

"You're editorializing."

"I'm coping." Lyon emerged from the turret's hatch with the rag over his shoulder and a mug of something that steamed. He was identical to Hyfe in every measurable way — the same height, the same build, the same arrangement of features on a face that was not handsome but was precise, the bones close to the surface, the jaw narrow, the eyes dark and set deep beneath a brow that gave everything he looked at the quality of being scrutinized. Clone 18. The previous iteration. He carried himself with the particular looseness of a man who had survived long enough to stop expecting to.

"Editorializing is coping," Lyon continued. He sat beside Hyfe, leaving a careful distance between them. The mug steamed. "Without editorializing, I'm just a man on a turret. With it, I'm a man on a turret with commentary. Commentary is the thin line between soldier and furniture."

Hyfe looked at him. Lyon was grinning — the crooked, self-amused grin of someone who found his own existence faintly ridiculous and had decided that the appropriate response was narration. It was the grin of every Clone 18 that Hyfe had known, and he had known three. The grin survived the transfer. Whatever the cloning process preserved — muscle memory, bone density, the fundamental architecture of personality — it preserved the grin.

It did not preserve memory. Lyon had no recollection of the previous Lyon, or the one before that. Each iteration arrived fully formed, physically mature, carrying the training and the instincts and the speech patterns of the template but none of the experience. Lyon knew how to field-strip the pulse cannon because the template knew. He did not know that he had done it yesterday because yesterday had belonged to someone else — someone with his face and his grin and his habit of editorializing, someone who was dead.

"Visor check," Hyfe said.

Lyon sighed. He set the mug on the ledge. "It's oh-six-hundred. They don't move until—"

"Visor check."

Lyon pulled the microwave visor from the rack inside the hatch. It was a heavy piece of equipment — a band of dark composite that fit across the eyes, its surface studded with receptor arrays that translated microwave radiation into a visual spectrum the human brain could interpret. The standard visible world vanished when you wore it. What replaced it was something else.

Lyon settled the visor over his eyes. He looked at the valley.

"Clear," he said.

Then he stopped.

"No," he said. "Wait."

Hyfe had his visor on before Lyon finished the word. The valley transformed. The terraced slopes, the scrub vegetation, the distant gleam of the basin's mineral lake — all of it vanished, replaced by a microwave landscape that was the same terrain seen in a different language. The rock was dark. The sky was darker. And rising from the valley floor, in the basin's center, where the visible spectrum showed nothing but stone and mist: columns.

They rose like smoke given vertebrae. Massive — each one thirty, forty meters tall, their bases rooted in the valley floor, their forms tapering upward into shapes that were almost human and were not. They were translucent in the microwave spectrum, their bodies composed of organized radiation — structured interference patterns that maintained coherence against the wind and the terrain and the fundamental tendency of energy to dissipate. They did not have faces. They did not have limbs. They were presences. Vertical. Silent. Vast.

The anomalies.

Hyfe counted seven. Six stood in a loose cluster near the basin's center. The seventh stood apart, closer to the valley's western wall, taller than the others, its form more defined — the suggestion of a head, of shoulders, of something that might have been a hand raised in a gesture that could have meant greeting or warning or nothing at all.

"Seven," Lyon said. His voice had lost its editorial quality. It was flat. Professional. The voice of a soldier performing the only function that mattered, which was seeing clearly when every part of him wanted to look away. "Azimuth two-seven-zero through two-nine-five. Range: one-point-four klicks. They're closer than yesterday."

Hyfe keyed the turret's comm. "Turret Nine to command. Seven anomalies in the basin. Bearing two-seven-zero. Range one-four-hundred. Requesting sunlight."

The response came in six seconds. A voice — clipped, nasal, the voice of someone who had given this order before and would give it again and had stopped hearing it as extraordinary:

"Sunlight authorized. All turrets, brace."

Lyon was already inside the turret, pulling the blast shield down over the observation port. Hyfe swung his legs in from the ledge. Rodin hopped to his shoulder, the talons gripping the fabric of his field jacket. The raven did not startle. The raven had been through this before.

The missile launched from the battery on the ridgeline three kilometers north. Hyfe did not see it leave. He saw the contrail — a white line drawn on the dark sky with the casual violence of a blade across cloth — and then the flash.

The warhead detonated at two thousand meters. Not nuclear — thermobaric, a fuel-air device calibrated to produce the maximum possible luminosity at the minimum possible altitude. The sky turned white. Not the white of the twin suns, which was warm and directional and cast shadows. The white of absolute saturation — every wavelength at maximum amplitude, a wall of light that fell on the valley like a hammer on glass.

Through the blast shield, through the turret's armored viewport, Hyfe watched the anomalies dissolve. The microwave visor was unnecessary now — the light was so intense that the anomalies became briefly visible to the naked eye, their structured radiation patterns destabilized by the flood of competing frequencies. They flickered. They stuttered. Their vast columnar forms lost coherence — the careful interference patterns that gave them shape unraveling like cloth pulled from a loom. For one second they were visible as what they truly were: not columns, not giants, not any shape at all, but knots of organized energy caught in the moment of becoming disorganized, and the disorganization was violent and total and the light consumed them and they were gone.

The artificial sun faded. The sky returned to its usual grey. The valley was empty.

Lyon emerged from behind the blast shield. His visor was off. His face was pale beneath the field tan.

"I hate that," he said.

"I know."

"The part where you can see them. For that one second. The part where they look like—"

"I know."

Lyon did not finish the sentence. He did not need to. Hyfe knew what they looked like in that final second. They looked like people. Not in shape — in quality. In the way they came apart. The anomalies dissolved the way a person dissolves when the thing holding them together stops holding. Not dramatically. Not with spectacle. With a loosening, a release, a quiet structural failure that was worse than explosion because it suggested that the coherence had always been provisional, that the shape had always been a choice, and the choice had been revoked.

---

The mech-pods descended in the afternoon.

They were two-person vehicles — articulated shells of composite armor, each one mounted on four hydraulic legs that picked their way down the terraced slope with the cautious precision of insects navigating rubble. Hyfe took the left seat. Lyon took the right. The cockpit was tight, the controls physical — joysticks, pedals, a haptic wheel for the sensor suite. No automation. The Hupasa pods were analog by design, because the anomalies could read digital systems the way they read thoughts, and the only defense against a thing that consumed data was to give it nothing to consume.

The valley floor was silent. The artificial sunlight had burned off the morning mist and the terrain was visible in sharp detail — grey stone, patches of black lichen, the mineral sheen of the basin's shallow lake. The mech-pod's legs crunched on loose scree.

Lyon saw the first object at the base of the western wall.

A flower. Growing from a crack in the basalt where no flower had any reason to grow. Its petals were deep blue — the blue of a sky seen through water, the blue of something remembered rather than observed. Its stem was thin and impossibly straight. It should not have been there. The Hupasa Valley's soil was toxic — saturated with microwave residue that killed vegetation within days. Nothing grew on the valley floor. Nothing had grown in the years since the anomalies first appeared.

Hyfe stopped the pod. He extended the sensor arm — a telescoping limb tipped with a sampling claw — and plucked the flower. It came free easily. Its roots were shallow. It had not been growing. It had been placed. Or it had been remembered into existence by something that knew what flowers were and did not know that this one was extinct.

"Cerulean aster," Lyon said, reading the botanical readout on the pod's display. "Last recorded specimen: three hundred years ago. Vermillion lowlands. Extinct since the sulfur expansion."

They found more.

Scattered across the valley floor in a pattern that was not a pattern — that had the quality of debris, of things shaken loose by the anomalies' dissolution and left behind like shells after a wave. A wooden toy — small, carved, the shape of a horse with a broken leg, its paint faded to ghosts of color. A photograph printed on material that no longer existed, showing a woman's face caught in the act of laughter. A pair of sandals, child-sized, the leather cracked and the stitching sound. A ring of hammered copper, sized for a finger much larger than human standard. A book — cloth-bound, handwritten, the script in a language that the pod's translator could not identify.

Objects from memories. The anomalies read the minds of anyone who entered the valley, and what they read they reproduced — not perfectly, not functionally, but with the particular fidelity of a dream, which captures the emotional weight of an object while discarding its physics. The flower was real but could not survive. The toy was solid but would not bear play. The photograph showed a face that no living person remembered but that someone, once, had loved enough to carry in the deep architecture of their brain, where the anomalies could find it and hold it up to the light and say: *this. You lost this. Here it is.*

Hyfe collected them. He did not know why. Standard protocol was to log and leave — the objects were considered contaminated, and the field biologists who studied the anomalies wanted them left in situ for analysis. But Hyfe placed each one in the pod's sample bin with a care that was not protocol but instinct. The flower. The toy. The photograph. He handled them the way you handle relics — not because they are sacred, but because they were someone's and that someone was gone.

Lyon was quiet. He watched Hyfe collect the objects and he did not editorialize. His silence was the loudest thing he had said all day.

---

Lyon died at 14:47.

They were two hundred meters from the basin's center when the pod's sensor suite registered a microwave spike — a sudden, localized burst, well above the background radiation, concentrated in a point six meters to their right. Lyon turned toward it. The visor was on his forehead, pushed up, not covering his eyes. He turned toward the spike with his bare eyes and the spike was not a spike but a presence — a single anomaly, small, no larger than a child, standing in the scree with its structured radiation pulsing at a frequency that the larger anomalies had not used.

It pulsed once.

Lyon's body seized. Not a convulsion — a lock. Every muscle firing simultaneously, the voluntary and the involuntary, the skeletal and the cardiac, a total systemic contraction that pulled his spine straight and his jaw shut and his hands into fists and his eyes wide open. Hyfe reached for him. His hand found Lyon's shoulder and the shoulder was rigid, the muscle beneath the fabric hard as the composite armor around them.

Lyon's eyes moved. They found Hyfe's. They held them. The pupils were dilated past the iris — black discs in which Hyfe could see his own face reflected.

Then the tension released. Lyon slumped. The body settled into the seat with the particular weight of a thing no longer inhabited, the way a coat falls when the hanger is removed. His eyes stayed open. The grin was gone.

Hyfe sat beside him. The pod's legs stood still. The valley was silent. The small anomaly — if it had been an anomaly — was gone. The sensor suite showed nothing. The baseline radiation hummed.

He closed Lyon's eyes with his thumb and forefinger. The eyelids were warm. They would be warm for a while yet. The body's heat did not know that the body was finished, and it continued its work with the faithful ignorance of a system that has not received the memo.

Hyfe keyed the comm. "Turret Nine to command. Clone 18 is down. Requesting extraction and replacement."

The voice on the comm did not pause. "Replacement en route. ETA forty minutes."

Forty minutes. The time it took to thaw a body from the reserve stock, to run the activation sequence, to load the transport. Forty minutes between one Lyon and the next. Hyfe looked at the body beside him — at the narrow jaw, the deep-set eyes now closed, the crooked grin that was not grinning — and he thought about the word *replacement* and what it meant when applied to a person and whether the word was honest or whether it was a lie that the military had told itself so many times it had become operational doctrine.

He drove the pod back to the ridge. He carried Lyon's body to the medical bay. He filed the report.

---

The transport landed at 15:31.

The rear hatch opened and a figure stepped out into the grey afternoon light, and the figure was Lyon. The same height. The same build. The same narrow jaw and deep-set eyes and the same way of standing — weight on the left foot, right hand in the pocket, the head tilted at a five-degree angle as if the world were a question he was not sure he wanted to answer.

Clone 18. Fresh. Unscratched. The field jacket still creased from storage. The eyes clear and rested and carrying no memory of turret duty or mug steam or the Hupasa Floor or the flower that should not have existed or the moment when a microwave pulse stopped a heart in the time it took to turn a head.

The new Lyon walked to the turret. He looked at Hyfe. The grin appeared — the same grin, the same crookedness, the same quality of self-amused resignation. It appeared because it was in the template, because the template was the man, or enough of the man to pass, or enough to make the difference between the original and the copy a question that the military had decided was not worth asking.

"Clone 18, reporting," Lyon said. "What did I miss?"

Hyfe looked at him. He looked at the grin. He looked at the eyes that were Lyon's eyes in every physical particular and that were empty of Lyon in every way that the physical could not capture.

"You ever wish you were someone else?" Hyfe asked.

The new Lyon blinked. The question did not compute against his template — it was the kind of question that required context, and context was the one thing the cloning process did not transfer. He processed it the way he processed an unfamiliar order: literally, without subtext.

"I'm Clone 18," he said. "Who else would I be?"

He walked into the turret. He found the rag Lyon had left on the cannon housing. He picked it up. He began to clean. The motions were identical — the same angle of the wrist, the same pressure, the same meditative rhythm. The template's muscle memory, performing its prayer.

Hyfe stood on the observation ledge. Rodin sat on the railing. The valley stretched below — grey and silent, the basin still, the scree fields unmarked except where the mech-pod's legs had left their prints. The artificial sunlight had long since faded. The real suns were setting — Aleph first, dropping below the western ridge in a line of white fire, then Beth following with its red.

He looked at Rodin.

The raven looked back. Its eyes were black — not dark but black, the total absence of color, two spheres of polished nothing set in the angular architecture of its skull. Hyfe had found Rodin in the Outsidelands three years ago — a fledgling, starving, its wing broken at the joint. He had set the bone with a splint of twig and gut-thread and carried the bird inside his jacket for a month until it healed. The raven had not left him since. It did not love him — ravens do not love. But it had chosen him, and the choice had the quality of a contract negotiated in a language neither party spoke fluently.

Hyfe looked into Rodin's eye. The black surface caught the last light and held it, and in the holding the eye became a lens, and in the lens Hyfe saw something that was not the valley and was not the sky and was not any part of the world his body occupied.

He saw a grid. A flat plane divided into squares, each square occupied by a figure — small, stylized, rendered in a visual language that suggested not life but the representation of life. The figures moved across the grid in patterns that were not random but were not natural either, the patterns of things directed by an intelligence outside the frame. He saw names — labels floating above the figures in a script he could read but could not explain how he could read. Names he recognized. Names that belonged to people he knew, to people he had fought beside, to people who were dead and had been replaced and were dead again.

He saw a room. Small. Dark. Lit by the glow of a screen that cast blue light on walls that were close and bare and covered with papers and maps and the detritus of obsession. A chair. A desk. Hands on a keyboard — not his hands, not anyone's hands he recognized, but hands that moved with the practiced fluency of someone for whom this screen was home and this room was the world and the figures on the grid were more real than anything outside the door.

He saw a planet. Blue. Not the bruised blue of a gas giant or the cold blue of an ice world but the blue of water — of oceans, of an atmosphere so thick with moisture that the light filtering through it turned the whole sphere into something that looked like an eye weeping upward. A planet he had never visited. A planet that might not exist. A planet that sat in Rodin's black eye like a memory belonging to someone else, placed there by a process that had no name, seen by a man who was the nineteenth copy of a man who had been the eighteenth copy of a man who had been the seventeenth, and so on downward through the iterations, each copy carrying in its template a residue that the cloning process could not reach and could not erase and could not explain — a ghost in the architecture, a whisper in the machine, the faintest imprint of a world that was not this world and a game that was not a game and a basement on a planet that was blue.

The image held for one breath. Then Rodin blinked and the eye was an eye and the valley was a valley and the suns were gone and the dark was coming and somewhere inside the turret the new Lyon was cleaning the cannon with the old Lyon's rag and humming a tune that the template knew and the man did not.

Hyfe stood on the ledge. The wind came up the valley, cold and mineral, carrying the Taste. Below, in the basin, the night was assembling itself — the mist returning, the scree darkening, the shallow lake going black. Somewhere beneath the stone, the anomalies were rebuilding. Restructuring. Learning from the sunlight that had destroyed them, adapting their interference patterns, preparing for the next time they would rise from the valley floor and stand in their vast silent columns and wait to be seen by eyes that could not look away.

He touched Rodin's breast feathers. The bird shifted but did not fly.

He had seen the blue planet before. In dreams. In the space between waking and sleep where the template's residue leaked through — images without narrative, sensations without source, the echo of a world that existed somewhere in the chain of copies, in the original of the original, in the first version of the man he was the nineteenth version of. He did not know what it meant. He did not know if it meant anything. But he carried it the way he carried the flower from the valley floor — carefully, without understanding, with the instinct that some things are kept not because they are useful but because they are true.

The dark came. The turret's running lights activated — red, low, the minimum luminescence required by regulation. Inside, Lyon made tea. Outside, Hyfe watched the valley and the valley watched back and the night held them both in its indifferent hand.
