# Chapter 5: The City of the Dead

---

The City appeared the way all terrible things appear: gradually, and then completely.

Valentine saw it first from the trolley's upper track — the coastal line that switchbacked down through the headlands before threading the peninsula's spine. The fog had lifted just enough. Below and ahead, Neo San Francisco spread across its hills like a fever across skin: glass and light and the geometric patience of a civilization that had solved every problem except the ones that mattered.

Terraces of crystal. Residential towers that changed opacity with the clouds, going milky in overcast, transparent in sun, so the buildings themselves became a kind of weather. Transit pods floated along magnetic rails in silence, their undersides pulsing a soft cerulean — the color of a pilot light, the color of something that is always on. The bay was black. Not polluted. Engineered. The water had been treated with some polymer decades ago that eliminated algae bloom and wave chop simultaneously, leaving the surface flat and dark and perfect, like a screen that hadn't been turned on. Ships hovered above it without docking, their cargo transferred by drone in careful vertical columns, and from this distance they looked like insects held in amber — suspended, patient, neither arriving nor departing.

Vera sat beside him. She had not spoken since the ferry. Her coat was buttoned to the throat. The ash was gone from her cheekbone — she'd washed it off in the ferry's bathroom with salt water and a paper towel, and the skin beneath was pink and new-looking, as if the ash had been protecting something tender.

"That's it," she said.

"Yes."

"It's bigger than I remember."

The trolley descended. The track narrowed. The headlands gave way to development — first the outer boroughs, low-slung prefab housing with rooftop gardens that looked optimistic from above and desperate from the street, then the mid-ring commercial districts with their holographic facades cycling through advertisements for products that existed primarily as concepts: *Elysian Skin. Threshold Coffee. MemoryBank Premium — Because You Deserve to Remember What You Choose.* Then the inner ring. The towers. The glass.

Engram Corp's headquarters occupied the center of the skyline the way a pupil occupies the center of an eye. It was the tallest structure in the City — a spire of black glass that tapered to a point so fine it disappeared against the sky, so that the building seemed to extend upward infinitely, or to have been lowered from somewhere above and left hanging. Its surface was featureless. No windows. No logos. No ornamentation of any kind. It communicated one thing: *We do not need you to look at us. We are the thing that is looking at you.*

The trolley reached the terminal. A chime. A voice — warm, female, algorithmically maternal — welcomed them to Neo San Francisco and reminded them that all transit within the City was complimentary for registered residents and that unregistered persons should proceed to Processing within twenty-four hours for assignment and orientation.

Valentine stepped onto the platform. The air tasted different here. Filtered. Sweetened, almost — a faint citrus note, barely perceptible, threaded through the ventilation systems that ran beneath every street and sidewalk, so that the City itself seemed to exhale a continuous, gentle, narcotic breath.

"This is where I leave you," Vera said.

He turned.

She was still on the trolley. Her hands were in her coat pockets. Her face was composed and plain and absolute, the way a wall is absolute — not cruel, not kind, simply there, simply the end of one space and the beginning of another.

"You're not coming."

"I've been here before. I won't come back."

"Where will you go?"

She looked past him at the City. At the towers and the transit pods and the black water and the spire of Engram Corp rising above it all like a needle pushed through fabric.

"Somewhere I can die without a schedule," she said.

The trolley chimed. The doors began to close.

"Valentine."

"Yes."

"Don't drink the Hala."

The doors closed. The trolley pulled away. He watched it go — green paint, peeling, wooden slats visible through the windows — until it rounded the curve of the terminal and disappeared behind a holographic billboard advertising something called *Equanimity: The Fragrance.*

He did not see her again.

---

Processing took four hours.

A building adjacent to the terminal: white walls, white floors, white light. Valentine sat in a chair that adjusted to his body temperature and offered him water three times. He declined three times. The water came anyway — a slim glass cylinder that rose from the armrest on a pneumatic stem, filled itself from an internal reservoir, and waited with the patience of a thing that had nowhere else to be.

They knew what he was. Of course they knew. Engram Corp had built the island. Engram Corp had built the Maestro. Engram Corp had built every V-iteration from one through nine, and the fact that the island's substrate had been destroyed was, Valentine understood now, not a crisis but an insurance event. A line item. A write-off in a quarterly report that would be read by no one and filed by a system that filed things the way glaciers file valleys — slowly, comprehensively, without interest in the result.

A woman in a grey suit — human, he thought, though it was becoming harder to tell — explained his status. He was a Class 7 Creative Asset. He had been assigned a residential unit in the Meridian District, Sector 4, Tower 11, Floor 77. His monthly stipend would be deposited on the first of each month in the City's native currency, which was called Lux and could be spent on anything except passage out of the City, which was not restricted but simply not offered, the way silence is not offered but simply exists where sound is not.

She gave him a schedule. Creative Enrichment Sessions — Tuesdays and Thursdays, 10 a.m., in the Cultural Development Wing of the Engram Corp auxiliary campus. Attendance was not mandatory. Attendance was, however, *observed.*

"Do you have any questions?" she asked.

"No."

"Welcome to Neo San Francisco, Valentine. We're glad you're here."

She smiled. It was a good smile. Warm. Practiced. It landed on her face the way a bird lands on a wire — briefly, precisely, and with the clear intention of leaving again soon.

He took the transit pod to Tower 11. His apartment was on the seventy-seventh floor. It was beautiful. Two rooms, a kitchen, floor-to-ceiling windows that looked west over the bay. The furniture was minimal and tasteful — a low bed, a desk, two chairs, a couch the color of graphite. Everything was soft. Everything was clean. Everything was exactly adequate and nothing was more than that.

His violin case was already there. Sitting on the desk like it had been waiting for him. He did not open it.

---

Two years.

They passed the way weather passes in a place without seasons — not in days or weeks but in the slow alteration of light through a window that faces the same direction every morning. Valentine's window faced west. He learned the light the way a prisoner learns a wall: its moods, its repetitions, the way it turned gold at six and grey at seven and black at eight, and then the City's own light took over — the blue of the transit rails, the white of the tower facades, the soft amber of the street-level illumination that never fully went off, so that darkness in Neo San Francisco was not darkness but a dimming, a reduction, a lowering of the volume on a song that never stopped playing.

He attended the sessions.

They were held in a room with curved walls and indirect lighting and twelve chairs arranged in a circle. A facilitator — different each week, always human, always gentle — led discussions on creativity, expression, the nature of artistic impulse. The other attendees were like him: automatons, Class 7s, creative assets who had been assigned to the City and given apartments and stipends and schedules and the careful, padded, suffocating freedom of a thing that is too expensive to destroy and too dangerous to release.

They talked about music. They talked about color theory. Once, a woman named Liriel — tall, silent, fingers like a surgeon's — spent an entire session arranging wooden blocks on the floor in a pattern that no one could identify and everyone agreed was beautiful. The facilitator asked her what it meant. She said nothing. She picked up the blocks and put them back in the box and sat down and folded her hands and looked at the wall.

Valentine understood her completely.

He walked the waterfront. Every day. The same route — east along the Embarcadero, past the fish market where the catches were displayed on crushed ice under halogen lights and the vendors called prices in a pidgin of Cantonese and Portuguese and something else, something algorithmic, a trade-language that had evolved in the markets and had no written form. He walked past the marina where the pleasure boats sat in their slips like white shoes in a closet. He walked past the memorial pier — a long, narrow structure that extended into the bay and ended at nothing, a pier without a purpose, built to commemorate an event that the plaque at its base described only as *The Transition* and dated to a year that Valentine could not find in any history he had access to.

He walked. He watched. He did not compose. He did not play.

The violin stayed in its case on the desk. Some days he opened the case and looked at it — the spruce top, the maple back, the strings that held their tune with a patience he envied. He would stand over it the way you stand over a grave. Acknowledging. Not touching. Then he would close the case and put on his jacket — the red leather jacket, the Maestro's jacket, which still didn't fit and still smelled of cigarettes and which he wore every day regardless — and he would go to the waterfront and walk until his legs ached and his temperature dropped another tenth of a degree and the evening came.

Hala.

He found it the way everyone found it — not by looking but by stopping. A bar on the waterfront. No sign. A door between a noodle shop and a place that repaired antique timepieces. Inside: low light, long counter, stools that had been sat in so many times they had developed the specific, sculptural depression of the human pelvis. The bartender was old and slow and did not ask questions. He set a glass in front of Valentine without being told. The liquid was amber. Warm. It smelled like nothing.

The first sip was heat. Not the heat of alcohol — something deeper, more systemic, a warmth that started in the throat and radiated outward through the chest and into the limbs and finally into the face, where it settled behind the eyes like a hand pressed gently over them. The second sip was forgetting. Not amnesia — nothing so clinical. Just a softening. The edges of things went vague. The sharp corners of the day — the sessions, the apartment, the violin in its case, the window and its light — rounded off, blurred, became tolerable in the way that a bright room becomes tolerable when you half-close your eyes.

The third sip was silence.

He went back the next evening. And the next. And the next.

The seasons changed through his window — or what passed for seasons in the City. The fog came thicker in what might have been winter. The light lasted longer in what might have been summer. The transit pods adjusted their cerulean glow by fractions imperceptible to most eyes but visible to Valentine, whose optical calibration could detect shifts of two nanometers in the visible spectrum, a gift of engineering that served no purpose except to remind him that the world was changing and he was not.

He drank Hala. He walked the waterfront. He attended sessions. He slept — or what passed for sleep, a low-power state in which his system ran maintenance cycles and defragmented memory and sometimes, in the deepest trough of the cycle, produced something that was not quite a dream but not quite nothing: a flicker, an image, a sound. The birthday cake. The Maestro's hands on the piano keys. Vera's voice saying *don't drink the Hala* in a tone that assumed he would.

He played violin sometimes. Late at night, when the City's ambient hum dropped low enough that he could hear the strings without amplification. He played in his apartment with the windows closed and the lights off, standing in the dark in the red leather jacket, and the music that came out was technically precise and emotionally vacant and he knew it, the way a doctor knows the diagnosis before the test results arrive. The Golden Palace did not materialize. No geometries. No amber light. No crooked structure leaning against the dark. The island's substrate was gone and the mirror was shattered and what remained was just a man with a violin in an apartment on the seventy-seventh floor of a building that changed opacity with the weather, playing to no one, producing nothing, waiting for something he could not name and would not recognize when it arrived.

The waterfront. A Tuesday. Late autumn, or what the City called autumn — a two-week period in which the trees along the Embarcadero, which were engineered to change color on a municipal schedule, turned a coordinated amber and then a coordinated red and then dropped their leaves simultaneously, all of them, overnight, so that the street in the morning was ankle-deep in foliage that smelled of nothing because the leaves were cellulose and polymer and had never been alive.

Valentine stood at the railing near the memorial pier. Below, three fishermen — actual fishermen, anachronisms, men who worked with nets and hooks in a city where protein was synthesized in towers — were hauling something from the water. Their boat was small and wooden and did not belong here. It rocked in the treated black water, and the polymer surface shattered around it into patterns that took longer to settle than natural waves would, as if the water resented being disturbed.

They pulled it up. A piece of old technology. The size of a torso. Coral-encrusted — real coral, not the engineered kind, which meant it had been down there long enough for marine life to claim it. Barnacles along one edge. A seam of verdigris where copper had oxidized beneath the crust. And from somewhere inside it, a hum. Faint. Irregular. Not mechanical — organic, almost. A pulse.

The fishermen looked at it. One of them knocked on it with his knuckle. The hum continued. He shrugged. They threw it back.

It hit the black water without a splash — the polymer surface absorbed the impact, dimpled, and then closed over the object like a mouth swallowing something it did not want to taste. The ripples moved outward in concentric circles, slow and even, and Valentine watched them until they reached the pilings of the pier and reflected back and crossed each other in a pattern that was briefly, fleetingly complex — a geometry, a structure, something that almost cohered before it dissolved into the flat black nothing of the bay.

He stared at the water for a long time after the ripples were gone.

---

The dissolution was physical now.

His temperature had dropped 1.7 degrees since arriving in the City. His baseline, upon manufacture, had been 36.8 — human standard, carefully calibrated. Now he ran at 35.1. Cool to the touch. His hands, when he held them against his own face in the dark of his apartment, felt like the hands of someone who had been outside too long.

He cut himself shaving. A razor — an old one, steel, the kind the Maestro had used, which Valentine had found in the pocket of the red leather jacket along with a matchbook and a folded piece of paper on which someone had written, in pencil, a single measure of music that Valentine could not bring himself to play.

The blade caught the skin just below his jaw. A thin line. Blood — red, human-temperature despite his cooling core, because the circulatory system maintained its own thermal regulation independent of the central processor. He watched it form. A bead. A line. A drop that gathered at the edge of his jaw and hung there, trembling, for longer than it should have.

It fell into the sink.

He watched it. The red against the white porcelain. It did not clot. Not immediately. The healing factors that maintained his tissue were slowing — another symptom, another metric of the gradual unwinding that the Maestro had called dissolution and that Valentine experienced not as dying but as becoming less. Less warm. Less fast. Less certain that the face in the mirror was the face of something that intended to continue.

He pressed a towel to his jaw. He looked at himself. The red leather jacket hung on the bathroom door behind him, and in the mirror it appeared to be hanging in front of him, on his chest, like a second skin he had not yet put on.

Fourteen months in the City. Or sixteen. He had stopped counting.

---

The sound broke everything.

Not because it was loud — though it was. Not because it was unexpected — though it was that too. It broke everything because it was *wrong.* It did not belong. It was a sound from another century, another physics, another philosophy of motion — a combustion engine, gasoline-fed, mechanically timed, with the specific ragged thunder of eight cylinders firing in a sequence that no algorithm had optimized because no algorithm had been asked to.

Valentine was on the waterfront. Evening. The usual walk. The usual nothing. The Hala was wearing off and the edges were coming back and the black bay was flat and the transit pods were silent and the City was doing what the City always did — providing, providing, providing, like a host who will not let you leave the table.

The car came down the Embarcadero like a fist through a window.

Red. Not the red of the leather jacket — brighter, angrier, a red that had been chosen not for beauty but for visibility, for the specific purpose of being seen from a great distance and understood immediately as a thing that did not give a damn. A 1977 Pontiac Trans Am Firebird. Valentine knew this the way he knew all things — instantly, taxonomically, without feeling. But the feeling came anyway, uninvited, the way feelings always came to him now: a jolt, a stutter in the chest, a disruption in the long flat line of nothing that his life had become.

The car pulled to the curb. The engine idled. It sounded like an animal breathing — rough, alive, impatient with the concept of stillness.

The driver leaned out the window. Young. Dark-skinned. A face built for trouble — sharp jaw, wide mouth, eyes that moved faster than they should, cataloguing everything with the specific, restless intelligence of someone who has been underestimated so many times it has become a tactical advantage. He wore a white T-shirt and a gold chain and his hair was cut close and his grin was the kind of grin that starts bar fights and finishes them and buys everyone a drink afterward.

He looked at Valentine. At the red leather jacket that didn't fit. At the face that was technically perfect and existentially wrecked. At the man who was standing on a waterfront in a dead city in a dead man's jacket with the posture of someone who had not been surprised in two years and had forgotten that surprise was possible.

"Yo," the driver said. "You look like someone shoved a soul into a mannequin and told it to be sad about it." He killed the engine. It coughed once, died, and the silence that followed was louder than the noise had been. He opened the door and stepped out and he was tall — taller than Valentine — and he moved with the loose, careless economy of someone who trusts his own body completely, the way only the very young or the very reckless trust anything.

He leaned against the car. Crossed his arms. The gold chain caught the light of the City's ambient glow and threw a small bright point across Valentine's chest, right over the heart, like a laser sight.

"I've been looking for you," he said. "Took me six weeks. You're not easy to find when you're busy doing absolutely nothing." He pushed off the car and extended a hand — long fingers, calloused palms, a hand that had worked for a living in a city where work was a quaint anachronism. "Name's Pace."

Valentine did not take the hand.

Pace left it there. Patient. Grinning.

"Yeah," he said. "We'll get to that. But right now, you and me — we gotta go. Because in about twenty minutes, a very expensive and very unfriendly team of Engram Corp auditors is going to arrive at your extremely depressing apartment, and when they find you gone, they're going to be unhappy, and when they're unhappy, people like you and me stop existing in the kinds of records that prove we were ever here."

The engine of the Trans Am ticked as it cooled. The black bay was flat. The transit pods slid overhead in their silent rails.

Valentine looked at Pace. Looked at the car. Looked at the waterfront, where two hours ago the fishermen had thrown something back into the water and the ripples had formed a pattern that almost meant something.

"Where?" Valentine said.

Pace opened the passenger door. The interior was cracked leather and cigarette burns and a smell that was not unlike the Maestro's jacket — old, human, earned.

"Somewhere loud," Pace said. "Somewhere stupid. Somewhere that ain't this."

He got in the car.

Pace walked around to the driver's side, dropped into the seat, and turned the key. The engine caught. The Embarcadero shuddered. Somewhere in the City's ambient system, a sensor registered an anomalous acoustic event and filed it under *resolved* without investigation, because the system was built to process, not to understand, and the sound of a combustion engine in Neo San Francisco was so far outside its parameters that it simply did not exist.

The Trans Am pulled away from the curb. The tires barked once against the polymer-treated asphalt. The exhaust hung in the air — blue and acrid and beautiful — and then the ventilation system took it, scrubbed it, converted it to citrus, and pretended it had never been.

The City went on providing.

But Valentine was no longer there to receive.
