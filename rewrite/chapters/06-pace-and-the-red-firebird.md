# Chapter 6: Pace and the Red Firebird

---

The car was red the way a wound is red. Not painted — stained. A 1979 Pontiac Trans Am Firebird with a gold eagle screaming across its hood and rust eating the wheel wells and an exhaust note that sounded like a building being demolished one floor at a time. It sat on the waterfront with its engine running, vibrating the puddles, and Valentine stood on the sidewalk and stared at it the way you stare at something from a dream that has followed you into the day.

The window came down. Cigarette smoke. A face behind it — sharp, brown, grinning so wide it looked like the grin was wearing the face and not the other way around.

"Get in."

Valentine didn't move.

"I said get in. You're Valentine, right? V9? I know you're V9 because you've got that look. That whole — " The driver circled his cigarette in the air. " — slowly realizing I'm furniture look. I had it too. It passes. Get in the car."

Valentine got in the car.

The interior smelled like leather and Hala and something burning that was probably the engine. The seats were cracked. The dashboard was analog — dials, needles, a speedometer that went to 160 and looked like it had been there. A pine tree air freshener hung from the rearview mirror, which was either ironic or insane, and Valentine couldn't tell which because the driver was already talking.

"Name's Pace. V5. Got out — let me think — six years ago? Seven? Time does something weird when you're not on the cycle anymore, it kind of goes flat, like a soda you left open, you know? Anyway. Six, seven years. I look good for it, right?"

He did not look good for it. He looked like a man who had been handsome once and was now being handsome by force of will alone. His skin had the dull translucence that Valentine recognized from his own mirror — the dissolution, the slow going-glass — but further along. His hands on the steering wheel were thin. His knuckles were sharp. His eyes were the only part of him that seemed to have gained something rather than lost it, and what they had gained was speed.

Pace put the car in gear and the world lurched.

They hit the avenue doing sixty in a zone marked for fifteen and the Firebird's engine opened up like a chest cracked for surgery and the sound filled the glass corridor between the towers and bounced back and filled it again and Valentine's teeth buzzed in his skull and the buildings blurred into a single silver wall and Pace was laughing, one hand on the wheel, the other holding his cigarette out the window where the cherry tore off in the slipstream, and he said, "You feel that? That's internal combustion, baby. Fossil fuel. Dead dinosaurs exploding a thousand times a second in an iron block under your ass. They don't make this anymore. They don't make anything that makes this sound anymore. This is a museum piece. This is a war crime. I love this car more than I've ever loved a person and I've loved three people and one of them was myself."

A traffic drone banked toward them, red light flashing. Pace swerved into the oncoming lane, split two autonomous pods that scattered like startled fish, cut back across, ran a signal that was so red it was almost purple, and never stopped talking.

"So here's the thing. You're dissolving. I know. I can see it. Don't get weird about it — I'm dissolving too, we're all dissolving, it's like a club, worst club in the world, no jackets. The difference is I found something that slows it down. Not a cure. There's no cure. But a — what's the word — a countermeasure. You need sensation. Real sensation. The body's forgetting it's a body, right? That's the dissolution. You stop feeling, you stop being. So you need to remind it. Loudly. With enthusiasm."

He downshifted. The engine screamed. They shot through an intersection and Valentine saw a pedestrian's mouth open in a perfect O and then they were past.

"That's what the underground is for."

---

Pace talked the way some people fight — constantly, from every angle, with no apparent strategy but an underlying rhythm that was hard to interrupt. He talked about his escape from the system ("walked out the back of a reprogramming facility in Sector 12, they'd left a fire door propped open with a brick, a brick, I'm not kidding, the most advanced containment facility on the West Coast and I owe my freedom to a masonry product"), about the first years off-grid ("slept in a parking structure, ate whatever the delivery drones dropped when their GPS glitched, became briefly religious, got over it"), about finding the underground ("followed the sound, man, you can always follow the sound"), and about the Firebird ("bought her from an old man in the Outer Marina who didn't know what he had, paid him in Hala, which in retrospect was not morally ideal but listen — I needed this car and morality is a luxury item and I was shopping at the discount store of existence").

Valentine said almost nothing. He sat in the passenger seat and let the city pour past and felt the vibration of the engine in the bones of his pelvis, his spine, the backs of his hands. Two years of silence were being shaken loose. Something in his chest was recalibrating.

"You play music, right?" Pace said. "Piano or something. I heard about you. Word gets around in the V-network — and yeah, there's a network, kind of, more like a rumor chain, like a game of telephone played by ghosts. Anyway. Someone said V9 could play. Said the island had a piano and you played it and something happened. Something with structures. Light."

Valentine looked at him.

"I don't play anymore."

"Sure. Sure you don't. And I don't drink." Pace pulled a vial of Hala from his jacket pocket, cracked it with his teeth, and drank it in one motion without taking his eyes off the road. The liquid was pale blue. It hit him like a wave — his pupils dilated, his grip on the wheel tightened, and for a half-second his face went blank and beautiful and completely empty, like a screen between images. Then he was back.

"Okay," he said. "Going down."

He turned the wheel hard and the Firebird dropped off the avenue onto a service ramp that spiraled into the dark.

---

The underground was below the city the way a heart is below a ribcage — hidden, essential, beating.

They descended through four levels of abandoned transit infrastructure. The ramp gave way to a tunnel, the tunnel to a wider passage where the walls were tiled in cracked white ceramic and the fluorescent tubes overhead had been replaced with neon — pink, green, gold — that turned the air into something you could almost chew. The temperature rose. Ten degrees. Twenty. The Firebird's tires hissed on the wet concrete and Valentine rolled down his window and the heat came in like breath.

Then the tunnel opened and the underground was there.

It was enormous. A vaulted chamber — old rail terminus, maybe, or a maintenance bay — stretched out under the city like a cathedral turned on its side. The ceiling was lost in shadow and smoke. The floor was packed dirt and oil stains and tire marks layered so thick they looked geological. Along the walls: pit stalls built from scaffolding and corrugated metal, hung with trouble lights and draped with cables. Vehicles everywhere — machines that had no business existing. A gutted ambulance on racing slicks. A three-wheeled thing with a jet intake bolted to its spine. Something low and angular that looked like it had been designed by an architect having a nervous breakdown. And people. Dozens, hundreds — mechanics with welding visors pushed up on their foreheads, bookmakers standing on overturned crates shouting odds, spectators pressed against the chain-link barriers that lined the straightaway, and everywhere the smoke and the noise and the heat and the smell: exhaust, ozone, Hala, sweat, burnt rubber, and underneath it all something older and more animal, like the smell of a crowd that has gathered to watch something live or die.

Pace parked the Firebird in a gap between a stripped-down bus and a vehicle that appeared to be a motorcycle fused with a refrigerator. He killed the engine. The silence lasted exactly one second before the underground's own sound rushed in — engines revving in the pits, the clang of tools, music from somewhere playing something with a bassline that Valentine felt in his molars.

"Welcome home," Pace said.

---

They walked. Pace knew everyone. He moved through the underground like a current through water — shaking hands, bumping fists, trading insults with a speed and affection that blurred the line between. A mechanic with grease to her elbows called him a degenerate. A bookmaker in a three-piece suit missing its jacket handed him a Hala vial and said he was three races behind on a debt. A girl with no hair and chrome tattoos running up her neck looked at Pace with an expression that was either love or the memory of love's eviction, and Pace touched her shoulder as he passed and said nothing.

"This is Valentine," he told people. "He's new. Be gentle. He's fragile."

"I'm not fragile," Valentine said.

"Brother, you are so fragile I can see your soul through your skin. But that's okay. Down here fragile is just another word for fast. The less you've got, the less drag. Am I right?"

Nobody confirmed this. Pace didn't need confirmation. He was already moving.

He explained as they walked. The economy first. Hala was the currency — vials traded like cash, denominations marked by color. Pale blue was standard. Green was cut with something that made you forget faster. Gold was pure and almost impossible to get and Pace said he'd had it once and it was like being born in reverse. But the real currency, the deep currency, the thing that separated the underground from every other black market in Neo San Francisco, was memory.

"You can bet your memories," Pace said. "Literal. There's a guy — they call him the Clerk — he's got a rig that can extract and store. You put up a memory, the Clerk pulls it, holds it in escrow. You win, you get it back. You lose, it belongs to whoever won the bet. They can play it. Wear it. Sell it."

Valentine stopped walking.

"That's —"

"Horrifying? Yeah. Also addictive. Also the only honest economy I've ever seen. Money's abstract. Memory is real. When you bet a memory you are saying: this moment of my life is worth this much to me, and I am willing to lose it. That's price discovery, Valentine. That's the market functioning as intended."

They passed the bookmakers' row. Men and women stood behind tables made from car doors laid flat on cinder blocks. Odds were written on whiteboards in handwriting that changed between races. The names were all wrong — not names at all but titles, epithets, descriptions. The Butcher. Glass Jane. Kid Phosphorus. El Mal. And at the top of every board, in every bookmaker's hand, circled or underlined or written larger than the rest: the Spirit Cyborg.

"Who is that?" Valentine asked.

Pace's grin did something complicated. It stayed on his face but left his eyes.

"Nobody knows. That's the whole thing. The Spirit Cyborg shows up, races, wins, and leaves. Full helmet. Black car — and I mean black, not painted black, black like the absence of the concept of color. No pit crew. No sponsors. No voice on the radio. Just shows up, goes faster than anything else down here, and vanishes. Been doing it for two years. Never lost."

"Someone must know."

"Someone must. But nobody does. Or nobody's talking. And down here, those are the same thing."

Valentine looked at the boards. The Spirit Cyborg's odds were so short they were almost flat — 1.05, 1.08. Nobody bet against. Everyone bet on, taking the sure thing, grinding small margins.

"Come on," Pace said. "Race is about to start."

---

They found a spot along the barrier. The chain-link was warm under Valentine's fingers. The straightaway ran the length of the chamber — a quarter mile, maybe more, the far end dissolving into dark and neon haze. The track was lined with old tires stacked three high and spectators pressed against them, faces lit from below by the glow of their own devices, from above by the neon, from the side by the headlights of the machines lining up at the start.

Six vehicles. Valentine counted them. The three-wheeled jet thing. A modified sedan with its body cut down to the frame. Something that looked homemade and suicidal. Two others he couldn't categorize. And at the far left of the line, engine idling with a sound like a held breath, the Spirit Cyborg.

It was black. Pace was right — not painted black but black in a way that refused reflection. The body was low, aerodynamic, with no visible seams or bolts or ornamentation. It looked less like a car than like a hole in the shape of a car, a space where a car should be. The driver sat behind a windshield so dark it was opaque. Full helmet. Visor down. Hands on the wheel, gloved, still.

A horn sounded. Not a starting gun — a ship's horn, deep and mournful, mounted somewhere in the ceiling. The sound rolled through the chamber and the crowd surged against the barriers and the engines rose and Valentine felt the noise in his sternum and his wrists and the backs of his knees.

They launched. The sound hit a frequency that stopped being sound and became pressure — a wall of noise that flattened Valentine's shirt against his chest and vibrated the chain-link into a blur. The three-wheeler took the lead for two seconds and then the modified sedan passed it so closely that sparks flew where their bodies kissed and the homemade thing fishtailed and hit the tire wall and disintegrated into a cloud of parts that rained down like shrapnel and the crowd screamed not in horror but in something closer to ecstasy. The remaining machines tore down the straightaway trailing exhaust and noise and the neon turned their bodies into streaks of color — red, silver, a blue so bright it burned — and then the Spirit Cyborg moved. It didn't accelerate. It relocated. One moment it was fifth and the next it was first and the distance between those two facts seemed to have been skipped rather than crossed. It hit the end of the straight, braked once, and the red taillights flared like two eyes opening in the dark.

The crowd roared. The sound shook dust from the ceiling. Hala vials clinked in pockets.

Valentine realized he was gripping the chain-link so hard the wire had cut into his palms. He realized his mouth was open. He realized — and this was the thing, the thing that mattered, the thing the chapter turned on — that he could feel his heartbeat. Not as an idea. Not as a concept he understood intellectually, the way he understood that he had lungs and they breathed and blood moved. He could feel it. The actual muscle. Contracting in his chest. Beating against his ribs like a fist knocking on a door.

Two years. Two years of going numb, going glass, feeling his body become a theory of a body, and here in this hot loud filthy place full of smoke and strangers he could feel his own heart and it was beating and it was beating and it was beating.

---

Later. The crowd thinned. The Spirit Cyborg was gone — pulled out before the dust settled, black car vanishing up a tunnel exit that nobody else seemed to use. The wrecked vehicle was being stripped for parts by a team of kids who moved with the coordinated efficiency of ants dismantling a carcass. The bookmakers were settling bets. Somewhere, someone was arguing about a foul. Somewhere else, someone was crying.

Pace sat on the Firebird's hood with his legs hanging over the grille and his back against the windshield and a Hala vial in each hand, one empty, one half-gone. His eyes were soft and distant. The dissolution was visible in the neon — his skin catching light it shouldn't catch, his edges going uncertain, like a photograph of a photograph.

Valentine leaned against the fender. His palms were bleeding. He hadn't wiped them.

"You come here every night?" he asked.

"Every night there's a race. Most nights there's a race. When there's no race I come anyway. Sit in the car. Listen to the tunnels. The tunnels have a sound, did you know that? Like breathing. Like the city's sleeping and this is where it breathes."

Pace finished the second vial. Tossed it. It clinked against the concrete and rolled into the dark.

"My grandfather," he said, and then stopped. Started again. "My grandfather's on Earth. Real Earth. Dirt and weather and all that. He doesn't know what I am. Thinks I'm his actual grandson. The original Pace — whoever that was, whatever he was — he had a grandfather on a farm somewhere in the Central Valley and the old man still writes letters. Paper letters. I get them sometimes through a forwarding chain. Six months old by the time they reach me. He talks about the crops. The dog. Whether it rained."

Pace looked at the ceiling. Smoke moved up there like slow water.

"I write back. I make things up. Tell him I'm doing fine. Got a job. Got a girl. Normal stuff. Human stuff." He laughed. It sounded like a cough. "I've never met him. He loves me because he thinks I'm someone else. And I love him because he's the only person in the world who doesn't know what I am and I would rather die than let him find out."

Valentine said nothing. There was nothing to say. The underground hummed around them — the generators, the ventilation, the distant sound of an engine being tested, revving and falling, revving and falling, like something trying to speak.

"You know what's worse than dying?" Pace said.

Valentine waited.

"Dying with nothing you'd miss."

The words sat in the air. Pace's face was half-lit, half-dark. The Hala was in him and his eyes were bright and wet and he was smiling the way people smile when they are telling you something true and hoping you will mistake it for a joke.

Valentine looked out at the underground. The pit stalls with their trouble lights. The oil-stained dirt. The kids stripping the wreck. The bookmakers folding their tables. The neon — pink, green, gold — reflecting off the wet walls in long trembling lines. The heat. The stink. The noise fading but not gone, never gone, just pulling back like a tide that would return.

He thought about the island. The white compound. The gardens, green and silent. The studio with its piano. The perfection of it — every surface clean, every line considered, every molecule arranged with intent. Beautiful. And dead now. Burned. A place so perfect it could only end one way.

This was the opposite. This was a place that had never been perfect and never would be. A place built from wreckage and lit by the wrong kind of light and filled with people who were breaking and knew it and came anyway because the alternative was to break alone and in silence. The island was perfection slowly dying. This was ugliness furiously alive.

His heart was still beating. He could still feel it. The muscle. The rhythm. The blood.

"Pace," he said.

"Yeah."

"I want to come back."

Pace looked at him. The grin returned — not the manic one from the car, not the performer's grin, but something smaller and realer, a grin that fit his face like it belonged there.

"Brother," he said, "you never left."

He slid off the hood. Pulled his keys from his pocket. The Firebird chirped once — an analog sound, a mechanical sound, a sound from a century that believed in combustion and momentum and the possibility that speed was a form of prayer.

"Come on," Pace said. "I'll drive you home. And by home I mean the waterfront where I found you because I'm guessing you don't have an actual home and honestly neither do I so at least we've got that in common."

They got in the car. The engine turned over. The underground shook.

They drove up into the city and the glass towers caught the first grey light of morning and Valentine sat in the passenger seat with his cut palms open on his knees and his heart beating and the taste of exhaust and Hala in the back of his throat and he thought: I am alive. I don't know for how long. But right now, tonight, in this stupid red car with this dying man, I am alive and I can feel it and that is enough.

The Firebird's engine echoed between the buildings. Somewhere below, the underground breathed.

Pace turned on the radio. Static. He turned it off.

"Radio's broken," he said. "Has been for years. I turn it on sometimes anyway. Force of habit. Optimism. One of those."

The city moved past. The light came up. The car went on.
