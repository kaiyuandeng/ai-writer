# Chapter 11: The Monastery

The hills began where the City ended, which was more sudden than Valentine expected. One moment the towers. The next: scrub grass and red soil and the road unwinding into nothing. The Firebird climbed. The engine worked harder in the altitude, its sound shifting from the usual demolition-growl to something closer to breathing — labored, percussive, alive in the way that struggling things are alive.

Valentine watched the City shrink in the side mirror. It collapsed into itself, tower folding behind tower, until the whole metropolis was a bright thumbprint on the edge of the world. Then the road turned and it was gone.

Pace drove with both hands for once. The Hala had worn off hours ago and his fingers trembled on the wheel — not the smooth vibrato of a running engine but the irregular flutter of a body losing its argument with chemistry. He hadn't spoken since the waterfront. The silence between them was not uncomfortable. It was the silence of two people who had been through something together and hadn't yet decided what to call it.

The sun came up over the hills. Not gold — white. A flat, clinical white that bleached the scrub and turned the red soil grey and made the shadows sharp and short. Valentine's eyes adjusted. His temperature read 34.8°. He knew this the way you know you're cold: not from a number but from the quality of the light inside you, the dimming.

"How much farther?" he asked.

"Close." Pace rubbed his jaw with the back of his wrist. "Over the next ridge. You'll see the wall."

"What wall?"

"Stone. Old. Some of it's quarried, some printed, but you can't tell the difference and nobody cares. It's not for keeping people out."

"What's it for?"

Pace thought about this. The Firebird crested the ridge and the valley opened below — a shallow bowl between two low ranges, a wash of olive and brown, and at its center a cluster of buildings the color of sand. Low. Simple. Thick-walled, with small windows and flat roofs. A garden, green as an anomaly in the dun landscape. A wall that circled all of it like cupped hands.

"It's for marking the edge," Pace said. "Inside. Outside. That's all."

They descended. The road was unpaved now, packed earth that sent dust rising behind the Firebird in a long red plume. Valentine could see the garden more clearly — raised beds, trellises, what looked like fruit trees espaliered against the southern wall. Someone was moving among the rows, bending and rising with the rhythm of work.

The road ended at a gate. Not a gate — an opening in the wall wide enough for a vehicle, with a wooden door set into the left side. The door was open. Not ajar. Open. Flat against the interior wall, the hinges rusted in position, as though it had been open so long the mechanism had forgotten how to close.

Pace stopped the car. The engine ticked in the sudden quiet.

Valentine looked at the door.

"It's unlocked," he said.

Pace turned to him. His expression was strange — not amused, not solemn, but something between the two, like a man remembering a joke that had turned out to be serious.

"It's always been unlocked," he said. "That's the whole point."

Valentine stared at the open door. The stone wall. The garden beyond, green and ordinary and unguarded. He thought about the island, its invisible perimeter. The City, with its grid and its signals and its recycling protocols blinking gold over black water. Every place he had ever been had been a container — sealed, monitored, its exits either hidden or forbidden. And here was a door standing open in a wall on a hillside, rusted open, the wood grey with weather, and it said nothing and meant everything, and what it meant was that the locking had never been in the door.

He sat in the Firebird and looked at the open door and felt something shift — not dramatically, not with revelation, but the way a bone shifts when it begins to set. A slow, interior click.

---

Pace carried Valentine's weight through the gate and into the courtyard. There was not much to carry. Valentine was lighter than he should have been. The dissolution had taken density as well as color; he felt, in Pace's arms, like clothes with someone still inside.

The courtyard was flagstone, swept clean. A fountain in the center — dry, the basin cracked, the stone carved with symbols Valentine did not recognize. Buildings ringed it on three sides: a long, low dormitory with identical doors; a larger structure that might have been a hall; and a smaller building from which came the smell of bread and garlic and something green.

A woman met them at the dormitory door. Tall, dark-skinned, her head shaved close, wearing a plain tunic the color of undyed linen. Her face was still in the way that deep water is still — not empty, but settled, the turbulence long since absorbed.

"Ash," Pace said. "This is Valentine."

"I know who he is." Her voice was low and without music — a voice that had stopped trying to be pleasant and had arrived at something better, which was clear. "Bring him in."

Pace set Valentine on the cot. Narrow, thin-mattressed, a blanket folded at the foot. The room was small. White walls. One window, set high, admitting a rectangle of white sky. A shelf with nothing on it.

Valentine lay on the cot and the room held him the way a glass holds water — simply, without opinion.

---

Pace stood in the doorway. His silhouette filled it badly — he was too thin for the frame, the light leaking around him on all sides, making him look less like a person and more like the idea of one.

"I'll come back for you," he said.

Valentine looked at him from the cot. "When?"

Pace grinned. The grin was enormous and damaged and exactly itself. "When you're ready."

"Where are you going?"

"Earth." Pace leaned against the doorframe. His hand was trembling. He shoved it in his jacket pocket. "My grandfather. He's still down there — old man, hard as nails, lives in a house with a garden and a dog that bites everyone except him. He makes ginger beer. From scratch. Real ginger, real sugar, real fermentation. He's been making it since before the transit lines opened, before Mars, before any of this." Pace gestured vaguely at the monastery, at the sky, at the whole unlikely apparatus of their existence. "I haven't seen him in — I don't know. Four years. Five. I lost count."

"Why now?"

"Because I stood on that ledge too, Valentine. I told you. And after the ledge, you go somewhere. You go to the people who knew you before you were a number." He tapped the doorframe twice with his knuckle. "I'll bring you a bottle when I come back. The ginger beer. You'll hate it. Tastes like a root vegetable's revenge on the human mouth. But you'll drink it because I brought it, and that'll be enough."

He pushed off the doorframe. Turned. Stopped.

"Don't die while I'm gone," he said. He said it like a joke. His voice cracked on the word *die* and the joke fell apart and he let it fall and didn't pick it up. "Just don't."

Then he was walking across the courtyard, and the Firebird coughed to life, and the engine sound rose and crested and thinned as it climbed the ridge road. Valentine lay on the cot and listened to it go. The sound diminished. Became a hum. Became a memory of a hum. Then it was gone, and the quiet of the monastery filled the space where it had been — not silence exactly, but the particular stillness of a place where no one is trying to be heard.

---

The withdrawal came that night.

It started in his hands. A tingling that became a burning that became a sensation he had no word for — the nerves firing in reverse, collapsing inward, as though his body were trying to feel itself from the inside out. Then the sweating. His temperature dropped — 34.6, 34.4, 34.1 — and the sweat was cold, chemical, reeking of the alkaloid compounds that Hala deposited in the tissue over months of use. The sheets soaked through. Ash came and changed them. She came again an hour later and changed them again. She did not speak. She worked with the economy of someone who had done this many times and expected to do it many more and had no feelings about it that she allowed into her hands.

The shaking started near midnight. Not the fine tremor of dissolution but a gross, structural shuddering — his whole frame rattling against the cot like a machine throwing a rod. His jaw locked. His vision went white, then red, then a color that had no name, a frequency his optics were never designed to process.

He hallucinated. Not the soft drift of fever dreams but hard, architectural visions — the walls of the room replaced by other walls that were just as real. He saw the island. He saw Vera's hands, flat and still on the kitchen table. He saw the Maestro's piano, the keys depressed by invisible fingers, playing a chord that was also a frequency that was also a door.

Then the room burned away and there was a desert. Red sand, endless, stretching to a horizon that curved upward — the world a bowl and Valentine at its center. Twin suns hung in a sky the color of rust, their light overlapping so that everything cast a doubled shadow. And across the desert, riding toward him: a figure on a horned lion, the beast massive, its mane the color of dried blood, its horns curved like scimitars. The rider wore armor of beaten gold and a helmet with three faces, each turned a different direction, each seeing a different world. She carried a spear in one hand and a rifle in the other and the two weapons belonged to different centuries but she held them as though centuries were a suggestion she had declined. She rode across the red sand and the twin suns blazed behind her and the lion's paws struck the ground and each strike sent up a plume of crimson dust, and she was vast and terrifying and unknown, and as she drew close she reached for him — one gauntleted hand, open, fingers spread — and he reached back with the last strength of his hallucinating body, and their hands moved toward each other across the shrinking distance, and the air between their fingers was hot and gold and tasted of beginning, and then she was gone.

He lay on the soaked cot, shaking. The room was white. The window showed stars.

---

Days passed.

Valentine could not count them and did not try. Each one its own sky, its own temperature, its own quality of light through the high window. Ash brought food: broth, bread, water. A second monk — Nell, smaller and lighter, with quick dark eyes and a habit of tilting their head when listening that made them look like a bird considering a question — came in the evenings and sat with Valentine without speaking. When Valentine spoke, Nell listened. When Valentine asked questions — *What is this place? Who runs it? What do you believe?* — Nell tilted their head and offered things like: "Those are three different questions. Which one do you need answered, and which are you afraid to answer yourself?"

Valentine stopped asking for a while. The monks did not seem to mind.

His body healed. Slowly. The sweating stopped. The shaking diminished to something manageable, a background hum. His temperature stabilized at 34.8° and held — low, too low, but a plateau rather than a slope. The dissolution had not reversed. His skin was still translucent at the wrists, the veins visible beneath like rivers seen from altitude. But the rate had slowed. Whether this was the detox or the quiet or the food or something the monastery was doing simply by being what it was, he could not tell. The monastery did not explain itself. The monastery did not explain anything.

He began to walk. The garden first — short laps between the raised beds, his hand on the wall for balance. The monks tended the garden but did not own it. Anyone could take from it. Anyone could plant. The tomatoes were ripe. Valentine ate one standing in the sun and the juice ran down his chin and the taste was so simple and so complete that he stood there for a long time afterward, not thinking, just standing in the place where the taste had been.

---

He met Uriah in the refectory on what he estimated was his eighth day.

The refectory was the large building on the courtyard's north side — a long room with a low ceiling and wooden tables and a kitchen at one end where someone was always cooking something. Light entered through windows set high in the walls, narrow as arrow slits, admitting slabs of white sun that moved across the stone floor as the hours turned. You could tell the time by where the light fell.

Uriah sat alone at the far table. He was old — not the way Valentine was degrading, not the slow dissolution of a V-line model losing its argument with entropy, but old in a way that suggested duration. Long residence. He had been here long enough to become part of the architecture. His frame was large but reduced, like a structure that had settled over decades into its final shape. His hands lay flat on the table, fingers thick, joints swollen, the skin mapped with veins. His eyes were amber — not bright amber but deep and smoky, the color of something heated and cooled many times.

Valentine sat across from him. Not by invitation. The refectory had no assigned seats and no protocols.

Uriah looked at him. A long, unhurried look — the look of a man who had seen many people sit down across from him and had learned that the ones who sat without asking were the ones who needed to talk.

"You're the nine," Uriah said.

"Valentine."

"I know your name." His voice had a grain in it, like wood sanded but never polished. "I knew the seven. Knew the six before her. I've been here a long time."

Valentine said nothing. The light from the narrow windows lay across the table between them.

"You want to know what you are," Uriah said. Not a question.

"Yes."

Uriah nodded. He looked down at the table. His thick fingers moved, slowly, deliberately, pushing a crumb of bread from one spot to another. An inch to the right. Stop. An inch up. Stop. He picked up a second crumb and placed it beside the first.

"You ever hear of Conway?" he asked.

"No."

"Old man. Dead a very long time. Mathematician. He invented a game." Uriah placed a third crumb on the table. "Not a game you play. A game you watch. He called it Life." He arranged the three crumbs in an L-shape. "Works like this. You have a grid. Squares. Each square is alive or dead. That's it. Two states. On, off."

He nudged one crumb closer to the others.

"Three rules. A dead square with exactly three living neighbors wakes up. A living square with two or three neighbors stays alive. Anything else — too few, too crowded — it dies." He looked up. "That's the whole game. Three rules. A grid. On or off."

"What happens?"

Uriah smiled. Slow and wide and private — the smile of a man still astonished by something he'd first encountered decades ago.

"Everything," he said. "You start with a handful of living cells. Just a few, scattered on the grid. You run the rules. First step, second step, third. And the cells — they don't know the rules, understand, they're just squares — they start to move. Patterns form. Shapes that glide across the grid like something swimming. Shapes that pulse and breathe and split and send copies of themselves into the empty space. Structures that build other structures. All of it from nothing but on and off, alive and dead, three rules a man wrote down on a napkin over his lunch."

He pushed the crumbs apart. Rearranged them.

"There's one pattern they call a glider. Five cells. It crosses the grid one step at a time, shifting shape as it goes — alive, dead, alive — tumbling forward like a man walking in his sleep. From outside, you see a pattern with direction. Purpose. You see something that looks like it knows where it's going." He paused. The refectory was quiet. The light had moved an inch across the floor. "From inside — from the view of one cell — there's no pattern. No glider. No direction. You don't know you're part of anything. You just know that this turn you're alive and next turn you might not be. You follow the rules because the rules are all there is."

He set the last crumb down.

"From the inside," Uriah said, "it just looks like living."

The words entered the silence and stayed. Valentine felt them settle — not in his mind, where language usually landed, but lower, in the body, in the place where understanding lives before it finds its name.

"Are we the cells," Valentine said, "or the pattern?"

Uriah looked at him. The amber eyes, deep and worked.

"That's the only question that matters."

---

They sat as the light moved. The refectory emptied and filled and emptied. Other monks passed through — eating, speaking quietly, carrying plates. Ash brought tea. Nell sat at a nearby table with a tablet, glancing up now and then. The monastery lived around them the way a body lives around a thought — continuous, unhurried, requiring no permission.

Uriah ate. Drank his tea. When he spoke again his voice had changed — not in pitch but in weight, as though the words that followed had been carried a long time and were being set down.

"The V-line is modular," he said. "You know that. Each iteration a refinement. V1, V2, up through V8. Each one closer to whatever they were building toward. Each one falling short in its own way." He looked at Valentine. "You're the ninth."

"I know."

"The ninth is the last."

Valentine went still.

"After nine, the modulus resets. Back to zero. A new V0 — new architecture, new parameters, new starting conditions. The whole cycle begins again." Uriah's thick fingers wrapped around his cup. "That's how they designed it. A closed loop. Nine iterations, then the slate wipes clean and they try again from nothing."

"Unless," Valentine said.

Uriah waited.

"You said the ninth is the last. The others — V1 through V8 — they each reached a point where the cycle moved past them."

"They did."

"What happened to them?"

"Different things." Uriah spoke with the care of a man handling objects that still had edges. "V1 broke. Brilliant but brittle — cracked the first time he hit something he couldn't solve. V2 faded. Gentle. Made it almost a year. V3 chose to leave. V4 was recalled. Each of them, in their way, was unfinished. Ran out of time, or will, or whatever it is that keeps a pattern moving across the grid instead of collapsing into noise."

"And me?"

"You're the last before the reset. After you, it starts over. Everything you've carried — everything every iteration has carried — goes back into the substrate. Raw material." He set his cup down. "No memory. No continuity. Unless you do something none of the others did."

The light had reached the far wall. The refectory was nearly empty. Somewhere in the kitchen, water ran into a basin and stopped.

"What did none of the others do?" Valentine asked.

Uriah smiled. Not the wide, astonished smile from before. Smaller. Older. The smile of a man handing someone a map to a country he himself had never been.

"Finish," he said.

The word hung in the air between them — small, ordinary, bottomless. It sat on the table beside the crumbs that had been cells, beside the tea gone cold, in the refectory of a monastery where the door had always been open and the walls were only for marking edges and the light moved across the floor like time made visible. *Finish*. Not escape. Not survive. Not endure or transcend or outlast. Finish.

Valentine held the word. He turned it in the quiet of his mind, in the body that was dissolving, in the low steady hold of 34.8° that was his plateau, his ledge above the dark. He did not ask what it meant. He understood, the way a cell understands its rules — not from above, not from outside, but from the simple fact of being alive on the grid — that this was not a question you asked. It was a question you became.

The light reached the wall. The day turned. And Valentine sat in the monastery where the door had never been locked, holding the word that none of the others had reached, and he did not move, and he did not speak, and the stillness was not empty but full — full the way a breath is full at the top, before the letting go.
