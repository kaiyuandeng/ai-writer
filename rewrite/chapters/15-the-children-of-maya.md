# Chapter 15: The Children of Maya

The dream came again.

Anais woke on the roof with her fingers clawing stone, her shift soaked through, her teeth locked against the scream that wanted out. The night air of Maya pressed against her skin like a cool hand. Above her, the twin moons hung in their eternal argument — Rho pale and swollen, Kaph sharp as a sickle — and the stars between them were wrong. They were always wrong after the dream. Too many. Too close. As if the sky remembered a configuration it had never worn.

She sat up. Drew her knees to her chest.

The dream: a planet that was blue. Not blue like the fountains in the lower courtyard or blue like the veins in Auntie's wrist-seams. Blue like an eye. Blue like grief. The whole world wrapped in it — water and gas and light refracting through an atmosphere so thick it looked edible. And people. Millions of them. Billions. Packed into towers of glass that caught the light of a single sun and threw it back as commerce. They rode inside metal boxes that moved on black rivers. They spoke into black rectangles they held in their hands. They spoke constantly, all of them, their mouths shaping words that the air swallowed before anyone could hear.

They spoke but never listened.

Anais pressed her palms against her eyes until she saw phosphenes. The dream always left a residue — a taste like copper, a pressure behind her sternum, the conviction that she had witnessed something real. Not a story. Not a myth from the Hazzan's book. Something that had happened, was happening, would happen. She was eight years old and she did not have the language for temporal displacement, but she knew the feeling: time folding over itself like wet cloth, one layer pressing through another.

A sound. Stone against stone. The scrape of a sandal on the palace's western wall.

She froze. The wall rose forty cubits from the garden floor to the parapet where she sat. No handholds. No ledges. The architects of Maya had designed it that way — the Hazzan's family was meant to be unreachable from below, sealed in their high rooms like figures in a reliquary. And yet something was climbing it.

Anais pressed herself flat against the warm stone. The scraping grew louder. A hand appeared over the parapet's edge — brown-gold, the sunskin catching moonlight like hammered metal — and then a forearm, a shoulder, a face she knew better than her own.

"Aharah," she hissed.

Her sister hauled herself over the edge and landed in a crouch. Twelve years old, compact as a fist, breathing hard. Her training wrap was torn at the shoulder and her knees were bleeding where the sandstone had bitten through the cloth. The sunskin along her arms blazed faintly — a side effect of exertion, the photosynthetic pigment metabolizing moonlight into fuel. She looked, Anais thought, like a lantern someone had tried to put out.

"Go back to bed," Aharah said.

"You go back to bed."

"I'm training."

"You're bleeding."

Aharah glanced at her knees as if noticing them for the first time. She wiped the blood with the hem of her wrap. "It's nothing."

"If Nala sees—"

"Nala won't see. Nala doesn't look down."

Anais pulled her knees tighter. Aharah sat beside her, and for a moment neither of them spoke. Below, the City of Maya slept. The sandstone spires of the ministerial quarter caught the moonlight and held it — pale gold, almost white, tapering to points so fine they looked like they might puncture the sky. Between them, the cypress groves stood in dark ranks, their roots drinking from the underground springs that fed the city. Somewhere a fountain sang its idiot song: water over stone, water over stone, the same phrase repeated since before their grandmother's grandmother was born.

And beyond it all, at the city's edge, the Maya shimmered.

The barrier. The veil. The lie that kept them safe. From where Anais sat, it looked like heat-haze — the air rippling, distorting — but she knew what it did. Anyone approaching from the desert would see ruins. Collapsed walls, dead wells, sand reclaiming stone. A city long abandoned. Walk into the shimmer and you would find yourself turning around, convinced there was nothing here, your memories rewriting themselves with each step. Only those born within the barrier, or those carrying the blood-key in their marrow, could pass through without forgetting.

Maya the Illusion. Maya the Shield. Maya the Cage.

"I had the dream again," Anais said.

"Which one?"

"The blue planet."

Aharah pulled a stone chip from her palm and flicked it over the parapet. It fell without sound. "You always dream about the blue planet."

"Because it's always there."

"So is the roof. You don't dream about the roof."

"The people on the blue planet—" Anais stopped. She wasn't sure how to say it. They lived in boxes. They ate food that came in boxes. They stared into boxes that glowed. Everything was boxes. And they spoke to each other through the boxes but never touched, never actually heard. They were drowning in their own noise and they thought the noise was living. "They were sad," she finished, inadequately.

Aharah was quiet for a moment. Then: "Come on. Before the sentries cycle."

They climbed down together — Aharah first, finding handholds in the dark by touch, Anais following with her fingers in the grooves Aharah's fingers left. They dropped into the interior courtyard and padded barefoot through the colonnade, past the automaton that stood perpetual guard at the eastern door. It was one of the old ones — military-grade, its face a bronze mask locked in an expression of serene alertness. It had not moved in forty years. Its power source still hummed faintly, but whatever instructions it followed had been overwritten by time, or by some update no one living knew how to reverse. Now it stood. It guarded. It guarded nothing.

They slipped back into the sisters' quarters and Anais crawled into her bed. Aharah stood in the doorway.

"Stop dreaming about dead planets," Aharah said.

"I don't choose the dreams."

"Then tell them to stop choosing you."

She was gone. Anais lay in the dark and listened to the fountain and tried not to think about blue.

---

Morning in the Jian household began with Auntie.

Auntie was an automaton of a kind no longer manufactured — domestic-class, fourth generation, with a bronzen face that centuries of oxidation had turned the green of old copper. She wore a kaftan embroidered with pomegranates over a shift of yellowed linen, and she had wrapped her cranial casing in a scarf of Hazzan red, and she moved through the kitchen with the particular authority of a being that had been performing the same tasks for longer than anyone could remember. Her joints clicked when she bent. Her voice, when she spoke, came from somewhere in her chest — a warm contralto with a faint burr, as if the speaker mechanism had worn smooth in places.

"Sit," Auntie said.

The three sisters sat.

The breakfast table was stone. The food was simple: flatbread, white cheese, olives in oil, a bowl of dried figs. A pot of mint tea steamed between them. Nala sat at the head — she always sat at the head now, had claimed it the day she turned fifteen, and no one had challenged her. Her hair was pulled back in the military style, tight against her skull, revealing the architecture of her face: high cheekbones, a jaw that could have been drawn with a straightedge, eyes the color of wet obsidian. She was tall. She had always been tall. She wore height like a weapon.

Aharah sat at Nala's left, cross-legged, her knees freshly bandaged under the table. She ate quickly, tearing bread with her fingers, her eyes on nothing.

Anais sat at the end, her feet not touching the floor. She held a fig in both hands and did not eat it.

"You were on the roof again," Nala said. She did not look up from her plate.

Anais went still. Aharah kept eating.

"The sentries noted movement on the western parapet. Third bell." Nala's voice was flat. Informational. The voice of someone delivering a military report to herself. "I told them it was a bird."

"Thank you," Anais said.

"I didn't do it for you. I did it because the alternative is explaining to the Hazzan why his granddaughters are scaling walls at night like ferals, and I don't have the patience." Now she looked up. At Aharah. "You'll break your neck."

"I haven't yet," Aharah said, through bread.

"That's not the argument you think it is."

"Nala." Auntie placed a cup of tea before the eldest with a click of ceramic on stone. "The child trains. Let her train."

"She's twelve. She shouldn't need to train for anything."

"I trained at twelve," Auntie said mildly. Then, catching herself: "Or perhaps I was assembled at twelve. Memory is imprecise at my age."

Aharah snorted. Nala did not.

The morning light came through the eastern windows in bars of gold, and where it struck Aharah's skin, the sunskin drank it. The pigment responded to sunlight like a living thing — a slow, golden pulse that started at her shoulders and moved down her arms. All three sisters carried it, but Aharah's was the strongest. In direct sun, she blazed. In shadow, she dimmed. She was, Anais thought, a person who could not hide.

"Today," Nala said, and paused. She set down her tea. Her fingers on the cup were steady. Her fingers were always steady. "Today the Temple makes its announcement."

The table went quiet. Even Auntie stopped moving.

"You were chosen," Aharah said. Not a question.

"I was chosen." Nala met her eyes. "The Lyonessa ordeal. Three days from now."

Anais looked between her sisters. The Lyonessa ordeal. She knew what it was — every child in Maya knew. The Temple's gift to the worthy. The gene-treatment that rewrote the body's architecture: bone density doubled, healing accelerated, endurance pushed beyond human limits. The Lyonessa gene turned women into something more. Something older. The warrior-priestesses who had guarded the Hazzan since the First Landing carried it, and their daughters, and their daughters' daughters — though fewer each generation, as the knowledge of how to administer the treatment eroded.

It was an honor. It was the highest honor the Temple could bestow.

It was also, in roughly one case in five, fatal.

"Nala," Aharah said, and her voice had changed. Lower. Careful. The voice she used when she was thinking hard about something she didn't want to think about.

"Don't."

"Have you told the physician—"

"There is nothing to tell."

Anais looked at Aharah. Something passed between her two older sisters that she couldn't read — a current, subterranean, running beneath the words. Aharah's jaw tightened. Nala returned to her tea.

"I am honored by the Temple's selection," Nala said formally. "I will undergo the ordeal with gratitude. This conversation is finished."

She stood. She left. Her footsteps faded down the colonnade — measured, even, a metronome of composure.

Aharah did not move. She sat with her bread in her hand and stared at the place where Nala had been, and Anais watched her sister's face do something she had never seen it do before. Aharah looked afraid.

"What's wrong with her?" Anais asked.

Aharah blinked. The fear vanished — packed down, walled off, replaced by the usual stubborn blankness. "Nothing."

"You're lying."

"Eat your fig, Anais."

---

Anais ate her fig. She ate two more. Then she followed Aharah.

This was their arrangement, unspoken and unbreakable: Aharah moved and Anais followed, and Aharah pretended not to notice, and Anais pretended not to be there, and they both pretended the pretending wasn't a form of love.

Aharah walked through the city. Anais tracked her from two streets back, keeping to the shadows of the cypress colonnades, weaving between the morning foot traffic: merchants hauling bolts of dyed cloth on their backs, children driving a pair of sand-hares toward the market, a cluster of priests in their white shifts moving in formation toward the Temple.

And the automatons. They were everywhere in Maya, if you knew where to look. Some performed tasks that still made sense — the sweeping ones, the water-carriers, the ones that maintained the fountain mechanisms with their delicate bronze fingers. Others had lost their purpose entirely. A military automaton stood in the spice market's center, its caster arm raised, its targeting array tracking pigeons with a faint whirr. It had been doing this for decades. Children hung garlands from its wrist.

Auntie said there had been a time when the automatons were built. When people understood the mechanisms, could repair the joints, replace the power cells, update the behavioral protocols. That time was gone. Now the automatons were tended like elderly relatives — wiped down, dressed up, spoken to gently when they malfunctioned. When one stopped working, it was mourned. Its body was placed in the Corridor of the Still, where hundreds of them stood in rows, bronzen faces turned green, a cemetery of machines.

The casters were the same. Every Lyonessa carried one — a weapon that fit the forearm like a second skin and threw concentrated light in a beam that could cut stone. They were beautiful. They were irreplaceable. And no one living in Maya could build a new one. The ones they had were maintained by automatons who followed repair protocols they could not explain, using materials they could not name, in workshops where the tools operated themselves and the Lyonessas watched with the polite incomprehension of children watching rain.

Aharah stopped at the training ground behind the military quarter. Anais crouched behind a column and watched.

Her sister stood at the edge of the yard where the young soldiers drilled. She did not enter. She watched. Her eyes tracked the movements of the older girls — the footwork, the blade positions, the way they turned a parry into a strike with a shift of the hip. Aharah's hands moved at her sides, mirroring what she saw. Learning by echo.

Then her gaze shifted. Nala.

Nala was in the yard. She moved through the sparring line with the fluid economy of someone who had been training since she could walk. She was the best in her cohort and everyone knew it. Her blade work was clean, decisive, almost cold — technique distilled to its essence, every motion necessary, nothing wasted. She fought the way she spoke: with precision that felt like restraint.

But Aharah wasn't watching Nala fight. She was watching Nala's hands.

Between bouts, when Nala sheathed her practice blade and reached for the water skin, her hands trembled. A fine, constant vibration, as if something inside her were humming at a frequency the rest of her body couldn't absorb. She hid it well — the water skin to the lips, the quick wipe, the hands tucked behind her back or wrapped around the blade's grip. But Aharah saw.

The hala.

Anais did not know this word yet. She would learn it later — the poison that lived in bone marrow, dormant for years, then waking like a season. It was rare. It was hereditary. It came down through Nala's mother's line: Hagar, the servant woman who had borne the Hazzan's eldest daughter and died before her child could walk. Hagar's gift to her daughter: beauty, height, a jaw like a straightedge, and poison sleeping in the marrow, waiting for a trigger.

The Lyonessa ordeal was the trigger. The gene-treatment would find the hala and the hala would find the treatment and the two would tear Nala apart from the inside. Aharah knew this. She had overheard the physician telling Auntie six months ago, in a conversation she was not meant to hear, in a room she was not meant to be in, on a night she was not meant to be awake.

She stood at the edge of the training yard and watched her sister's hands shake and said nothing.

---

That night, Anais dreamed of fire.

Not the blue planet this time. A city — this city, her city — wreathed in flame. The Maya barrier flickering, failing, the shimmer going dark in patches like a dying eye. Sand pouring through the gaps. Figures in the streets she didn't recognize, carrying weapons she'd never seen. And above it all, a sound — low, sustained, the sound a world makes when it gives up pretending to be solid.

She woke with the scream in her throat and the taste of ash on her tongue. The room was dark. The fountain sang. The twin moons hung where they always hung.

She went to Aharah's bed. It was empty.

She went to the roof. Aharah was there, sitting on the parapet with her legs dangling over the forty-cubit drop, her sunskin dim in the moonlight, her face turned toward the Temple spire that rose above the city like a needle threaded with starlight.

Anais sat beside her. She did not dangle her legs. She was eight.

"I dreamed the city was burning," she said.

"I know," Aharah said. She did not look away from the Temple. "Go back to bed, Anais."

"What are you going to do?"

"I don't know yet."

But she did know. Anais could hear it in her voice — the flat calm that preceded action, the stillness that was not stillness but the coiling of a spring. Aharah had made a decision. She just hadn't told it to herself yet.

Anais looked at the Temple. It rose in tiers of pale stone, each tier narrower than the last, the whole structure tapering to a point where a gem caught the moonlight and split it into colors that had no name in the Shakyan tongue. The ordeal chamber was inside. Three days from now, Nala would enter it. The Temple would give her the Lyonessa gene or it would kill her. One in five. Those were the odds for a healthy candidate.

For Nala, the odds were worse.

"I'll be here when you get back," Anais said.

Aharah looked at her then. Really looked, the way she rarely did — not as the little sister to be managed, not as the shadow that followed, but as something else. Something that saw.

"You're strange, Anais."

"I know."

"Go to bed."

Anais went to bed. She lay in the dark and listened to the fountain and the wind in the cypress and the faint, faint hum of the automaton at the eastern door, and she waited for whatever was going to happen to happen.

The city of Maya slept on, sealed behind its beautiful lie.
