# Chapter 9: Mars, Mother

---

The first joke I ever told my mother went like this:

"Mama, why do birds fly south?"

She looked up from the stove. Dinner was rice again, with the brown edges where the pot had scorched. The kitchen window was shut and sealed with tape because the air outside was bad that week. The air was always bad, but some weeks it was worse. That week you could see it — a yellow haze that settled over the rooftops like something breathing.

"I don't know, baby. Why?"

"Because it's too far to walk!"

She laughed. Not the polite kind. The kind that comes from somewhere deep and takes your whole body with it — shoulders, stomach, the heels of your hands pressed against your knees. She laughed until she cried, and I stood there in the kitchen watching the tears roll down, proud as a king. I had done that. I had made that happen.

That's when I learned: if you can make someone laugh until they cry, you've touched them somewhere they keep locked.

She wiped her face with the back of her hand. "That's a good one," she said. "Remember that one. If you can make them laugh, they'll let you in."

I didn't know what she meant. I was five.

---

We lived on the third floor of a building with no elevator. The hallway smelled like cooking oil and concrete dust and the chemical tang of the air filters that Mrs. Park ran twenty-four hours a day next door. You could hear them through the wall — that low, constant whirr that meant someone was trying to breathe.

My mother rode a bicycle. It was blue once; now it was the color of nothing — paint stripped by weather, chain oiled with whatever she could find, handlebars wrapped in electrical tape where the rubber grips had worn through. She rode it six kilometers each way to her job at the packing plant. Six kilometers through air that required a mask.

The mask was the expensive thing in our house. Not the bicycle. Not the stove. The mask. A Taiyo 9 — secondhand, from a man at the night market who claimed it was military surplus. The filters were rated for two hundred hours, and she changed them exactly on time, marking the hours in pencil on the kitchen wall. A column of small, careful lines. When she breathed through it, the exhalation fogged the visor, and for a moment her face would disappear. Then the defogger kicked in — a small click, like a tongue against the roof of a mouth — and she came back.

Every morning I watched her come back.

She left before dawn. I heard the click of the mask, the creak of the bicycle on the landing, and then the sound of her carrying it down three flights because the stairwell was too narrow to ride. At the bottom, the building door. Then nothing. Silence, and the hum of Mrs. Park's filters.

I waited on the mattress for the grey light to fill the window. Then I got up and poured cereal from the box on the counter and ate it with powdered milk mixed in water. The spoon was too big for my hand. I used it anyway.

---

Mars was a word before it was a place.

I heard it on the radio — the small one on the kitchen shelf, the one with the cracked antenna my mother kept tuned to the news station because she said ignorance was the most expensive thing in the world. Mars Colony. Mars Transit Authority. Mars Permanent Residency Programme. The words came through the speaker in the anchor's clean, careful voice, and I imagined a red place, like the desert in the picture book my mother kept on the shelf above the mattress. I didn't know yet that deserts could be cold.

People were going. That was the thing. People were leaving Earth, and some of them weren't coming back, and nobody on the radio sounded sad about it. They sounded the way you sound when you talk about someone else's good luck — bright and distant.

My mother listened with her hands in the dishwater. She didn't say anything. But she listened the way you listen to a door opening in another room.

---

The consulate was downtown, in a building that used to be a bank. You could tell because the lobby had marble floors and high ceilings and the kind of hush that money leaves behind even after the money is gone. Now there were plastic chairs bolted to the floor in rows, and a number dispenser on the wall, and a counter with three windows behind thick glass.

We took the bus. My mother wore her good coat — the one she kept in a plastic bag at the back of the closet, the one that still had both buttons. She'd pressed my shirt with the iron the night before, heating it on the stove because the cord was broken. The collar sat stiff against my neck. I could feel where the heat had left a faint scorch mark on the inside, warm against my skin like a small secret.

We took a number. Forty-seven. The display behind the counter said twelve.

We sat.

My mother held a folder on her lap. Manila, thick, the edges soft from handling. Inside were papers I'd seen her filling out at the kitchen table after she thought I was asleep — forms, certificates, photocopies of photocopies. Her handwriting was small and precise, each letter formed with the care of someone who knows that a single mistake can close a door.

I swung my legs. The chair was too tall for my feet to reach the floor. My mother put her hand on my knee — not to stop me, just to touch me. I stopped anyway.

We waited two hours. The display changed slowly, like something unwinding. Other families sat around us, quiet in the way people are quiet when they are asking for something they need. A woman in the row ahead held a sleeping baby against her shoulder. A man near the window read the same page of a newspaper over and over. Everyone had folders.

They called our number.

---

The officer was thin. He sat behind the counter on a high stool, which made him taller than he was, and his screen threw blue light onto the underside of his chin, giving his face a drowned look. His badge said TRANSIT LIAISON, MARS RESIDENCY PROGRAMME. He did not look up when we sat down.

"Names," he said.

My mother gave them.

"Purpose of application."

"Permanent residency transfer." Her voice was level. She'd practiced this. I knew because I'd heard her saying it into the bathroom mirror the night before, over and over, the way you rehearse a prayer.

He typed. The keys were loud in the marble room.

"Employment status."

"Packing plant. Industrial sector nine. Four years."

"Savings."

She told him. It was a small number. I knew it was small because of the way she said it — not ashamed, but braced, the way you brace for a step you know will be uneven.

He looked at the number on his screen. He didn't say anything about it. He didn't have to. His silence said it.

"Education."

"Secondary complete. Trade certification, electrical maintenance."

"Languages."

"Two. English. Korean."

He typed all of this without expression. Then he turned to me. His eyes were the pale, watery kind — the kind that looked like they'd been left out in the rain.

"And the child."

"My son."

"Age."

"Six."

"Does the child understand why you're here?"

My mother's hand found mine under the counter. Her fingers closed around my fingers, warm, certain.

"He understands," she said.

The officer leaned forward. He looked at me the way you look at something you're measuring. "Do you know why your mother wants to go to Mars, son?"

I looked at him. I looked at my mother. Her eyes were on me, and in them was something I wouldn't understand for twenty years — a terror so vast and so controlled it looked like calm.

I told him a joke.

"Why do birds fly south?"

He blinked. "Excuse me?"

"Why do birds fly south?"

"I don't —"

"Because it's too far to walk."

He stared at me. He did not laugh. His mouth did not move. The blue light from his screen pooled in the hollows of his face, and he looked at me the way you look at a stain on a surface you're responsible for keeping clean.

My mother squeezed my hand. Once. Hard. I still feel it.

"The application is incomplete," the officer said. He turned back to my mother. "You're missing the Section 14 health clearance, the biometric update is expired, and your financial threshold doesn't meet the minimum for the transit class you've applied under." He said these words the way you read a list. Bread. Milk. Eggs. Denial. "You can resubmit in ninety days."

"We've submitted twice before," my mother said. Quiet. Not arguing. Stating.

"Then you'll be familiar with the process."

"The biometric update requires a clinic appointment. The waitlist is —"

"Ma'am." He said it the way you close a lid. "The requirements are the requirements. Ninety days."

He stamped something. A red mark on the folder. He slid it back through the slot in the glass. My mother took it. She stood. She took my hand.

We walked across the marble floor toward the glass doors. I could hear our shoes. Mine made small sounds. Hers made no sound at all.

---

Outside, the air was yellow. Not the clear yellow of afternoon sun but the thick, chemical yellow that meant the district ventilation filters were overdue for replacement. Three weeks overdue, according to the radio. My mother put on her mask. She helped me with mine — the child's model, the one she'd paid for by not eating lunch for a month. I know this because Mrs. Park told me, years later. Your mother ate nothing at work. Nothing. For a month. So you could breathe.

The bicycle was chained to a post near the bus stop. She unlocked it. She swung her leg over the seat.

Then she stopped.

She sat there. Both feet on the ground, both hands on the handlebars, the mask fogging and clearing, fogging and clearing. She didn't pedal. She didn't speak. She sat on the bicycle the way you sit when the road ahead and the road behind look exactly the same and neither one goes anywhere you need to be.

I stood beside her. I didn't say anything. I was six, and I didn't have words for what I was seeing. But I felt it — the way you feel weather change before the sky does. Something in her had gone quiet. The radio between stations.

Then she reached down and lifted me onto the rack behind the seat. She did this every day. Her hands under my arms, the small hoist, my legs settling on either side of the wheel. I put my arms around her waist. I could feel her ribs through the good coat. I could feel her breathing.

She started pedaling.

We rode through the yellow air. Past the consulate with its marble and its plastic chairs. Past the bus stop where a woman held a mask to her baby's face with one hand. Past the blocks of apartment buildings with their sealed windows and their filter hum. Past the billboard for the Mars Transit Authority — a family in white, standing on red soil, the sky behind them thin and pink and clean. The mother in the billboard had her arm around her son. She was smiling.

My mother pedaled. The mask fogged. I held her waist.

Six kilometers. I counted the blocks when I could. When I couldn't, I counted her breaths — the steady pull and release through the filter, the click of the defogger, the fog, the clearing, the fog again.

We didn't speak. Speaking wasted filter life.

But once — near the end, when our building appeared between the grey towers like a tooth in a jaw — I pressed my face against her back and said it. Quiet. Into the fabric of her coat, where only she could hear.

"Mars, mother."

She didn't answer. She pedaled. But her hand left the handlebar for a moment — just a moment — and covered mine where it gripped her waist.

---

She died when I was nineteen. The lungs. The filters. The air that was always bad and some weeks was worse.

I found the folder. It was in the closet, in the plastic bag behind the good coat. I took it to the kitchen table — her table, the one where she worked after she thought I was asleep — and I opened it.

The application was for one.

Not two. One. One child, unaccompanied minor, transit class C, Mars Permanent Residency Programme. The Section 14 health clearance was for one body. The biometric scan was for one face. The financial threshold — the small number that made the officer's silence loud — was calculated for one passage.

She was never trying to get to Mars.

She was trying to get me there.

Every form in that folder had my name on it. Every pencil line on the kitchen wall — the hours counted on the mask filter, marked and remarked — was time she was saving so she could save enough to send me. Every lunch she didn't eat. Every six-kilometer ride through the yellow air on a bicycle the color of nothing with handlebars wrapped in electrical tape. Every night at the kitchen table, filling in small boxes with her small, precise handwriting, each letter formed with the care of someone who knows that a single mistake can close a door.

The door was for me. It was only ever for me.

I sat at the table for a long time. The kitchen was quiet. Mrs. Park's filters hummed through the wall. Outside, the air was bad.

I thought about birds. Why they fly south. Because it's too far to walk, I'd told her, and she'd laughed until she'd cried, and I was five and proud as a king because I had made that happen.

I think about it now — her laughing, her crying — and I understand they were the same thing. She was laughing because her son had told a joke. She was crying because she was already planning to give him wings and stay on the ground.

Some jokes aren't funny. They're just true.

And the truest thing I know is this: my mother rode a bicycle through poisoned air every day for four years so that I might someday breathe.
