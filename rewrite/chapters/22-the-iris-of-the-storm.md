# Chapter 22: The Iris of the Storm

The Astromancery stood at the highest point of the city, above the Temple, above the ministerial spires, above even the cypress line where the evening wind turned cold. It was an observatory in the old sense — a stone drum forty cubits across, its walls seamless, its floor tiled in black glass that reflected the sky when the canopy opened. And the canopy did open. It was the building's single mechanical act: a ring of stone petals folding outward like the iris of a vast eye, revealing the dark above.

Anais climbed the spiral staircase barefoot. The stone was cool under her feet, slick with condensation that seeped from the walls in a slow mineral weep. She had been climbing this staircase for three months. Each night the same ascent, each night the same hundred and forty-four steps — she had counted them once and then stopped counting, because counting was a way of placing yourself in time, and what happened at the top of the staircase existed outside of time, or beside it, or beneath it, in a place where the word *when* lost its purchase.

The tank waited at the center of the observatory floor.

It was glass — or something like glass, something older, something that had been poured rather than blown, its walls thick and faintly green, the color of deep water seen from above. It stood waist-high on an adult, which meant chest-high on Anais, and it was filled with a fluid that was not water. Aerated saline, the Oracle called it. Oxygenated. Warmed to blood temperature. When Anais lowered herself into it, the fluid accepted her weight and held her — not floating, not sinking, but suspended, the way a thought is suspended between intention and speech.

She undressed. Folded her shift on the stone bench beside the tank. The night air raised the fine hairs along her arms and the sunskin responded — a faint pulse, involuntary, the pigment trying to metabolize starlight the way it metabolized sun. It could not. The energy was too thin. But the cells tried, and the trying produced a glow that lit her body from within: pale gold, dim as a candle seen through cloth.

She climbed the step-stool. She lowered herself in.

The fluid closed over her calves, her thighs, her hips. Warm. Dense. It held her the way held breath holds sound — from all directions, uniformly, without preference. She sank to her shoulders. Carbon dioxide rose from vents in the tank's floor in columns of fine bubbles that caught the starlight and carried it upward, each bubble a lens, a tiny convex world reflecting the observatory ceiling and the dark beyond it. They rose past her body like jellyfish — slow, deliberate, luminous — and burst at the surface in whispers.

She tipped her head back. Above, through the open canopy, the sky.

---

The monks filed in at the second bell.

There were nine of them — the full complement of the Astromancery, robed in indigo so dark it read as black, their heads shaved, their feet bare on the glass floor. They took their positions around the tank in a circle, equidistant, their backs to Anais, their gazes fixed on the walls. On the floor. On anything that was not above.

This was the rule: the monks did not look up.

The Astromancery existed to observe the sky, and the monks who served it were forbidden from doing so. This was not cruelty. It was engineering. The astromantic gift — the ability to read the light that fell from stars and decode in it the signature of events that had not yet occurred — required a single receiver. One set of eyes, one nervous system, one consciousness tuned to the frequency. If others looked, the signal scattered. The monks kept their gazes earthward so that Anais could look heavenward, and the arrangement was as old as the observatory itself, and no one questioned it, because in Shakya the things that worked were the things you did not question.

Anais looked up.

The sky was not the sky she knew.

The twin suns had set hours ago, but their light was still present — not as illumination but as architecture. The planets of the system — five gas giants, two ice worlds, a handful of rocky bodies too small to name — had aligned along a single orbital plane, and the alignment created a gravitational corridor through which the suns' residual light was bent and refocused, a lensing effect that transformed the upper atmosphere into a structure of concentric rings. Light folded over light. Ring inside ring inside ring, each one a different color — amber, violet, a green so pale it was almost white — spiraling inward toward a center point directly overhead.

The Iris of the Storm.

It looked like an eye. A vast, unblinking eye made of refracted starlight and planetary gravity, gazing down through the open canopy of the Astromancery with the calm attention of something that had been waiting to be seen. The rings pulsed — not visibly, not in any way a normal eye could detect, but Anais felt the pulse in her sternum, in her wrists, in the fluid that held her body. Each ring carried information. Each color was a frequency. Each frequency was a moment in time — not the present, which was useless, but the far future, where light had traveled so long that it arrived bearing the fingerprints of events that would not occur for centuries.

Anais read the light.

She did not read it the way you read a page. She received it. The light entered her eyes and bypassed the visual cortex entirely — she saw no images, no scenes, no prophetic tableaux of future battles or future births. What she received was simpler and more devastating: certainty. A knowledge that settled into her body like temperature, like gravity, like the weight of the fluid around her. She knew things. She knew them the way she knew her own pulse.

The fishfolk were spawning.

She knew this the way she knew the bubbles were rising — as a condition of the present informed by the shape of what was coming. Millions of them, in the deep trenches beyond the continental shelf, their cold bodies pressing together in the lightless water, producing clutches of translucent eggs that adhered to the volcanic vents and grew fast, so fast, fed by the mineral-rich water and the geothermal heat that winter brought to the ocean floor. They were cold-blooded. They felt no pain. They amassed in numbers that made strategy irrelevant — they did not need tactics because they did not need to survive individually. The clutch survived. The species survived. The individual was a unit of expenditure, no more grieved than a spent cartridge.

They wanted the land.

Not to live on it — their physiology could not sustain terrestrial existence for more than a few days. They wanted to flood it. To raise the sea level by meters, by tens of meters, to terraform the coastal plains into the brackish marshland that their larvae required for the second stage of development. They had done this before. The geological record held the evidence: flood lines carved into the cliff faces north of Shakya, salt deposits in the highland soil, the fossilized remains of structures that had once stood at sea level and now stood underwater, their builders long forgotten.

The Iris pulsed. Anais received.

Winter. They would come in winter, when the Vermillion Desert became impassable — when the vyvyan beetles bloomed and the sulfur clouds rolled south and the toxic belt between Shakya and the Hazzan Empire sealed shut like a wound closing. Cut off. No reinforcements. No retreat. Shakya alone against the tide.

The light burned.

Not the fluid, not the air — the light itself, entering her eyes, carrying information at a density her optic nerves were not built to sustain. She felt it as heat, first in the eyes themselves, then behind them, spreading along the channels where sight met cognition. The monks had warned her. The Oracle had warned her. Astromancy was not observation. It was transaction. You gave your eyes to the future and the future gave you knowledge, and the exchange rate was not negotiable. Each session dimmed the world a little more. Each reading burned another layer of retinal tissue. The astromancers of old had all gone blind. Every one. It was not a risk. It was the fee.

Anais kept looking.

The Iris spiraled. The rings tightened. At the center — the pupil, the point where all the light converged — she saw something that was not light at all but absence, a darkness so complete it had texture, and in that darkness she felt the shape of a question she could not yet articulate, a question about the nature of what she was becoming, about the boundary between seeing and being seen, about the eye above and the eye below and whether they were the same eye looking at itself across the curvature of time.

Then the canopy closed. The stone petals folded inward. The Iris vanished behind rock and the observatory was dark and the fluid was warm and the bubbles still rose and Anais floated in the tank with her eyes open and tears running down her temples into the saline, and the tears were not grief but overflow — the body's only response to a volume of knowing it could not contain.

---

The Oracle was waiting in the chamber below.

She sat in the stone chair that had been built for her, or that she had been built into — the distinction had blurred over the centuries until no one living knew where the chair ended and the Oracle began. Cables ran from the base of her skull into channels carved in the floor, threading downward through the bedrock to the root systems of the Astromancery's intelligence. Her skin was dark and papery, drawn tight across a skull that seemed too large for the body beneath it. Her eyes were open. They were always open. They had not closed in living memory.

"The fishfolk are spawning," Anais said.

"Yes." The Oracle's voice came from her mouth and from somewhere else simultaneously — a harmonic, as if the walls were speaking the same word a half-beat behind her. "The tidal patterns confirm it. The water temperature in the northern trenches has risen two degrees. The spawning will peak in forty days."

"Winter."

"Winter. When we are cut off from the Haz by the toxic desert. When the sulfur belt closes and the overland route dies. When we are alone." The Oracle's head did not move. Her eyes, pale and depthless as quartz, tracked Anais without turning. "This is not new. This has happened before."

"How many times?"

"Seven, in recorded memory. Eleven, in the geological record. The fishfolk flood the coast. The brackish water destroys the lowland crops. The larvae colonize the estuaries. The process takes two seasons. By the time the desert reopens, the coastline has moved a mile inland."

Anais stood before the Oracle in her damp shift, her hair plastered to her neck, her eyes red from the light. She was eleven years old. She felt older than the stone.

Aharah entered from the western corridor. She moved the way she always moved — low, fast, a body trained to occupy as little space as possible while covering as much ground as it could. The gem-key at her wrist pulsed as she crossed the threshold, the blue-white light flaring briefly in the dark of the corridor, an announcement she could not suppress. She wore her desert wrappings still, the sand-colored cloth stained dark at the hems, and she carried herself with the particular tension of someone who had spent the day inspecting things that were not ready.

"We need reinforcements," Aharah said. She did not greet the Oracle. She did not sit. "The coastal batteries are half-staffed. The auto-turrets on the northern headland haven't been serviced in a year. The fishfolk hit the seawall last time with a surge that cracked the foundation at three points, and we patched those points with mortar and prayer and neither is load-bearing."

"The Haz has soldiers," Aharah continued. "They have mech-pods and bolt rifles and the kind of artillery that makes fishfolk irrelevant. One message to the border garrison—"

"No."

The word fell from the Oracle's mouth like a stone into still water.

"They are our rivals."

Aharah's jaw tightened. The sunskin along her forearms pulsed once — a flare of gold, involuntary, the body's response to a surge of feeling the mind had not authorized. She stepped closer. The cables on the floor hummed.

"But they're not our enemies."

The Oracle regarded her. The pale eyes, the motionless head, the body that was half-architecture and half-flesh. "The Dhukkha Khan does not send soldiers as gifts. He sends them as investments. Every garrison he places on Shakyan soil becomes a root. Every root becomes a claim. We have survived seven floods without the Haz. We will survive the eighth."

"At what cost?"

"The cost we can afford." The Oracle's voice dropped to a register that was almost subsonic, a vibration Anais felt in her teeth. "The cost we control. Shakyan blood on Shakyan stone. Not Hazzan soldiers in our corridors, learning our defenses, mapping our tunnels, reporting to a king who has spent his reign dismantling every power on this continent that is not his own."

The silence that followed was not empty. It was the silence of two forces pressing against each other, neither yielding, the pressure building in the stone and the cables and the still air of the chamber.

"Then we fight alone," Aharah said. It was not a question.

"We fight as Shakya has always fought. With what we have."

Aharah turned. She passed Anais without speaking, her footsteps sharp on the stone, the gem-key dimming as she moved away from the Oracle's field. At the corridor's mouth she stopped. She did not look back.

"When the seawall breaks," she said, "I want you to remember this conversation."

She was gone. The corridor swallowed her footsteps. The cables hummed and stilled.

Anais looked at the Oracle. The Oracle looked at the ceiling, or through it, at something only she could see.

"She is not wrong," Anais said quietly.

"She is not wrong about the wall," the Oracle replied. "She is wrong about the cost."

---

The beach at night was a study in absence.

Black sky. Black sea. The only distinction between them was the foam — white lines that appeared and disappeared at the waterline, brief as breath on glass, tracing the shape of waves that were otherwise invisible. The twin moons had set. The orabaun had not yet risen. The dark was total and Aharah walked through it with her sandals in one hand, barefoot, feeling the sand.

She tested it as she walked. Weight forward on the ball of the foot, reading the density. Here: packed wet, good footing, a surface you could fight on. Ten paces seaward: soft, saturated, a surface that would swallow an ankle and hold it. She mapped it with her body. She had been doing this for hours, walking the full length of the northern beach from the headland cliffs to the river mouth, building a tactile inventory of the terrain the way Dzuvag had taught her to map the desert: not with instruments but with skin.

The auto-drones moved offshore. She could not see them — they were dark, low-profile, running silent — but she could feel the displacement of their passage in the water, a series of faint pressure changes that registered against her calves when she waded to her knees. They were mapping the seafloor. Bathymetric arrays, sonar pings so far below human hearing that only the fishfolk could detect them, and by the time they did it would be too late to alter whatever was down there. The drones had been running for a week. The data they returned was not encouraging. The trenches were deeper than the old charts showed. The spawning grounds were closer to shore.

She reached the firepoint at the beach's midpoint. A low bunker of poured stone, half-buried in the dune line, its aperture facing the sea. A figure sat on the bunker's roof with its legs dangling, the orange point of a cigarette tracing small circles in the dark.

The soldier heard her approach and scrambled upright. Young — sixteen, perhaps seventeen, the age when the body has finished growing but the face has not yet learned what to do with the result. He wore coastal militia blues, ill-fitting, the sleeves rolled to the elbow. His hands shook. The cigarette shook with them, shedding ash that the wind took.

"Stand easy," Aharah said.

He stood easier but not easy. She climbed the bunker's seaward face and sat beside him. The concrete was still warm from the day's sun. She could smell the tobacco — cheap, harsh, the kind the militia commissary issued because no one had complained loudly enough to change it.

The wind gusted. The cigarette's ember flared and died. The soldier cupped his hands around it, trying to relight. His fingers fumbled the igniter — once, twice, the spark catching nothing, the wind eating each small flame before it could take hold.

Aharah reached over. She cupped her hands around his, creating a windbreak. Their fingers overlapped. Her sunskin was warm. His hands were cold.

The flame caught. The tobacco glowed. The soldier inhaled and the relief on his face was disproportionate — the relief of a small victory in a night full of large fears.

"Thanks," he said.

"What's your name?"

"Corporal Shenn. Second coastal." He smoked quickly, hungrily. "You're the Lyonessa."

"Aharah."

"I know." He looked at the sea. The dark, featureless expanse of it. "They say the fishfolk can see in the dark. That they navigate by electrical fields. That they can feel a heartbeat through fifty meters of water."

"Some of that is true."

"Which parts?"

"The parts that matter."

Shenn smoked. The wind blew. The waves drew their white lines on the black sand and erased them.

"I've never fought anything," he said. He said it the way you state a medical fact — flatly, without self-pity, as a condition to be acknowledged. "I trained on the dummies and I scored well on the range but I've never — nothing has ever tried to kill me. And they say the fishfolk don't stop. That you can cut them and they keep coming. That they don't feel it."

"They don't feel pain the way we do."

"How do they feel it?"

"As information. The wound tells them where you are. It doesn't tell them to stop."

Shenn was quiet for a long time. The cigarette burned down to his fingers and he dropped it and did not light another. His eyes stayed on the water.

"I'm scared," he said.

Aharah looked at the sea. The auto-drones pulsed somewhere beneath the surface, mapping the terrain of the coming war. The foam drew its lines.

"Good," she said. "Fear is the body's way of paying attention. When it stops, you've stopped listening."

She sat with him on the bunker until the orabaun rose — the chain of ancient satellites crossing the sky in their slow, pearl-bright procession, casting just enough light to separate the sea from the sky. Then she dropped to the sand and walked on, barefoot, mapping the beach with her body, leaving the soldier on the bunker with his fear and his empty hands and the dark water beyond.

---

The garden behind the Astromancery was small and walled and lit by bioluminescent moss that grew in the mortar between the stones. The light it cast was green and shadowless, the light of a place that existed outside the jurisdiction of the suns. Fig trees grew against the southern wall. A stone bench sat beneath them.

Anais was waiting on the bench when Aharah came through the gate. She sat with her legs drawn up, her chin on her knees, her eyes — red-rimmed, dimmer than they had been a month ago, the cost of the Iris accumulating — fixed on the moss-light.

"You walked the whole beach," Anais said.

"How do you know?"

"I know things." A pause. The faintest ghost of a smile. "And you have sand between your toes."

Aharah sat beside her. The fig tree's branches formed a low canopy above them, the leaves broad and dark, their edges silver with moss-light. The garden smelled of earth and stone and the sweet rot of fallen figs.

"The Oracle won't call the Haz," Aharah said.

"I know."

"The seawall isn't strong enough."

"I know that too."

Aharah looked at her youngest sister. Eleven years old. Eyes that were slowly trading their sight for something else — a vision that reached further and cost more, a transaction Anais had entered voluntarily and could not reverse. The sunskin was faint on her, barely visible — she had always been the palest of the three, as if the pigment that blazed in Aharah and darkened in Nala had been spent before it reached the youngest.

"You should stay in the Astromancery," Aharah said. "When it comes. The walls are thick. The elevation—"

"No."

"Anais."

"I said no." Anais turned to her. The red-rimmed eyes were steady. "I sat inside the barrier when you walked into the desert. I sat inside the city when Nala rode to the frontier. I have spent my entire life sitting inside things while the people I love walk into the dark." She unfolded from the bench and stood. She was small. She had always been small. But she stood in the moss-light with her fists at her sides and her jaw set in the way that their mother — dead before any of them could remember her — had reportedly set hers, and the resemblance was not physical but structural, a thing of posture and will.

"I'm not letting you go alone again."

The words landed in the garden like stones in still water. Aharah looked at her sister. She saw the red eyes and the fading sunskin and the fists and the jaw and the eleven years that should have been enough to keep this child safe and were not, had never been, because safety was a fiction the Jian sisters had stopped believing in the night Aharah stole an ordeal and broke the family into its component parts.

She did not argue. She reached out and took Anais's hand. The fingers were cold. The sunskin barely pulsed. But the grip was strong — stronger than it should have been, as if the body had decided that what it could not illuminate it would hold.

They sat in the garden beneath the fig tree in the green moss-light, and the night moved around them the way the sea moved around the shore — constant, indifferent, carrying on its surface the shapes of things not yet arrived.
