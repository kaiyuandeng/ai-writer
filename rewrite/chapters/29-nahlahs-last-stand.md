# Chapter 29: Nahlah's Last Stand

The fishfolk came at dawn.

They rose from the Hupasa crossing in a tide of grey-green bodies — amphibious, cold-blooded, their movements synchronized in the way of organisms that did not think individually but responded to a collective signal pulsing through the brackish water like a heartbeat. Thousands in the first wave. Tens of thousands behind them, in the deep channels where the river met the sea, their forms visible as shadows beneath the surface, patient, innumerable, the reserves of a species that counted its losses in acceptable percentages and found every percentage acceptable.

Nahlah watched from the command post on the eastern bluff.

The Hupasa crossing was the last defensible position between the northern coast and the interior — a narrow defile where the river cut through basalt cliffs, the current fast and deep, the banks steep enough to channel an attack into a killing zone. Nahlah had chosen this ground. She had spent three weeks fortifying it: caster emplacements on both bluffs, auto-turrets at the waterline, a trench system connecting the gun positions in a network that allowed rapid redeployment. The batteries were charged. The fields of fire were interlocking. The positions were manned by Shakyan militia and Hazzan chimera riders — an alliance that had been unthinkable a year ago and that the marriage of Hyfe and Anais had made merely improbable.

She stood at the command post's parapet with her one arm resting on the stone. Her silver armor caught the first light of Aleph — the white sun, always first, its blade along the eastern ridge — and threw it back in cold flashes. The armor covered her right arm and shoulder, her torso, the right side of her body. The left side, where the arm had been, was bare. Not unprotected — Nahlah did not leave vulnerabilities unaddressed — but empty, the socket sealed with a plate of the same silver alloy, polished to a mirror.

In her right hand: the blade. Silver, long, weighted for a one-armed fighter. She had commissioned it after the Hupasa bridge — after the first time, two years ago, when the fishfolk had come and she had held the bridge for six hours and lost the arm in the sixth hour and cauterized the wound herself and finished the battle. The blade was designed to compensate. Its balance point was forward, allowing momentum to supplement the single arm's power. Its edge held a monomolecular line that did not dull. When she moved it, the blade caught the air differently — not slicing but displacing, the metal so fine it seemed liquid, mercury in the shape of a sword.

"General." The adjutant's voice from behind. "Battery two reports full charge. Battery three at eighty. Auto-turrets are online."

"Hold fire until they clear the waterline."

"Yes, General."

"And tell the chimera riders to stay mounted. I want them on the flanks, not in the river."

The adjutant left. Nahlah watched the crossing.

The fishfolk emerged. The first line cleared the waterline — grey-green bodies on the dark stone of the riverbank, their movements shifting from aquatic to terrestrial in the span of three steps, the gills closing, the vestigial lungs expanding, the flat black eyes adjusting to the air's light. They carried weapons — barbed pikes, woven from the same chitinous material as their scales, biological extensions of their own bodies. They did not wear armor. They did not need armor. They did not feel pain the way humans felt pain; a wound was information, a signal that reported the attacker's position and the weapon's type, and the signal triggered not retreat but orientation. They turned toward whatever cut them.

"Batteries: fire."

The casters opened. Blue-white light — the same frequency as the gem-key, the same energy the Temple had once monopolized — lanced across the crossing in parallel beams that struck the first wave at the waterline and converted matter to heat. The fishfolk did not scream. The fishfolk did not make vocal sounds of any kind. They burned in silence, their bodies coming apart without protest, and the ones behind them stepped over the burning ones and continued forward, and the ones behind those stepped over those, and the tide advanced.

The auto-turrets tracked and fired. Mechanical, precise, the targeting algorithms degraded by decades of unmaintained operation but still functional enough to identify and engage targets in the killing zone. Bolt rounds punched through the fishfolk's chitinous scales with a sound like ice cracking — sharp, clean, final. Bodies fell. Bodies were replaced. The river disgorged its grey-green multitude with the mechanical patience of a system that did not calculate odds because odds were irrelevant when the numerator was infinite.

Nahlah watched. She did not give orders — not yet. The battle's opening phase was the batteries' work, and the batteries were performing. The killing zone functioned as designed. The fishfolk died in the crossing and the crossing filled with their dead and the dead became a ramp and the living climbed the ramp, and the batteries fired, and the auto-turrets fired, and the living became dead and the dead became ramp and the living climbed the ramp.

They were going to get through. Nahlah knew this the way she knew the architecture of the defense she had built — from the inside, structurally, the knowledge of a builder who can see the load-bearing walls and the walls that are not load-bearing and the point at which the structure will fail. The batteries would run down. The auto-turrets would overheat. The killing zone would fill with dead until it was no longer a zone but a bridge, and the fishfolk would cross the bridge of their own corpses and reach the bluffs, and then it would be close work. Blade work. The kind of fighting that rewarded ferocity and punished hesitation.

She had not hesitated in twenty-three years of military service. She did not intend to start.

---

By midmorning, the batteries were at forty percent.

The crossing was choked with dead fishfolk. They lay in mounds — grey-green, still, their flat black eyes open and reflecting the twin suns' light. The smell was mineral, not organic — the fishfolk's physiology was closer to coral than to flesh, their bodies decomposing into calcium and salt rather than the rot of mammalian death. The smell was clean and terrible. It was the smell of a reef dying.

The second wave came from the north.

Nahlah had expected this. The Hupasa was the primary crossing, but the river forked two miles upstream, and the northern fork was shallow — knee-deep, fordable, the banks gentle. She had placed a secondary battery there, a squad of chimera riders, a line of stakes in the riverbed. Minimal. The northern fork was a delaying action, not a defense.

The chimera riders met the second wave at the ford. The chimeras — gene-modified warbeasts, part equine, part something older, their hides thick as armor, their temperaments tuned by generations of selective breeding to a state of controlled aggression — charged into the shallow water and the riders fired from their saddles with hand casters, the blue-white bolts punching through the fishfolk's ranks with the staccato precision of a drum. The fishfolk pressed forward. The chimeras held. The ford became a second killing zone, smaller, louder, the water turning dark with calcium runoff.

But the third wave came from the sea itself.

They surfaced a mile offshore and swam toward the coastal cliffs in a formation that was not a formation — no lines, no structure, only volume, a tide of grey-green bodies moving through the water with the mindless persistence of a natural force. The coastal batteries tracked them. The auto-drones — the last twelve, their AI barely functional — dove and fired and dove again, each pass thinning the column, each thinning replaced by the column's depth.

Nahlah stood at the command post and received reports from three fronts simultaneously. The Hupasa holding. The northern ford holding. The coastal approach advancing. She processed the data with the particular efficiency of a mind that had been at war for more than two decades and that had learned to convert chaos into geometry — to see the battle not as events but as shapes, as pressure and resistance, as the architecture of force applied to structure.

The structure was failing.

"Battery three is down," the adjutant reported. "Overheated. Cooldown cycle: four hours."

"Northern ford: chimera riders are dismounting. The warbeasts won't enter deep water."

"Coastal auto-turrets are offline. Ammunition depleted."

Nahlah's silver blade hung at her side. Her right hand rested on its grip. The grip was warm — not from the sun but from her hand, from the heat of a body preparing itself for the thing it was built to do.

"Assemble the reserve," she said. "Fifty infantry. Trench line two. I want the chimera riders to fall back from the northern ford to the bluff — they'll anchor the right flank."

"And the coastal approach?"

Nahlah looked at the sea. The fishfolk were close now — five hundred meters, four hundred. The water between the coast and the first bodies was grey-green and alive and coming.

"I'll handle the coast."

---

She descended the bluff at a run. The silver armor moved with her — it had been fitted to accommodate the missing arm, the weight redistributed, the left side's plates removed and the remaining structure rebalanced so that her body moved as a single asymmetric instrument, compensated, optimized, a machine rebuilt around its damage.

The coastal rocks were slick with spray. The fishfolk were clearing the surf line — the first ones up, their bodies shifting from horizontal to vertical, the transition from swimmer to walker occurring in the time it took to draw breath. They were tall. Taller than the river fishfolk — a deep-water variant, adapted to pressure, their bodies denser, their scales thicker, the chitinous pikes in their hands longer and heavier.

Nahlah met them at the tideline.

The blade moved. It moved the way mercury moves — fluid, continuous, the transitions between strikes invisible, the edge catching the light in a silver thread that wrote its sentence across the morning air. She cut the first fishfolk at the throat, a horizontal stroke that separated the gill structure from the body and dropped it without sound. The second she took at the knee — the joint, the point of structural failure in the fishfolk's anatomy, where the aquatic-to-terrestrial transition left a gap in the chitinous plating. The third she did not cut at all. She stepped inside its pike's reach and drove the blade's pommel into the center of its chest, and the fishfolk folded around the impact and the weight of its fall carried it back into the surf.

She fought alone on the rocks for eleven minutes.

The fishfolk came in ones and twos and then in fives, clearing the surf, finding their footing, raising their pikes, and Nahlah was there — silver and fast and lethal, the blade weaving its mercury patterns, her single arm executing combinations that two-armed fighters could not replicate because two arms create options and options create hesitation and Nahlah did not hesitate. The blade was an extension of the arm and the arm was an extension of the will and the will was the thing that twenty-three years of service and a severed limb and a stolen birthright and a sister's betrayal had forged into something the fishfolk's information-processing could not model: a human being who fought not from aggression but from structure, whose violence was architectural, whose every cut was load-bearing.

Eleven minutes. The rocks ran with calcium. The fishfolk's dead piled at the tideline like driftwood after a storm.

The militia arrived — the reserve, Nahlah's fifty, running down the bluff path with their casters charged and their faces holding the particular expression of soldiers who have just watched their general do something they will describe to their grandchildren. They took positions on the rocks. The caster fire resumed. The coastal approach slowed, stalled, the fishfolk's advance meeting resistance it could not climb.

Nahlah stood among the dead at the waterline. Her silver armor was dark with calcium spray. Her blade dripped. Her breath came in measured cycles — not gasping, not ragged, the controlled respiration of a body that had been trained to treat exertion as data and data as fuel.

She looked at the sea. The fishfolk were regrouping. The third wave's front had broken on the rocks but the depth remained — shadows beneath the surface, patient, innumerable, waiting for the next signal, the next pulse from the collective intelligence that synchronized their movements.

The pulse came. The fishfolk surged.

"Hold position," Nahlah said. Her voice carried above the surf. Not loud — clear. The clarity of a voice that had issued orders in every engagement for two decades and that had learned to project not through volume but through certainty.

The line held. The casters fired. The fishfolk died and were replaced and died and were replaced.

---

Nahlah pushed too hard. She knew she was pushing too hard. She knew it the way she knew the defense's architecture — from the inside, structurally, the knowledge of her own body's load-bearing capacity as precise as her knowledge of the trench system's. She was past the point of sustainable exertion. She had been past it since the eleventh minute on the rocks.

But the northern ford had broken. The chimera riders had fallen back to the bluff as ordered, but the fishfolk had forded the river in force and were now moving south along the ridge, flanking the Hupasa position, and if they reached the eastern bluff the command post would fall and the defense would collapse from the center.

Nahlah led the counterattack personally.

Thirty soldiers. A run up the bluff path, across the ridge, into the flank of the fishfolk column that was moving south through the scrub. She hit them at the column's midpoint — the place where the formation was thinnest, where the fishfolk's collective intelligence was dividing its attention between the advance and the rear, where a sharp enough blow could split the organism into halves that would each need to re-establish coordination.

The blade. The mercury blade, catching the twin suns' light, the monomolecular edge opening the fishfolk's chitinous armor the way a key opens a lock — with precision, with specificity, with the intimate knowledge of the mechanism's weakness. Nahlah cut through the column. Her thirty soldiers followed. The chimera riders, seeing the counterattack, charged from the bluff, and the fishfolk column broke — not retreating, they did not retreat, but splitting, the two halves losing coherence, the collective pulse stuttering.

Nahlah stood at the column's break point. The ridge around her was littered with grey-green bodies. Her soldiers held their line. The chimera riders wheeled and charged and wheeled again, their warbeasts trampling the disoriented fishfolk with hooves that cracked chitin like glass.

The battle turned. Not won — turned. The fishfolk would come again. They always came again. But the immediate threat had been met, and the defense held, and Nahlah stood on the ridge with her silver blade at her side and the twin suns above her and she breathed.

And then she stopped.

---

Not from a wound.

The blade did not fall from her hand. Her knees did not buckle. There was no dramatic collapse, no slow-motion descent, no cinematic progression from standing to fallen. She simply stopped. The way a clock stops — the mechanism ceasing, the hands still, the face unchanged. One moment she was breathing. The next she was not.

The hala.

The poison that had lived in her marrow since childhood. The hereditary toxin that the Jian bloodline carried — dormant, invisible, a molecular sleeper agent encoded in the bone. Aharah had stolen the Lyonessa ordeal to prevent the gene-treatment from activating it. And the gene-treatment had not activated it. And the ordeal had not activated it. And the years of service had not activated it, and the severed arm had not activated it, and the war had not activated it, because the hala did not respond to trauma. It responded to exhaustion. It waited for the body's reserves to empty — for the marrow to draw on its deepest stores, for the cellular machinery to reach the layer where the toxin slept — and then it woke.

Twenty-three years of dormancy. Ended by eleven minutes on the rocks and a counterattack up a ridge and the accumulated expenditure of a life spent converting pain into purpose.

The hala did not hurt. It did not burn or sting or produce any of the sensations the body associates with destruction. It simply stopped things. The marrow ceased to produce. The blood thinned. The oxygen that the muscles needed to contract and the heart needed to beat and the brain needed to process the twin suns' light and the sound of the sea and the sight of the fishfolk's dead and the weight of the silver blade arrived more slowly, and then less, and then not at all.

Nahlah sat down.

She did not choose to sit down. Her body sat down. It folded itself into the command chair at the eastern bluff's parapet — the chair she had occupied at the battle's start, the chair her adjutant had vacated when the coastal approach required reinforcement. She sat in the chair and her silver armor settled around her and her blade rested across her knees, and the battle continued below her, and above her the twin suns tracked their paths, and Nahlah Jian, General of the Shakyan defense, eldest daughter of the Jian lineage, the woman who should have been Lyonessa, sat in her command chair and died.

Her purple eyes stayed open.

They stayed open and they did not dim. The color held — the deep violet that was the Jian signature, the pigment that in Aharah expressed as gold sunskin and in Anais expressed as astromantic sight and in Nahlah expressed as this: a purple so vivid it looked like a wound, a color that belonged on an iris the way a jewel belongs in a setting, precise and unnatural and beautiful.

The eyes stayed open. The purple held. The twin suns reached their apex and the shadows shortened and the battle at the crossing turned and held and the fishfolk's assault broke against the Hupasa defense like a wave against the basalt cliffs, and the soldiers fought and the batteries fired and the chimera riders charged and wheeled, and Nahlah sat in her command chair with her blade across her knees and her purple eyes open and did not see any of it.

---

Aharah arrived three days later.

She came from the Winter Palace. She had left the day after the wedding — not to follow Hyfe and Anais into the Crater but to return to the front, to the war that did not pause for pilgrimages or prophecies or the recognition that the planet's heart was calling. The war was the war. Someone had to fight it while the seekers descended.

She rode hard. Three days across the frozen plateau, down through the Outsidelands' margin, south along the river valley to the Hupasa. She rode a chimera — one of the Hazzan warbeasts, grey-flanked, bad-tempered, its gait designed for endurance rather than speed. She did not sleep. The sunskin metabolized the twin suns' light and converted it to energy and the energy sustained her body the way food and sleep could not, and she rode through the cold and the dark and the intermittent green flicker of the microwave anomalies.

She smelled the battle before she saw it. Calcium and ozone. The mineral death-scent of fishfolk. The sharp tang of caster discharge. The particular silence that follows a major engagement — not quiet but emptied, the air itself exhausted.

The Hupasa was held. The crossing was choked with grey-green dead. The batteries stood on the bluffs, their housings scorched, their power cells dark. Soldiers moved through the aftermath with the stunned, mechanical efficiency of people completing tasks their bodies had memorized and their minds had not yet processed.

Aharah dismounted at the eastern bluff. She climbed the path. She reached the command post.

The adjutant met her. Young. His face held the particular damage of a person who has seen a thing he will carry for the rest of his life. He did not speak. He stepped aside.

Aharah walked to the command chair.

Nahlah sat in it. Silver armor. Silver blade across her knees. Purple eyes open, fixed on the crossing below, holding the twin suns' light without blinking, because blinking was a function of the living and Nahlah was not living.

Aharah stood before her sister.

---

Three days of silence.

She did not speak. She did not weep. She did not perform any of the rituals that Shakyan tradition prescribed for the dead — the anointing, the prayers, the formal address to Mater that committed the body to the planet's keeping. She was silent the way stone is silent: not empty but dense, holding within its structure a pressure that had no outlet and that would, in time, reshape the stone itself.

She washed her sister's body.

She removed the silver armor piece by piece. The breastplate first — unlatching the clasps at the shoulder and side, lifting it away, revealing the tunic beneath, dark with dried sweat. The right arm's vambrace, the gauntlet, the articulated finger-plates that had allowed the blade hand to grip and release with mechanical precision. The empty socket's mirror plate, polished, reflecting Aharah's face back at her — a face she did not recognize, a face that had been rearranged by the thing it was looking at.

She washed the body with water from the river. Cold water. She washed the face, the neck, the arm that had held the blade, the shoulder where the arm that was missing had been. She washed her sister's hands — the right hand, calloused from the sword grip, the fingers strong and curved even in death, holding the shape of the weapon they had carried. She washed the short-cropped hair that Nahlah had cut the morning Aharah stole the ordeal and that Nahlah had never grown back, keeping it short, military, a declaration made in keratin.

She dressed her sister in clean cloth. White. Shakyan burial linen, brought from the camp's stores. She wrapped the body the way Auntie had taught them to wrap — methodically, beginning at the feet, each fold precise, each layer a sentence in a language older than speech.

She sharpened the blade.

She sat on the bluff's edge with a whetstone and the silver blade across her knees — the same position Nahlah had died in, the same orientation, the blade's weight on her legs, the twin suns' light on the monomolecular edge. She drew the stone along the blade's length. The edge did not need sharpening. The monomolecular line did not dull. But Aharah sharpened it anyway, because sharpening was care, was attention, was the act of tending a thing that belonged to a person who could no longer tend it, and the act was the grief. The whetstone's sound was the grief. The repetition — stone on metal, stone on metal, the rhythm steady, mindless, the body performing a function the mind could not face — was the grief.

She laid the blade across the pyre.

The pyre was simple. Wood from the supply stores, stacked in the Shakyan configuration — a rectangular base, a platform at hip height, the body placed on the platform with the head toward Aleph and the feet toward Beth, facing both suns, refusing to choose. The blade lay across Nahlah's wrapped chest, its silver length catching the firelight that had not yet been lit, reflecting flames that did not yet exist.

Aharah lit the pyre at dusk. She used no accelerant. She knelt and held the flame to the wood's base and the wood caught and the fire climbed and the soldiers on the bluff stood at distance and watched and were silent.

The fire burned.

It burned hot and clean. The white cloth darkened, crisped, dissolved. The blade's silver dulled in the heat, the metal losing its cold luster, taking on the amber of the flames. Nahlah's body was inside the fire and the fire was inside the night and the night was vast and cold and held the fire the way the planet held everything — patiently, without opinion, with the geological indifference of a body that had absorbed a million deaths and would absorb a million more.

When the fire reached its peak — when the flames stood vertical and still, as if the wind itself had paused out of respect — Aharah reached in.

She reached into the fire with her bare hand.

The sunskin metabolized heat. This was its primary function — the photosynthetic pigment that covered her body, the Lyonessa gene's most visible expression, absorbed electromagnetic radiation and converted it to cellular energy. Sunlight was its preferred frequency. Fire was not sunlight. Fire was hotter, cruder, the radiation profile broader and more chaotic. The sunskin took what it could and rejected the rest and the rest was pain — a searing, total pain that began at the fingertips and climbed the arm and filled the body with the information that it was being damaged, that the tissue was cooking, that the hand inside the fire was a hand that would carry the scar of this moment for the rest of its life.

Aharah did not withdraw.

Her fingers found the medallion. The Lyonessa medallion — the lion's tooth, the sigil of the Jian lineage, the disc of dark metal that Nahlah had worn at her throat since enlisting, the emblem of the title she had been denied. It was half-melted. The lion's profile was distorted, the features running, the metal losing the definition of its casting and becoming something rawer — a shape that suggested a lion the way grief suggests its cause, imprecisely, through damage.

Aharah pulled the medallion from the fire. It was glowing. The metal was soft with heat, orange at the center, red at the edges, the surface molten enough to deform under pressure. She held it in her burned hand and looked at it. The lion looked back through its melting face.

She pressed the medallion against her chest.

The sound was brief. The smell was not. The burning of flesh — her flesh, the sunskin cooking under the medallion's heat, the tissue charring, the Lyonessa gene scrambling to repair what the fire was destroying — produced a smell that the soldiers on the bluff would carry to their own pyres. Sweet and acrid. The smell of something alive becoming something permanent.

Aharah held the medallion against her skin until the metal cooled. Until the heat transferred. Until the lion's half-melted profile fused with the sunskin of her sternum, the damaged tissue growing over and around and through the metal, the body and the emblem becoming one — not worn but integrated, not carried but incorporated, the way a tree incorporates a nail.

She released her hand. The medallion stayed. Fused to her chest, half-melted, the lion's face distorted, the metal and the skin married by fire. It hurt. It would always hurt. That was the point.

---

The commanders came the next morning.

They came because the defense needed orders. The fishfolk had retreated to the deep channels but the retreat was temporary — a spawning cycle's pause, a regrouping, the collective intelligence redistributing its reserves. They would come again. Days, perhaps hours. The Hupasa had held but the holding had cost: batteries at twelve percent, two auto-turrets destroyed, thirty-one soldiers dead, the chimera riders' warbeasts wounded and exhausted.

The commanders needed a general. Nahlah was dead. The chain of command reached for the next link and the next link was a bureaucratic question no one in the field had the authority to answer.

They stood before Aharah — five officers, their armor battle-stained, their faces carrying the specific fatigue of people who have been effective for too long and are approaching the border where effectiveness dissolves. They looked at the woman before them: the Lyonessa, the exile, the thief. Her hand was burned. Her chest bore a fused medallion that smelled of scorched metal and cooked skin. Her face was a surface that no expression could occupy.

"By what authority?" the senior commander asked. Not a challenge — a genuine question. A procedural necessity. The defense required legitimacy. The soldiers needed to know who was in command and by what right.

The answer came from the east, carried by a Shakyan messenger on a spent chimera, a folded page bearing the Oracle-designate's seal. The messenger had ridden from the Winter Palace. The message was one word.

Anais — blind, brilliant, descending into the Crater at the head of a hundred pilgrims — had felt it. Had felt her sister's death across the electromagnetic field that she read the way others read light. Had felt the hala's activation, the marrow's cessation, the moment when Nahlah Jian's body stopped being a body and became a monument. And from the Crater's depths, she had sent a word.

The commander opened the message. He read it.

*Blood.*

He looked at Aharah. Aharah looked at the Hupasa crossing, at the grey-green dead, at the batteries standing dark on the bluffs, at the war that did not pause for grief.

She did not speak. She walked to the command post. She opened the case that Nahlah's adjutant had guarded for three days — a case of dark wood, lined in silk, containing a helmet. The three-faced golden helmet of the Hazzan generals: three visages cast in gold, each facing a different direction. The strategist's face, looking left. The diplomat's face, looking right. The warrior's face, looking forward.

Aharah lifted the helmet. It was heavy. It was meant to be heavy. Command was not a lightness.

She placed it on her head. The warrior's face forward.

The commanders watched. The soldiers on the bluff watched. The twin suns watched from their separate positions in the sky, Aleph's white light and Beth's red light falling on the golden helmet and splitting into the twin colors of an empire at war.

Aharah walked to the parapet. She looked at the crossing. The fishfolk's dead. The dark water beyond. The deep channels where the enemy regrouped. She looked at the terrain with the eyes of a general and the grief of a sister and the burned hand of a woman who had pulled a lion's tooth from a pyre and pressed it into her own chest because there was no other way to carry what needed carrying.

She turned. She faced the commanders.

"Recharge the batteries. Service the turrets. Rotate the chimera riders to the eastern flank. I want scouts at the northern ford by midday and a runner to the border garrison requesting Hazzan artillery support." She paused. "The Oracle said no Hazzan soldiers on Shakyan soil. The Oracle is dead. The war is not."

The commanders moved. The orders propagated through the defense like a pulse through a nerve — electric, immediate, the machinery of command reactivating around a new center. Soldiers repositioned. Batteries hummed. The chimera riders mounted and wheeled toward the eastern flank, their warbeasts fresh from three hours of rest, their riders carrying the expression of people who have been given direction after a period of directionlessness and who are grateful for it.

Aharah stood at the parapet in the golden helmet. The warrior's face forward. The burned hand at her side. The lion's tooth fused to her chest, beneath the armor, a secret and a scar and a vow.

She would hold the line. She would hold it for Nahlah, who had held lines her entire life. She would hold it for Anais, who was descending into the planet's heart to find the thing that all the lines were drawn to protect. She would hold it for Hyfe, who had answered violence with understanding and who was walking, even now, toward the answer.

And when the line held, when the fishfolk broke and retreated and the defense stabilized, she would turn from the coast and walk toward the Crater. Toward the Khan, who had ordered the oracle's death. Toward the war's center. Toward the place where the planet's heart beat in its buried chamber, where her sister walked blind among pilgrims, where the question that Hassan had been asking since a game in a basement first gave it a name waited for its answer.

The twin suns rose. The golden helmet caught their light.

Aharah faced the Crater.
