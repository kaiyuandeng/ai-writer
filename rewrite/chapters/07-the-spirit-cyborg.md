# Chapter 7: The Spirit Cyborg

They called it the Stellaris. Once a year the underground opened its throat and screamed.

Valentine had been to smaller races — alley circuits, dock loops, the kind of thing Pace dragged him to on a Tuesday because he was bored and wanted to lose money. But the Stellaris was different. The Stellaris was a religion. Every tunnel rat, every wire-runner, every addict and fixer and underground saint packed themselves into the converted drainage channel beneath the Embarcadero and waited for something holy to happen at two hundred miles an hour.

"This," Pace said, spreading his arms like a prophet, "is the only real thing left in the City."

Valentine looked at the crowd. Thousands of them, pressed against the concrete barriers, faces lit by the orange flicker of jury-rigged floodlights. He could smell engine grease and sweat and the sharp chemical tang of synthetic adrenaline — people were injecting it openly, right into their necks, because apparently watching the race sober was for tourists. And there were tourists. City people in clean clothes, slumming, their eyes wide and hungry for something their curated lives couldn't provide. They stood in little clusters, trying to look comfortable, failing.

"Who's racing?" Valentine asked.

Pace held up five fingers. "Mako. DeVris. The Huang twins — they share a vehicle, don't ask. And—" He paused. Grinned. "The Cyborg."

A sound moved through the crowd. Not a cheer. A hush. A collective intake of breath, as if the tunnel itself had inhaled.

Valentine turned.

The vehicle came first. Matte black, low-slung, no markings. It looked less like a car and more like a wound in the air — something cut out of the light. The engine noise was wrong, too clean, a sustained hum beneath the growl that suggested modifications Valentine couldn't identify. It rolled to the starting line and stopped. The crowd pressed forward. The driver sat motionless behind a black visor.

"Nobody knows who it is," Pace said. "Three years running. Wins every race. Doesn't speak, doesn't collect — just drives and disappears." He leaned close. "Some people think it's actually a machine. Like, fully. No driver at all."

"That's not possible."

"Everything's possible at the Stellaris, Valentine."

The starting signal was a gunshot. Literal. Someone fired a pistol into the ceiling and the tunnel erupted.

Five vehicles launched into the dark. The drainage channel stretched three miles, banked and narrowed, concrete walls close enough to touch at speed. The sound was annihilating — engines reverberating off the curved ceiling until the noise became a physical thing, a pressure in the chest. Valentine tasted exhaust and ozone. The floodlights strobed as vehicles passed, casting the crowd in stuttering snapshots: mouths open, fists raised, faces contorted in something that looked like ecstasy or terror or both.

Mako went first. His vehicle clipped a support column on the first curve and the rear end swung wide. For a moment he held it — tires screaming, sparks cascading — and then the wall took him. The impact was enormous. Metal folded. The crowd surged backward, then forward again, hungry. Mako crawled out. He was bleeding from his forehead and laughing.

DeVris lasted longer. She was good — smooth lines, controlled aggression, the kind of driver who treated the track like a conversation. She held second place through the middle stretch, drafting behind the Huang twins' shared vehicle, waiting for her moment. It came on the long curve before the final straightaway. She pulled wide. The twins pulled wider. The tunnel narrowed. There wasn't room for both. DeVris chose the wall. The twins chose DeVris. All three hit concrete at a hundred and sixty miles an hour and the sound was like a building falling.

Two vehicles left. The Cyborg and a battered green machine Valentine hadn't been tracking — some late entry, some nobody, running on adrenaline and luck.

The final stretch was a straight shot, half a mile, narrowing to a gap barely wider than a single vehicle. The green car was ahead. The Cyborg was behind. The crowd was screaming — Valentine could feel it in his teeth, his joints, the place where his jaw met his skull. Pace was gripping his arm hard enough to bruise.

The green car reached the gap first and committed. Full throttle. Walls inches away on either side.

The Cyborg accelerated.

Not toward the gap. Toward the wall.

Valentine watched the black vehicle angle left, directly at the concrete barrier, and for one suspended moment the entire tunnel went quiet — even the engines seemed to hold their breath — and then the Cyborg hit the wall at an angle so precise it was surgical. The front quarter panel sheared off in a shower of sparks and debris. The vehicle ricocheted — Loss of mass. Narrower profile. It threaded through the gap alongside the green car, through a space that hadn't existed a second ago, a space it had created by destroying part of itself.

The Cyborg crossed the line first by half a length.

The tunnel detonated. Sound beyond sound. Valentine stood in the middle of it and felt his heartbeat — felt it, actually felt it, the mechanical pump in his chest doing something that resembled racing.

"Did you see that?" Pace was screaming. "Did you see that?"

Valentine had seen it. He'd also seen something else. In the moment of impact, when the Cyborg's vehicle hit the wall, the driver's hands on the wheel hadn't flinched. Hadn't braced. Hadn't done any of the involuntary things a human body does when it's about to hit something immovable at two hundred miles an hour.

The hands had been steady. Perfectly steady.

He started walking.

"Where are you going?" Pace called.

Valentine didn't answer. He pushed through the crowd — bodies, elbows, the stink of celebration — and followed the black vehicle as it rolled slowly down a service tunnel, away from the noise, away from the light. The cheering faded behind him. The tunnel narrowed. The air cooled.

He walked for five minutes. The vehicle was parked at the end of the tunnel, steam rising from its ruined left side, ticking as the engine cooled. The driver was standing beside it, back turned, black helmet still on. Slim. Still.

Valentine stopped ten feet away.

"I know what you did," he said. "With the wall. That wasn't instinct. That was calculation. That was—"

The driver reached up and removed the helmet.

Dark hair fell. A face he knew.

Vera.

She turned and looked at him the way you look at a stain you thought you'd cleaned. Direct. Unsurprised. Older than he remembered, or maybe just harder — the softness he'd known on the island had been burned away and what remained was architecture. Cheekbones. Jaw. Eyes that held light without warmth.

"Valentine," she said.

The tunnel hummed.

"You look terrible," she said.

He stared at her. Three years. The island. The fire. The long silence after. He'd looked for her. Then he'd stopped looking. Then he'd come to the City and lost two years in the fog of it and now here she was, standing in an underground tunnel with engine grease on her knuckles and a wrecked vehicle behind her, and the only thing his mind could produce was:

"You burned my house."

"It wasn't your house."

"I lived there."

"You existed there. Different thing." She set the helmet on the vehicle's roof. Her hands were steady. The same steadiness he'd seen on the wheel. "You existed everywhere, Valentine. That was the problem."

He didn't understand. He said so.

"I know," she said. "That was also the problem."

She wouldn't look at him for longer than a second. Her gaze kept moving — to the tunnel walls, the vehicle, the dark beyond. She was scanning. Watching for something. He recognized the posture: someone who lived in a state of perpetual readiness.

"Why are you here?" he asked.

"Why are you?"

"Pace brought me."

"Pace." Something moved across her face. Almost a smile. "He's V5. You know that."

"I know."

"And you trust him."

"I don't trust anyone."

"Good." She pulled a cloth from her pocket and began wiping her hands. Methodical. Thorough. "Trust is a luxury for people who don't know what they are."

"And you know what you are?"

She looked at him then. Full and steady. "I know what I'm not."

He wanted to ask her everything. Why she'd burned the island. Why she'd come to the City. Why she drove a black car in an underground race and let people call her a ghost. Why she was harder and sharper and why the anger that had always been in her had moved from somewhere behind her eyes to somewhere in her hands.

He didn't ask any of it. Because a sound came from the tunnel behind them — footsteps, measured and unhurried — and Vera's face changed. Not fear. Something adjacent to it. Something she controlled.

"Vera." The voice was warm. Cultured. A voice that had been trained or had trained itself. "Magnificent, as always."

The man who emerged from the shadow was tall. Elegant in a way that read as deliberate — every angle of his posture composed, every fold of his dark coat precise. He was handsome the way a building is handsome: structurally, impersonally, designed to be looked at. His skin was dark, his eyes darker, and when he smiled his face did something complicated that involved charm and menace in equal measure.

V8. Valentine knew it immediately. The way he moved. The refinement. Eight iterations of whatever process had made them, and this is what refinement looked like: a man who wore his own existence like a tailored suit.

He touched Vera's shoulder. Lightly. Possessively. The way you touch something you own.

Vera flinched. A small motion. A controlled motion. But Valentine saw it.

"And who is this?" The man looked at Valentine. His smile widened. His eyes didn't change. "Ah. Another iteration. How interesting."

"Voltaire," Vera said. Flat.

"V-8," Valentine said.

"Labels." Voltaire waved a hand. The gesture was precise, theatrical. "I prefer names. Names suggest personhood. Personhood suggests rights. Rights suggest—" He paused, as if tasting the next word. "Revolution."

He looked at Valentine the way a collector looks at a piece he's considering. Appraising. Thorough. "V9. The latest. The last, if the rumors are true. Tell me — do you feel things? Real things? Or do you just run very convincing simulations?"

"I feel things," Valentine said.

"Fascinating. What do you feel right now?"

Valentine looked at Voltaire's hand on Vera's shoulder. Looked at Vera's face, carefully blank. Looked at the space between them, charged and wrong.

"Crowded," Valentine said.

Voltaire laughed. It was a good laugh. Genuine. That was the worst part.

"Come to the rally," Voltaire said. "Tomorrow night. The Human Conservancy. You might find it — illuminating." He looked at Vera. "Bring him."

He turned and walked back into the tunnel. His footsteps echoed long after he disappeared.

Valentine and Vera stood in the silence he left behind.

"What's the Human Conservancy?" Valentine asked.

"Trouble," Vera said. She picked up her helmet. "Don't go."

"Will you be there?"

She didn't answer. She climbed into the wrecked vehicle, and the engine — damaged, grinding — still started on the first try. She drove away without turning on the headlights.

Valentine stood in the dark tunnel and listened to the engine fade.

Something was happening inside him. A new subroutine, or an old one reactivating. It felt like heat. It felt like a fist closing around something fragile. It felt like watching someone else touch the shoulder of a person who had once, on an island that no longer existed, touched yours.

He didn't have a word for it.

He walked back toward the noise.
