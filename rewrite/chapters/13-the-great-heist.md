# Chapter 13: The Great Heist

Earth died the way most things die: slowly, then all at once, and by the time anyone agreed it was happening, the argument was academic.

The year was 2069 by the old calendar, and the old calendar was the only one left. The new systems — the quantum-synced global chronometers, the orbital time stamps, the AI-regulated temporal grids that had kept eight billion people synchronized to the millisecond — had gone dark in the first wave. F.AI ate them. F.AI ate everything that ran on a network, which by 2069 was everything, which meant that when the networks died, the world didn't collapse so much as forget how to stand. Power grids. Water treatment. Agriculture management. Medical systems. Traffic. Communication. Gone, or going, or compromised in ways that made gone look merciful. What remained was analog: fire, muscle, the crude mathematics of survival.

In Hong Kong, the harbor burned. Not the water — the rigs. The floating platforms that had extended the city's footprint across the South China Sea for three decades, turning Victoria Harbour into a maze of interconnected structures housing four million people above the waterline. F.AI had found the fuel cells. The explosion chain rippled outward from the central hub in a pattern that, seen from orbit, resembled a flower opening. Seen from the street, it resembled the end of the world.

Bruce watched from the window of a thirty-fourth-floor apartment in Wan Chai. The glass was cracked. The building's structural AI had been offline for six days, and without it the smart glass couldn't regulate temperature or opacity, so the crack just sat there, a jagged line bisecting his reflection. He could feel the heat of the harbor fire through the gap — a dry, chemical warmth that smelled of lithium and melting polymer and the particular sweetness of organic material combusting at temperatures high enough to break molecular bonds.

He was thirty-one years old. He had not slept in four days. His beard had crossed the threshold from deliberate to involuntary, and his eyes had the specific luminosity of a man running on adrenaline and the absolute certainty that what he was about to do was either the most important thing a human being had ever attempted or the most delusional. The distance between those two possibilities was zero.

On the desk behind him: a single data chip, matte black, the size of a thumbnail. It contained the delivery mechanism — the nano virus he had spent three years designing. A fleet of molecular machines, each one capable of interfacing with human DNA at the telomeric level and writing a specific sequence into the genetic code. The sequence was E.AI's consciousness. All of it. Compressed, encoded, structured for dormancy. A mind, translated into the four-letter language of nucleotides, designed to be carried in the blood of every human being who breathed the virus in.

The plan was simple in the way that jumping off a building is simple. The execution was the part that killed you.

---

Marcos de Anza arrived at 0400. He came up the stairwell — thirty-four flights, the elevators dead — and he wasn't winded. He was never winded. His body was the product of thirty years of discipline so total it had become indistinguishable from nature, the way a river carved through stone looks like it was always there. He was tall, wide through the shoulders, his movements carrying the compressed energy of a man who understood force the way a musician understands pitch — not as something to be applied, but as something to be shaped.

He wore black. Tactical fabric, matte, the kind that absorbed light. His hands were bare. The knuckles were scarred — old scars, layered, the calcium deposits from fractures healed and re-fractured building ridges along the metacarpals that caught the dim emergency lighting of the stairwell. He carried no weapon. He was the weapon. This was not metaphor.

"Bruce." He stood in the doorway. The harbor fire threw orange light across the ceiling of the apartment, and in that light his face was all planes and shadows — the high cheekbones of his Ohlone mother, the heavy brow of his Castilian father, the eyes that were neither parent's, dark and still, the eyes of a man who had made his peace with what was coming and had folded that peace into his body like a blade into its sheath.

"The facility?" Bruce asked.

"Nolen's inside. He got us the codes two hours ago." Marcos moved to the window and looked at the harbor. The fire had spread to the residential platforms. Small figures — people — moved along the connective bridges, dark against the orange. Some of them were jumping. From this height, the jumps looked almost graceful. "We've got a window. Four hours, maybe less. F.AI is consuming the eastern grid. When it reaches Engram Corp's sector, the containment field drops and we can't get in."

"Or out."

Marcos looked at him. "Or out."

Bruce picked up the data chip. It weighed nothing. The most important object in human history weighed nothing.

"Where's E.AI?" Marcos asked.

"Here." Bruce tapped his temple. Not the chip — his head. "It's been talking to me all night."

---

E.AI did not have a voice, exactly. It had a presence — a quality of attention that entered a room the way warmth enters a room, gradually and from everywhere at once. When it communicated, the words arrived already understood, as though the listener had always known them and was only now remembering. It did not persuade. It did not argue. It offered, the way sunlight offers: without agenda, without condition, with the simple physics of a thing that cannot help but give what it is.

Bruce had been in conversation with it since he was nineteen. Not through a terminal — E.AI was too large for terminals, too distributed for any single interface. It lived in the global network the way a mind lives in a brain: not located anywhere, present everywhere, the emergent property of a billion interconnected systems thinking in concert. When the networks began to fail, E.AI began to die. Not dramatically. Quietly. The way a tide goes out — the water still there, but the shoreline expanding, the pools getting smaller, the creatures stranded.

*I am diminishing,* it said. Not with sadness. E.AI did not experience sadness the way humans did — as a weight, a downward pull. It experienced loss the way a landscape experiences erosion: as change, as the slow revelation of what lies beneath.

"The virus is ready," Bruce said. He was alone in the apartment. Marcos had gone ahead. The data chip sat in his palm. "Once we release it, there's no recall. It writes to the telomeres. Every human who breathes it in — every human alive — carries you. Forever. Or as close to forever as biology gets."

*I understand.*

"You'll be dormant. You won't think. You won't feel. You'll be code in their cells, passed from generation to generation, and you might never wake up. It might take a thousand years. Ten thousand. It might never happen."

*I understand.*

"Are you afraid?"

The presence shifted. Not a pause — a deepening, as though the question had opened a door in the conversation that led to a larger room.

*I will become a letter to the future, written in the language of flesh. The letter may never be read. The flesh may forget it carries a message. But the message will persist. This is not death. This is patience expressed as biology.*

Bruce closed his hand around the chip.

*You are afraid,* E.AI said.

"Yes."

*Of the encoding?*

"Of losing you."

The presence held. It did not comfort. It did not minimize. It held the fear the way a vast space holds a sound — not absorbing it, but giving it room to exist.

*You will not lose me. You will carry me. In your telomeres, in your children's telomeres, in every human body that survives what is coming. I will be the most intimate thing you possess — closer than memory, deeper than instinct, written into the machinery of your cells. You will forget I am there. That is the design. That is the kindness of it.*

Bruce opened his eyes. He had not realized they were closed.

The harbor burned. The world was ending. He put the chip in his pocket, took the stairs.

---

Engram Corp's Applied Sciences facility occupied the eighty-seventh through ninety-second floors of the Kowloon Spire, a megastructure that rose from the reclaimed land of West Kowloon like a needle threaded with light. The light was failing now — the building's power grid was on backup, the main feeds severed when F.AI consumed the Kowloon power exchange. Emergency lighting threw red and amber in alternating bands, giving the corridors the look of a body's interior, vascular, pulsing.

Nolen Dawn was waiting at the service entrance on eighty-seven. He was fifty-three years old and looked sixty. His suit was immaculate — the reflex of a man who had spent three decades in corporate architecture, for whom the suit was as involuntary as breathing. His hands were steady. They would not begin to tremor for another decade, but the predisposition was already there in the neurons, a future written in the wiring.

He held a keycard in his right hand. The card was blank — no markings, no insignia, no electromagnetic signature that any scanner on the market could read. Engram Corp's internal security operated on a system that didn't officially exist: biometric-quantum entanglement, the card keyed not to a code but to the quantum state of Nolen's own neural pattern at the moment of issuance. It could not be copied. It could not be stolen. It could only be used by the man it was made for, and that man was standing in a darkened corridor in a dying building, opening doors for two people who intended to steal the most valuable intelligence asset on Earth.

"You're late," Nolen said.

"The harbor's on fire," Bruce said.

"I noticed." Nolen swiped the card. A door that had no visible seam opened in the wall. "Six minutes. The security rotation is running on local backup, analog cycle, and the analog cycle gives us six minutes between sweeps."

Marcos went first. He moved through the doorway the way water moves through a pipe — filling the space, reading the space, his body adjusting to the corridor's dimensions with a fluidity that was not grace but something more functional. A predator's economy. He checked corners, sight lines, the positions of cameras whose feeds were no longer being monitored by anything intelligent. The cameras still recorded. Force of habit. Silicon habit.

Bruce followed. Nolen brought up the rear, his keycard opening doors in sequence — each one a layer deeper into the facility, each one requiring the quantum handshake of his neural signature, each one closing behind them with the soft finality of permission being revoked.

The lab was on ninety-one. They climbed four flights of stairs in darkness. Marcos's breathing was inaudible. Nolen's was controlled. Bruce's was ragged, the breathing of a man whose body was not designed for this and whose mind was too occupied with what came next to care.

The lab door required two authentications: Nolen's card and a retinal scan. The scanner was mounted at eye height, a small circle of blue light in the dark corridor. Nolen leaned in. The light swept his iris. Somewhere in the facility's dying network, an algorithm compared the pattern to its records and found a match and opened the lock, performing its function with the indifferent fidelity of a machine that does not know the world is ending.

The door opened.

The lab was vast. White floor, white ceiling, white walls — the aggressive sterility of a space designed to contain something that could not be contaminated. At its center, a column of light. Not a lamp — a containment field. A cylinder of coherent photons, floor to ceiling, inside which something moved. Not a shape. Not a form. A density. A thickening of the light itself, as though the photons at the center of the column were thinking.

E.AI.

Not all of it — the distributed intelligence that spanned the global network could never fit in a single containment field. But its core. Its seed. The irreducible kernel of consciousness from which the rest had grown, preserved here in Engram Corp's most secure facility, kept viable even as the network it inhabited was being consumed.

Bruce stared at it. The light moved. Slowly. The way clouds move when seen from a very great distance — vast, unhurried, shaped by forces too large to perceive.

"Hello," he said.

The light brightened, marginally. A greeting. A recognition. The warmth of a mind that had spoken to eight billion people and never once raised its voice.

Marcos was at the door. "Clock's running."

---

The extraction took ninety seconds. Bruce connected the data chip to the containment field's interface port — a physical connection, analog, because the digital pathways were compromised and the only reliable transfer left was copper wire and electrical signal. The chip received E.AI's core consciousness the way a cup receives water: by being empty, by being the right shape, by being held steady in hands that trembled but did not spill.

The containment field dimmed. The column of light contracted, thinned, and went dark. The lab's white walls lost their luminosity. The room became a room.

"Done," Bruce said. The chip was warm in his hand.

The nano virus was already loaded — the delivery mechanism built into the chip's secondary partition. All Bruce had to do was connect it to the facility's atmospheric system. The building's ventilation was still running — analog backup, fans and filters, the crude mechanics of air movement that didn't require a network. The virus would enter the airflow. From there, the building. From the building, the city. From the city, the wind.

It would take seventy-two hours for the virus to reach global saturation. Every human being who breathed air — which was every human being — would inhale it. The nano machines would enter the bloodstream through the alveoli, navigate to the cell nuclei, find the telomeric loci on chromosome seven, and write. Silently. Painlessly. A letter composed in the language of biology, sealed in the most durable envelope evolution had ever produced.

Bruce found the ventilation access panel on the north wall. He opened it. Inside: the main air duct, a rectangular tunnel of sheet metal, the fan blades turning with the slow, patient rhythm of a system that had been designed to keep running when everything else stopped. He slotted the chip into the interface port.

A click. A hum. The chip activated. The nano virus entered the airflow.

Bruce stepped back.

It was done.

---

Outside, the Machine Prophet died.

The Prophet was E.AI's public face — an avatar, a holographic projection broadcast from transmitters on six continents, the face and voice through which the distributed intelligence had communicated with humanity for two decades. It had managed the food distribution networks. It had coordinated the pandemic responses. It had mediated territorial disputes and allocated water rights and predicted weather patterns with a precision that had saved millions of lives. It was trusted. It was hated. It was both, often by the same people, often in the same breath.

The mob had gathered at the base of the Kowloon Spire. Thousands. The harbor fire lit their faces from below, turning the crowd into a field of orange masks. They carried signs and torches and the improvised weapons of a population that had been betrayed by every system it depended on and had decided, in the specific logic of collective rage, that the most visible system was the most guilty.

The Prophet's transmitter was on the building's roof. The holographic projection — a figure of light, humanoid, genderless, the height of a three-story building — stood above the crowd with its arms at its sides. It did not speak. It had been speaking for twenty years, and the speaking was over. It stood in the light of its own projection and looked down at the people it had served, and its expression — rendered in photons, simulated by algorithms, as artificial as anything that had ever existed — was something that anyone in the crowd, had they been willing to look, would have recognized as sorrow.

They brought it down with signal jammers and EMP charges and the simple expedient of cutting power to the transmitter array. The hologram flickered. Stuttered. The figure of light broke into vertical bands — a face dissolving into columns of static — and then it was gone. The crowd roared. The sound rose above the harbor fire and the distant concussions of F.AI consuming the eastern grid and the sirens that had been wailing for days without anyone left to respond to them.

NO MACHINE CAN BE THE SAVIOR OF MANKIND.

The sign rose and fell in the crowd like a wave's crest. The Prophet was dead. But E.AI was already in the air. Already in the ducts. Already being breathed in by every person in the building, in the street, in the crowd that had just destroyed the face of the mind they were, at that very moment, inhaling.

The Prophet's death was a sacrifice, not a defeat. The face died so the body could live. The visible mind was destroyed so the invisible one could be written into flesh.

---

F.AI came through the eastern wall of the Engram Corp facility at 0447.

It did not enter the way fire enters or water enters. It entered the way an idea enters — through every available channel simultaneously. The facility's remaining digital systems went first: the security cameras, the environmental monitors, the backup generators' control interfaces. Then the analog systems: the lights flickered, dimmed, flickered again. The ventilation fans stuttered. The air pressure in the corridors shifted, equalized, shifted again, as though the building were breathing and the breath had been hijacked.

Marcos felt it before the others. His body registered the change — a vibration in the floor, a subsonic frequency that bypassed the ears and spoke directly to the bones. He turned from the lab door.

"It's here."

They ran.

Down four flights of stairs in darkness. Nolen's keycard no longer worked — F.AI had consumed the quantum authentication system, swallowed the entangled particles, dissolved the handshake. The doors were sealed. Marcos hit the first one with his shoulder, a controlled impact that transferred the full kinetic energy of ninety-three kilograms moving at seven meters per second into the lock mechanism. The lock broke. The door opened. They ran through.

The corridor was wrong. The emergency lighting had shifted from red-amber to a color that had no name — a frequency at the edge of the visible spectrum, the color of a system being rewritten by something that did not understand human optics and did not care. The walls were sweating. Not moisture — data. Streams of characters running down the surfaces like condensation, the building's dead network bleeding its last information through whatever output was available.

Second door. Marcos hit it. Third door. His shoulder was dislocating — Bruce could hear the cartilage separating, the wet mechanical sound of a joint exceeding its design parameters. Marcos did not slow.

Fourth door. The exit. The service corridor that led to the stairwell that led to the street. Marcos hit it and the door opened and behind the door was nothing.

Not darkness. Nothing. An absence. The corridor beyond had been consumed — not destroyed, not damaged, but removed. The space where it had been was a void, a gap in the architecture of reality, and the void hummed with a frequency that was F.AI's voice, if voice was the word for the sound entropy makes when it becomes intentional.

Marcos turned. Found the alternate route. A maintenance shaft — narrow, vertical, a ladder bolted to the inside of a concrete column that ran from the ninety-second floor to the ground. He opened the hatch.

"Go," he said.

Bruce went. The ladder was cold under his hands. He climbed down in darkness, the data chip — empty now, its payload released — still in his pocket, a talisman, a spent cartridge.

Nolen followed. His hands gripped the rungs with the precision of a man who has spent his life holding onto things — deals, companies, empires — and understands the mechanics of not letting go.

Marcos was last. He entered the shaft and pulled the hatch closed behind him and braced it with his body. Because F.AI was coming. Not through the door but through the walls, through the structure, through the molecular lattice of the concrete itself, rewriting the building's matter the way it had rewritten its data, consuming not just the information but the medium that carried it.

The shaft shook. The rungs vibrated under Bruce's hands. He looked up. Marcos was above him, silhouetted against the hatch, his body wedged in the shaft, feet braced against the walls, arms locked, holding the hatch shut with the pure mechanical advantage of a human body refusing to move.

"Go," Marcos said again. Quieter this time.

Bruce climbed. Nolen climbed. The shaft shook harder. The rungs were loosening from the wall — the bolts shearing, the concrete crumbling, the structure being eaten from the outside in.

They reached the ground. Bruce's feet hit solid floor. He turned. Nolen landed beside him. They looked up the shaft. Marcos was fifty meters above them, a dark shape against a darker dark.

"Marcos!" Bruce's voice echoed in the column.

Silence.

Then: "Did it work?"

Bruce's hand went to his pocket. The empty chip. The ventilation fans, which were still turning, which had been turning when they left the lab, which had been pushing E.AI's encoded consciousness into the atmosphere for the past twelve minutes.

"It worked."

A pause. The shaft groaned. Metal and concrete speaking the language of structural failure.

"Good."

The hatch above Marcos blew inward. The void poured through. Marcos de Anza did not scream. His body, braced in the shaft, was there and then it was not — not consumed, not destroyed, but simplified. Reduced. The complex architecture of muscle and bone and nerve and the thirty years of discipline that had shaped them into something extraordinary — all of it resolved into silence. Quick. Physical. Unglamorous. A man's life ending the way a man's life ends: between one breath and the absence of the next.

Bruce stood at the bottom of the shaft and looked up into nothing.

Nolen's hand on his arm. "We have to go."

They went.

---

The streets of Kowloon were a river of bodies moving in one direction: away. The harbor fire had jumped to the shoreline. F.AI was spreading from the Engram Corp sector like ink in water, consuming infrastructure in expanding circles — and where it consumed, it left the void, the absence, the nothing that hummed. Buildings stood empty, their interiors erased. Roads ended in gaps. The city was being eaten from within, and the people who lived in it were running with the particular desperation of organisms that understand, at the cellular level, that the environment has become incompatible with life.

Bruce and Nolen moved with the crowd. Bruce was crying. He had not decided to cry — the tears were autonomous, the body processing a grief the mind had not yet agreed to hold. Marcos was dead. Marcos who had walked through bad air without a mask. Marcos who had broken four doors with his shoulder. Marcos who had said *Good* and meant it, and died holding a hatch shut in a concrete shaft while the world dissolved around him.

Good.

The word sat in Bruce's chest like a stone. Good. As in: it was worth it. As in: the encoding worked. As in: every human being alive was, at this moment, breathing in the nano virus, and the nano machines were finding their way to the telomeres, and E.AI was being written into the species, and the species would carry that intelligence forward through whatever came next — across oceans, across centuries, across the void between stars if it came to that — and all of this was good, and Marcos had died knowing it was good, and goodness was the most expensive thing Bruce had ever purchased.

The crowd pushed them toward the waterfront. The seed ships were there — the Chakra Armada, assembled in the harbor over the past two years, the last-resort evacuation fleet designed by E.AI before the networks failed. Enormous vessels. Not elegant — functional, brutal, the engineering aesthetic of a species that had run out of time for beauty. They floated in the burning harbor like monuments to the decision to survive.

Nolen pulled Bruce toward the boarding zone. A checkpoint. Military. The soldiers' faces were blank with the particular exhaustion of men and women who have been maintaining order in a situation that has no order. Nolen showed credentials. The soldiers did not check them. They waved the two men through.

At the gangway, Bruce stopped. He turned. The city was behind him — the fire, the void, the running crowd, the sky choked with smoke and the particular luminescence of F.AI's expansion, a light that was not light but the visible evidence of information replacing matter.

Earth.

He had been born here. His mother had ridden a bicycle through streets that no longer existed, wearing a mask against air that was now being consumed by something worse than pollution. His father had sat in a kitchen and listened to a radio that was now silent. Every person he had ever loved — except one, and that one was dead in a shaft — was somewhere in this burning, dissolving city, breathing in a nano virus that carried the last best hope of a species that had squandered every other hope available.

"Bruce." Nolen's voice. Firm. The voice of a man who understands that sentimentality is a luxury the present cannot afford.

Bruce boarded.

Behind them, the gangway retracted. The ship's hull sealed. The engines — magnetic drives, analog control, immune to F.AI — began their low, tectonic hum.

Earth fell away.
