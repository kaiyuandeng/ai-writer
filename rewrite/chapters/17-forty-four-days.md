# Chapter 17: Forty-Four Days

The desert was red.

Not the red of blood or of fire or of any living thing. The red of iron. The red of rust. The red of a wound so old it had become geology. The Vermillion Desert stretched to every horizon — a sea of oxidized sand broken by ridges of darker stone that rose and fell like the spines of buried animals. No roads. No markers. No shade. The twin suns hung in a sky bleached to bone-white, and their light fell on the sand and the sand threw it back as heat.

Aharah walked.

Day one. Day two. Day three.

She set her course by the orabaun — the chain of satellites that crossed the night sky in a slow procession, pearl-bright, evenly spaced, their orbital paths unchanged since the First Landing. Navigation was the one use the satellites still served. Whatever else they had been built for — communication, surveillance, the thousand functions of a civilization that no longer remembered itself — had gone dark. The pearls moved across the sky and Aharah followed them east-southeast, toward the coordinates Auntie had pressed into her palm at the gate.

The first days were not hard. Her body was still adjusting to the Lyonessa gene — the new density of her bones, the altered rhythm of her heart, the way her muscles recovered from effort faster than her mind could register fatigue. She moved quickly across the hardpan where the sand was thin and the stone underneath provided footing. The sunskin worked, mostly. The photosynthetic pigment drank the twin suns' light and converted it to energy, a warmth that spread from her shoulders to her core and staved off the hunger that would have otherwise hollowed her within days. But the ordeal had damaged the pigment in patches. Along her left side, from hip to shoulder, the sunskin was mottled — dark gold shading to brown, the cells scarred, their efficiency halved. She could feel the difference: a cold stripe down her left flank even at midday, as if that side of her body had been eclipsed.

She rationed the water. Eight days of supply for forty-four days of travel. The math was execution and she knew it. But Auntie had told her about the salt flats on day ten, the oasis at day twenty-two, the seasonal rain that sometimes fell in the desert's eastern reach during late Kaph-month. Sometimes. Aharah did not build plans on sometimes. She built them on what her feet could confirm.

Walk. Rest. Walk. The rhythm of endurance. The simplest equation: distance divided by time, the body as vehicle, the mind as passenger. She had trained for this on the palace walls — not this specifically, but the principle. The refusal to stop. The conversion of pain into information and information into movement.

The sword was useful in ways she had not anticipated. On the fourth morning she reached the first of the great dune fields — sand piled hundreds of cubits high in sinuous ridges by winds that blew from the north in patterns no one charted anymore — and discovered that the blade, laid flat against the sand, made a surface she could ride. She stood on the steel, one foot forward, one back, her weight centered, and the dune's leeward slope did the rest. She surfed the red sand in long diagonal runs, the sword's edge cutting a hissing line in the surface, the wind filling her training wrap like a sail. It was absurd. It was effective. It saved her legs and she did not care about the indignity.

---

Day twelve.

The water ran out.

She had known it would. She had drunk carefully — measured sips at dawn and dusk, nothing at midday when the body sweated faster than the stomach could absorb. The canteen emptied on the morning of the twelfth day, the last mouthful warm and metallic, and she hung it on her belt and kept walking.

The salt flat appeared in the afternoon. A vast white expanse, flat as a blade, the salt crusted in hexagonal tiles that crunched under her sandals. The light off the surface was blinding. She wrapped her face in the torn hem of her training wrap and navigated by the feel of the sun on her right shoulder.

The body began its argument. First the throat: a constriction, a tightening, as if the muscles were trying to swallow something that wasn't there. Then the head: a pressure behind the eyes that blurred the horizon. Then the thoughts, which came loose from their moorings and drifted.

She saw Nala. Not Nala as she was — shorn, enlisted, furious — but Nala as she had been, years ago, when Aharah was small enough to ride on her sister's hip. Nala's face above her, framed by black hair that fell like water, her voice low and warm, singing a song in old Shakyan that Aharah could never remember the words to but whose melody lived in her body like a second pulse. The memory was so vivid that Aharah reached for it — actually reached, her hand rising from her side — and grasped hot air.

She walked faster. The salt flat ended. The red sand resumed. She found a boulder at dusk and pressed her back against it and did not sleep. The twin moons rose. The orabaun crossed the sky, pearl by pearl, their light cold and indifferent.

She licked the dew from the boulder's surface at dawn. A mouthful. Less. Enough to make the throat remember what water was and want more.

---

Day eighteen.

The desert changed. The flat red sand gave way to terrain that rose and fell in sharp ridges, the stone beneath breaking through the surface in dark eruptions. Vermilia had been here once — a nation, a kingdom, one of the four that had divided the continent before the Haz King brought his war. Auntie had told the story: Vermilia refused to kneel. The Haz King turned their rivers to sand. The nation sank. Its people scattered. Its cities were buried so deep that only the tallest spires still broke the surface, and those had been scoured by centuries of wind into shapes that looked natural if you didn't know what to look for.

Aharah knew what to look for. She found a spire — dark stone, squared at the base, tapering to a blunted point — and dug at its base with her sword. Two feet down, she hit tile. Three feet down, she hit pipe. The pipe was cracked, but water — brackish, faintly green, tasting of minerals and time — seeped through the fracture. She lay on her stomach and pressed her mouth to the crack and drank.

She drank until her stomach cramped. Then she filled the canteen with what seeped through and walked on.

---

The nights were when the desert spoke.

Not in words. In sound. The dunes shifted after sundown, the sand grains resettling as the temperature dropped, and the shifting produced a low, sustained moan that traveled for miles. It was the sound of the desert breathing. The sound of something vast rearranging itself in sleep. Aharah lay on the cooling sand and listened and felt the sound in her sternum, a vibration that matched the pulse of the gem-key in her wrist.

Other sounds, too. The click of manoks — armored insects the length of her forearm, their mandibles tapping against stone as they emerged from their burrows to hunt. They were not dangerous unless threatened. Aharah was careful not to threaten them. She lay still and they moved around her like water around a rock, their carapaces catching the moonlight in flashes of iridescent green.

And once, on the twenty-third night, the sound of something larger. A disturbance in the sand to the north — rhythmic, heavy, the displacement of something massive moving below the surface. A hydra. She had never seen one. Auntie said they could grow to thirty cubits, their bodies segmented, their heads crowned with sensory spines that detected vibration across hundreds of meters. Aharah lay flat and breathed into the sand and willed her heartbeat to slow. The disturbance passed. The sand resettled. She did not sleep the rest of that night.

---

Day thirty. The smell of sulfur.

It came from the east — a sharp, acrid tang that the wind carried like a warning. The vyvyan beetles, Auntie had said. In winter they bred in the billions, their bodies producing a fine powder that settled on the sand like yellow frost. The powder was toxic: inhaled in quantity, it scarred the lungs; absorbed through the skin, it caused lesions that wept and would not heal. Aharah wrapped her face tighter. She adjusted her course south, skirting the sulfur bloom.

The detour cost her three days. She did not count the cost.

Her body had changed. She noticed it in the way she noticed weather — not all at once, but gradually, the accumulation of small differences that resolved into a new condition. Her hands were harder. The skin across her knuckles had thickened, the sunskin pigment concentrating there as if her body had decided that her hands were the part of her most likely to strike something. Her vision was sharper, particularly at night — the Lyonessa gene enhancing the retina's sensitivity, turning the dark desert into a landscape of grey gradients that she navigated with an ease that would have been impossible a month ago. And the healing. She had cut her foot on a salt crystal on day fifteen. By day seventeen, the cut was gone. Not scarred. Gone.

She was becoming something. She was twelve years old and she was becoming something, and the something had no template, because no twelve-year-old had undergone the ordeal in the history of Maya, and whatever the Lyonessa gene was doing to her adolescent body was improvisation — the sequence adapting to architecture it had not been designed for, building new structures in the gaps.

She did not think about this. Thinking about it was a luxury the desert did not offer. She walked.

---

Day thirty-eight.

The storm announced itself at dawn: a darkening on the northern horizon, a smudge of ochre against the white sky that spread as she watched. The wind shifted. The air grew heavy with static, the fine hairs on her arms rising, the sunskin prickling. She had heard of Vermillion storms — the old soldiers in Maya spoke of them with the quiet deference reserved for things that could not be fought. A wall of sand, they said. Moving at the speed of a galloping horse. It stripped flesh from bone if you were caught standing.

She could not outrun it. She looked for shelter. The terrain was open — low ridges, no caves, no buried spires. She dug. The sword was poor for digging but she used it anyway, carving a trench in the sand between two ridges and pressing herself into it face-down. She pulled the loose sand over her body. She wrapped the training cloth around her head until only a slit remained for breath.

The storm hit.

Sound first — a roar that began low and climbed until it occupied every frequency, until it was not a sound but a condition, the air itself vibrating. Then the sand. It struck her exposed back in waves — not grains but sheets, horizontal, abrasive, each impact a thousand tiny incisions. The training wrap tore. The sand found skin. It found the mottled sunskin along her left side and scoured it, and the pain was vivid and specific — the feeling of being erased, of the desert trying to take back the material it was made of.

She pressed her face into the trench and breathed through the cloth. The sand filled the space around her. She felt it accumulate — on her back, her legs, her arms — felt herself being buried. She kept breathing. The storm would pass. Storms passed. This was not conviction but repetition: storms passed, storms passed, storms passed, the words a rhythm to breathe by, a structure to hang consciousness on while the world outside tried to dissolve.

It passed.

She dug herself out at dusk. The desert had been rearranged — the ridges flattened, new dunes formed, the familiar landscape replaced by something alien. Her back was raw. The training wrap hung in strips. She stood and the sand fell from her in a red cascade and the twin moons rose on a desert she did not recognize.

She found east by the orabaun. She walked.

---

Day forty-four.

Rain.

She smelled it before she felt it — a change in the air, the sharp mineral tang of water on hot stone, a scent so vivid after weeks of dust that it stopped her in her tracks. She stood on the crest of a dune and looked up. The sky, white for weeks, had darkened to a bruised grey-violet at its eastern edge. Cloud. Real cloud, not dust haze. Cloud heavy with water, its underbelly lit by the last light of the setting suns.

The first drop struck her forehead. Warm. Fat. It ran down the bridge of her nose and she caught it on her tongue. Salt. Mineral. Alive.

The rain came. Not a storm — a falling, a slow and steady release, as if the sky had been holding its breath for months and could hold it no longer. The drops darkened the red sand in spots that spread and merged. The air cooled. Steam rose from the dunes in thin white columns. Aharah tipped her head back and opened her mouth and stood on the crest of the dune and drank the sky.

The sunskin responded. Even the damaged cells along her left side flickered — faintly, uncertainly, like embers stirred by wind. The water ran down her body and the pigment drank it along with the light and she felt, for the first time in forty-four days, something that was not endurance.

She stood in the rain until the clouds passed and the stars returned and the orabaun resumed their slow procession across the dark. Then she descended the dune's leeward slope.

The Tagata Rock was there.

---

It sat in the open desert like a fist pushed through the sand from below. Massive — twenty cubits high, roughly oval, its surface dark and smooth where the Vermillion sand had polished it over centuries. It was not native stone. Even in the moonlight, Aharah could see that: the color was wrong, too dark, almost black, and the texture too uniform. It had been placed here, or it had fallen here, or it had grown here by a process that had nothing to do with geology.

On its face, at the height of a tall woman's reach, a palm print.

Not carved. Not painted. Pressed. As if someone had placed their hand against the stone when it was soft and the stone had hardened around the impression, preserving every line, every whorl, the geometry of a hand that might have belonged to anyone.

The sign of the Tagata.

Aharah approached. She placed her own hand against the print. It was larger than hers — wider at the palm, longer at the fingers. The stone was cool. The gem-key at her wrist pulsed faster, as if recognizing something, and a faint warmth spread from the print into her palm, through her wrist, up her arm. Not painful. Interrogative. The stone was asking a question she did not know how to answer.

She pressed harder. Nothing changed. The rock stood. The print remained. The desert stretched in every direction, red and vast and empty, and whatever the Tagata was — being, state, enlightenment — it was not here. The rock was not a destination. It was a marker. A sign that said: *this is where the road begins.*

She had walked forty-four days to reach the beginning.

She laughed. It came out strange — dry, cracked, not quite a sound her throat remembered how to make. She leaned her forehead against the cool stone and closed her eyes and laughed, and the laughter became something else — not weeping, not relief, but a vibration in her chest that matched the hum of the gem-key and the low moan of the dunes and the pulse of the orabaun overhead, all of it one frequency, one note, the desert and the sky and the stone and the girl pressed together at a single point.

She slid down the rock's face. Her back against the stone. Her legs drawn up. The sword across her knees. The caster on her forearm, dormant, its power cell at twelve percent. She was out of water again. She was out of food. She was out of desert, too — there was nowhere farther to walk, no next coordinate, no Auntie-given instruction to follow. The ordeal had sent her here and here was a rock in the sand with a handprint on its face and nothing else.

She closed her eyes. The exhaustion she had been holding at arm's length for forty-four days arrived all at once, like a guest who had been waiting patiently at the door. It entered her body through her feet and rose — through the aching calves, the hard-won muscle of her thighs, the raw and scoured skin of her back, the dense new bones that the Lyonessa gene had built — and it reached her mind and turned off the lights.

She slept.

---

The footsteps woke her.

Not the click of manoks. Not the subsurface displacement of a hydra. Footsteps — bipedal, deliberate, the crunch of sand under weight. Human or near-human. Close.

Aharah opened her eyes. The twin suns were rising — Aleph first, white and blinding, then Beth, following with its red warmth. The desert was still. The rain had dried. The sand was undisturbed in every direction except one.

A figure approached from the east.

Tall. Lean. Moving with the unhurried gait of someone who belonged to the desert in the way that the dunes belonged to it — not visiting but native, not enduring but inhabiting. The figure wore wrappings the color of the sand, stained dark at the hems. A staff in one hand, taller than its carrier, its tip shod in metal that caught the sunlight. A face she could not make out against the glare.

Aharah's hand found her sword. Her fingers closed on the grip. She did not stand — her legs would not yet agree to it — but she straightened against the rock and watched.

The figure stopped ten paces away. The staff planted. The wrappings shifted in the wind. And then a voice — low, rough, accented in a dialect Aharah did not recognize, Shakyan with sand in its vowels:

"Forty-four days. Not bad for a child."

Aharah's grip tightened on the sword. "Who are you?"

The figure crouched. At this distance, the face resolved: weathered, dark, the sunskin a deep umber that suggested a lifetime under the twin suns. Eyes the color of the desert stone — not brown but red-black, like hematite. A scar along the jaw, old and silver. The expression was not hostile. It was not friendly. It was the expression of someone examining a thing that had washed up on their shore.

"Dzuvag," the figure said. "And you are the girl who stole an ordeal."

Aharah did not lower the sword. "How do you know that?"

Dzuvag looked at the gem-key pulsing at her wrist. Then at the Tagata Rock behind her, and the palm print, and the place where Aharah's hand had rested against it. A faint expression crossed the weathered face — not surprise, not recognition, but something adjacent to both. Interest.

"Because the rock told me," Dzuvag said. "It's been waiting. I wasn't sure for what." The hematite eyes returned to Aharah. "Now I am."

The twin suns climbed. The desert blazed. And Aharah sat against the Tagata Rock with her sword across her knees and looked at the stranger who had come out of the red sand, and understood — not with her mind but with her body, with the gem-key and the altered bones and the sunskin that had carried her through forty-four days of nothing — that the journey she had thought was ending was only now beginning.
