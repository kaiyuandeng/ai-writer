# Chapter 21: The Three Sisters

The arm remembered itself at night.

Nahlah stood on the coastal rampart with the sea below and the wind coming in cold from the east and the phantom limb reaching for the railing. It reached every night. The nerves that had once connected to the muscles that had once connected to the bones that had once been her left arm still fired their signals, still sent their instructions to an architecture that was no longer there, and the result was a reaching — a flexing of nothing, a gripping of air, the ghost of a hand closing on the ghost of a rail.

The physicians said it would fade. The physicians had been saying this for two years.

She let the phantom reach. She had learned not to fight it. Fighting the phantom limb was like fighting the memory of the arm — possible, exhausting, and ultimately an argument with a part of herself that would not concede the point.

Below her, the coastal defenses. She had built them. Three tiers of fortification: the sea-wall at the waterline, barnacled and heavy, its stones fitted without mortar in the old Shakyan method that flexed with the tides instead of resisting them. Behind the sea-wall, the gun emplacements — casters mounted on swivels, their power cells at forty percent (always forty percent, never more; the cells did not recharge and could not be replaced, and every shot was an expenditure that could not be recovered). Behind the guns, the trench line, where the infantry would hold if the walls fell and the guns ran dry.

She inspected them each night. Alone. The soldiers knew not to follow. The General's nightly walk was not a patrol — it was a conversation, conducted in silence, between a woman and the defensive line that was the only thing standing between the city and the sea.

The sea was where the fishfolk lived.

Not fishfolk — the soldiers' name, contemptuous, imprecise. The Marin. A species that had evolved in the ocean trenches of Hassan's southern hemisphere and that the continent's human population had been fighting, in sporadic and inconclusive engagements, since before the founding of the Haz King's empire. They were amphibious. They were intelligent. They bred in cycles: a decade of dormancy, then a spawning season that produced millions, and the millions needed land — coastal land, river land, the strip of habitable territory where the human cities sat.

The spawning season was approaching. Nahlah could feel it in the water's behavior — a change in the current patterns, a turbidity that hadn't been there a month ago, the disappearance of the shore-fish that the herons hunted. The Marin's advance scouts had been sighted at the river mouth. Small parties, probing, testing the defenses' response time. Nahlah had ordered the auto-drones — the last functioning ones, a squadron of twelve, their AI degraded to simple pattern-recognition — to map the underwater terrain beyond the sea-wall.

The maps were not encouraging. The Marin were massing in the deep channels. Thousands. Tens of thousands. The spawning would bring hundreds of thousands more.

Maya had four hundred soldiers. Twelve casters on the wall. A barrier that hid the city from land approaches but did nothing to conceal it from the sea. And now, returned from the desert without victory, the Lyonessa. Her sister. The thief.

Nahlah's phantom hand clenched.

She remembered the morning. Not this morning — the morning. The morning she reached for the badge and the table was empty and she went to the window and saw Aharah climbing the Temple steps, twelve years old, her sunskin mottled from the ordeal, the gem-key already pulsing at her wrist. The morning the world rearranged itself around an absence.

She had not screamed. She had not wept. She had cut her hair. She had enlisted. She had given the military her fury and the military had given her a structure to contain it, and the structure had held for years — through the training, through the promotions, through the battle at the Hupasa crossing where the Marin advance guard had come in force and Nahlah had held the bridge for six hours with thirty soldiers and had lost the arm in the sixth hour when a Marin pike punched through her guard and severed the limb above the elbow with the surgical precision of a species that understood joints.

She had cauterized the wound herself. She had finished the battle. She had held the bridge.

And now Aharah was back, and the fury that the structure contained pressed against its walls.

Nahlah looked at the sea. The water was dark. The twin moons threw a double track of light across the surface — Rho's track pale and wide, Kaph's narrow and sharp. The wind carried salt and the distant, low-frequency sound that might have been waves and might have been something moving in the deep channels.

"Let her fight," Nahlah said, to no one. To the sea. To the phantom arm that was still reaching for the railing.

She turned and walked back along the rampart, her golden armor catching the moonlight, the empty sleeve moving in the wind like a banner that had lost its pole.

---

The Oracle's chamber was beneath the Temple.

Not the ordeal chamber — that was deeper, sealed since the night Aharah had stolen the treatment. The Oracle's chamber was on the first subterranean level, accessible by a stairway of white stone that descended from the Hall of Names. The walls were the same stone as the hall above — pale, smooth, inscribed with script so old it had worn to texture. The air was cool and dry and it smelled of the machines that lined the chamber's perimeter: ozone, copper, the particular sterile tang of technology operating in a space designed for it.

The Oracle sat at the chamber's center.

She was old. Old beyond the count that Maya kept — her sunskin faded to a pale umber, her hands spotted with the dark marks of cells that had stopped regenerating, her body wrapped in the Temple's white robes that hung on her frame the way cloth hangs on a rack. Her eyes were the color of sand after rain: pale, translucent, depthless. She had been Oracle for sixty years. Before that, she had been the Oracle's apprentice. Before that, she had been a girl whose name the Temple had replaced with a title.

She sat on a chair of dark wood, the only furniture in the chamber. The machines hummed around her. Beneath the chair, cables ran from the floor into the walls, connecting the Oracle's seat to the Temple's systems — the same systems that operated the ordeal chamber, that maintained the Maya barrier, that communicated with the orabaun satellites in their slow procession overhead. The cables were not attached to the chair. They were attached to the Oracle. They entered her body at the base of the spine, threading through the robes, merging with the flesh at connection points that were neither surgical nor natural but something older — a hybridization of human and machine that predated the Oracle's memory and perhaps the Temple's records.

Aharah stood before her. The gem-key at her wrist pulsed. The Oracle's machines registered the pulse — a series of faint clicks along the chamber's walls, instruments noting the Lyonessa frequency, cataloging its strength, comparing it to the records of every Lyonessa who had stood in this chamber before.

"You were told not to return," the Oracle said. Her voice was dry. Not weak — dry. The voice of a throat that had spoken the same formal phrases for six decades and had worn smooth the way the Temple script had worn smooth, the meaning preserved in the shape even as the edges dissolved. "The vow is clear. Return without the Tagata and the penalty is death."

"I know the vow."

"Then you know why you're here."

"I'm here because the city is going to burn."

The Oracle's pale eyes regarded her. The machines clicked. The cables beneath the chair pulsed — a faint, rhythmic movement, the Temple's systems processing the exchange.

"Explain," the Oracle said.

"I had a vision. In the Vermillion. The Maya barrier falls. The city burns. Nahlah dies on the Temple steps. Something — insects, a horde, a king — comes through the gap." Aharah paused. The gem-key buzzed at her wrist, the frequency rising as if the device were corroborating her account. "Anais stands in the fire."

"Visions are not prophecy."

"Anais's visions are."

The Oracle was quiet. The machines clicked. The cables pulsed. Somewhere above them, the Temple's gem caught the morning sun and split it into colors, and the colors filtered down through the stone and touched the chamber's walls in faint, shifting patterns.

"The child has the sight," the Oracle said. Not a question.

"She dreams things that happen. The blue planet. The burning city. She told me the city would burn before I left for the Vermillion. I dreamed it while I was there."

"And you returned to warn us."

"Yes."

"Knowing the penalty."

"Yes."

The Oracle leaned forward. The cables at her spine shifted — a sound like silk dragging across stone. "The Tagata was your sentence and your salvation, child. Without it, you are a thief. A transgressor. The Temple's law is clear."

"Then delay the law." Aharah met the pale eyes. "The Marin are spawning. The fishfolk are coming. Nahlah knows it — the defenses are being reinforced. You need every fighter at the front. Let me fight. Delay the execution one season. If I die in battle, the debt is paid."

"And if you survive?"

"Then execute me after."

The Oracle studied her. The pale eyes moved across Aharah's face with the unhurried thoroughness of a machine reading code — which was, in a sense, exactly what was happening. The Temple's systems analyzed the Lyonessa gene's expression in Aharah's body, the sunskin's regeneration, the chain-sickle's weight on her hip, the hard new lines of a body the desert had rebuilt.

"One season," the Oracle said. "You will fight at the front. You will submit to General Nahlah's command. If you survive, you will return here and the sentence will be carried out."

"Agreed."

"Go."

Aharah turned. She climbed the white stone stairs. Behind her, the Oracle sat among her machines and the cables pulsed and the Temple's systems processed the exchange and filed it in whatever archive recorded the conversations of Oracles and thieves.

---

The garden was the only place in the Jian household that did not smell of stone.

It occupied the courtyard's eastern corner — a rectangle of cultivated earth behind a low wall, shaded by a fig tree whose canopy had been shaped by decades of Auntie's pruning into a broad, flat umbrella. The tree was old. Its trunk was grey and its bark was fissured and its roots had cracked the paving stones at the courtyard's edge. In autumn, the leaves turned copper and fell in slow spirals that collected on the stone bench beneath the canopy.

Aharah sat on the bench. The leaves were falling. A warm afternoon, the twin suns past their zenith, the light filtering through the fig canopy in coins of gold that shifted as the wind moved the branches. She had bathed. She had eaten. She had slept for fourteen hours in her old bed in the sisters' quarters, and when she woke, the room was the same — the stone walls, the narrow window, the shelf where Auntie had arranged her childhood things — and she was not.

The gem-key pulsed at her wrist. She turned her forearm and looked at it: a thumbnail-sized gem, embedded in the skin, its blue-white light visible through the flesh. The skin around it was smooth — not scarred, not raised, but integrated, the body having accepted the foreign object the way a tree accepts a nail, growing around it, incorporating it. The gem-key was part of her now. It monitored the Lyonessa gene's activity. It connected her to the Temple's systems. It counted down to the sentence the Oracle had imposed.

One season. Then death.

She was looking at the gem-key when Anais sat beside her.

Anais had changed too, though the changes were subtler than Aharah's. Taller — a full hand taller than when Aharah had left, the growth of months compressing into the startling difference between the child she'd said goodbye to and the child who sat beside her now. Her face was thinner. Her eyes were the same: dark, large, watchful, the eyes of someone who saw more than she could say and said less than she saw.

"Show me," Anais said.

Aharah held out her wrist. Anais took it in both hands and turned it, studying the gem-key the way she studied everything — with the focused, unblinking attention that adults mistook for rudeness and that was, in fact, the opposite. She was being thorough. She was giving the thing before her the respect of her full perception.

"Does it hurt?"

"Not anymore."

"It's counting."

Aharah looked at her. "You can see that?"

"I can feel it. It's like a clock that runs on your heartbeat." Anais pressed her thumb against the gem. Its pulse quickened — a brief flare, the light brightening beneath the skin. "How long?"

"One season. I'm going to the front. To fight the Marin."

"And after?"

Aharah did not answer.

Anais released her wrist. She sat back on the bench. A leaf detached from the fig tree and drifted between them, turning copper-gold in the afternoon light, its descent so slow it seemed to be making a decision.

"You took the ordeal for Nala," Anais said. Her voice was even. Too even — the deliberate calm of someone constructing a sentence out of materials that were trying to explode. "You walked forty-four days through the desert for the Tagata. You came back to save us from the vision. And now you're going to the front to die in battle so the Oracle doesn't execute you." She paused. "Do you see the pattern?"

"There's no pattern."

"The pattern is you. You take other people's pain and you carry it. You took Nala's ordeal because you couldn't watch her die. You're going to the front because you can't watch the city burn. You carry things. That's what you do. And the things you carry are going to kill you."

Aharah was quiet. The fig tree's shadow moved on the paving stones. In the courtyard beyond the garden wall, the fountain sang. Somewhere in the household, Auntie's joints clicked through their rounds.

"What would you have me do?" Aharah said. "Not fight?"

"I would have you stop pretending you're alone." Anais turned to face her. The watchful eyes were not watchful now. They were bright. Furious. The fury of an eight-year-old who had spent forty-three mornings on a stone bench waiting for a sister who might never come back and who was now, having come back, preparing to leave again. "You took the Lyonessa for Nala's sake. Now you're going to the front for my sake. For the city's sake. For everyone's sake except your own. When does it become about you?"

"It's not about me. It's about all of us."

"That's what people say when they don't want to admit they're scared."

The leaf landed on the bench between them. Copper-gold. Still.

Aharah looked at her sister. The youngest. The strange one. The one who dreamed of blue planets and burning cities and who spoke with a directness that cut through the adult language of duty and sacrifice and found the thing beneath — not courage, not selflessness, but fear. The fear of a girl who had been given a sentence and who was running toward the sword because running was easier than standing still.

"I'm going to the front," Aharah said. "That's decided."

"Then I'm coming with you."

"No."

"I've been training." Anais stood. She stood the way she did everything: precisely, deliberately, her body composing itself into a posture that was too old for her frame. "For as long as you have. For as long as Nala has. Auntie taught me. The automatons taught me. I can read the field arrays, I can plot the caster trajectories, I can interface with the defense systems through the Temple network. I've been doing it while you were in the desert and Nala was on the wall. Somebody had to."

"You're eight."

"You were twelve when you stole the ordeal. Nala was fifteen when she lost her arm." The fury in her eyes did not diminish. It refined itself — burning hotter, cleaner, the way beetle-husk fuel burned when you fed it air. "The three of us have been fighting since before we knew what we were fighting. I'm not letting you go alone again. Not you. Not Nala. It's time we fought together."

Aharah stared at her. The sun moved through the fig canopy. The leaf sat on the bench between them, copper-gold, motionless, balanced on its stem.

"You can't fight," Aharah said. "Not on the line."

"I don't need to be on the line. I need to be where I can see. The sight — the dreams, the visions — they're not random. They're data. I can read the battle the way I read the dreams. I can tell you where they're coming from before they arrive."

"You're asking me to bring my eight-year-old sister into a war."

"I'm telling you that if you go without me, you'll die. I've seen it." She paused. The fury banked. What replaced it was worse — certainty, the flat, mineral certainty of a child who had looked at the future and found it legible. "I've seen you die on the seawall without me there. I've seen Nala die on the Temple steps without me there. The only version where we survive is the one where we're together. All three of us."

The garden was quiet. The fountain sang. The fig tree's leaves turned in the wind.

Aharah looked at the gem-key in her wrist. One season. The light pulsed, steady, counting.

"Together," she said.

"Together."

The word hung between them. Not an agreement — a covenant, forged on a stone bench in a garden beneath a fig tree in a city that did not know it was going to burn.

Anais turned and walked toward the garden wall. She did not look back. Her footsteps on the paving stones were light and precise and they carried the specific weight of a person who had said the thing she came to say and was finished.

Aharah sat on the bench and watched her go.

---

The household settled into its evening rhythms. Auntie moved through the rooms with her cloth and her oil, tending surfaces, checking mechanisms, performing the maintenance rituals that had outlasted the civilization that designed them. The Hazzan was in his study, the door closed, the sound of his prayers filtering through the stone — rhythmic, nasal, the old formal Shakyan that no one spoke conversationally anymore. The fountain sang. The automatons stood at their posts.

Aharah lay in her bed. The room was dark. The gem-key's light was a blue-white star at her wrist, pulsing against the ceiling in slow, regular flares. She listened to the city's night sounds — the sentries calling the change, the distant splash of the fountains, the wind in the cypress — and she thought about the front. The seawall. The Marin massing in the deep channels. The one season that was both reprieve and countdown.

She did not think about the execution. She had placed it in the same locked room where she kept the stolen badge, the mottled sunskin, the sound of Nala's silence at the market — the room in her mind where the things that could not be carried were stored until they could.

Sleep came. It came slowly, circling, approaching the way the desert hydra approached — from the periphery, from the side, avoiding the direct path. Her body relaxed joint by joint, the Lyonessa gene monitoring the descent, the sunskin dimming as the photosynthetic cells entered their dormant cycle.

The dream found her.

Not fire this time. Not the burning city, not the locust king, not Nahlah on the Temple steps. This dream was different. This dream was Anais.

She stood in a place that was not a place — a darkness so total it had texture, a void that pressed against her skin like velvet. And in the darkness, a light. A single point, yellow, impossibly far, burning with the steady patience of a thing that had been burning for billions of years and would burn for billions more.

A star.

Anais was beside her, or below her, or inside her — the dream's geometry did not obey the rules of bodies in space. Anais was everywhere and she was pointing at the star, and her voice was not a child's voice but the voice of the thing the child would become, the voice of the sight stripped of its human container, pure and vast and calibrated to frequencies that the waking ear could not hold.

*Look.*

The star resolved. Not a point but a system — a yellow star orbited by bodies, each one tracing its ellipse in the dark. Eight planets. She counted them. The inner four were small and dense. The outer four were vast and pale. And the third one — the third from the yellow star — was blue.

Blue like an eye. Blue like grief. Blue like water and gas and light refracting through an atmosphere so thick it looked edible. The planet she had heard Anais describe a hundred times, the planet of the dream, the world of metal boxes and black rectangles and people who spoke but never listened.

*The coordinates,* Anais's voice said. *Three. Twenty-five. Fifteen. Twenty-one. Twenty-three. One.*

Aharah did not understand. The numbers fell through the dream like stones through water, their meaning sinking beyond her reach. She grasped at them. They passed through her fingers.

*Not yet,* Anais said. *But soon.*

The dream dissolved. The star faded. The darkness pressed in and then it too was gone, replaced by the bedroom ceiling and the gem-key's pulse and the sound of the fountain singing its idiot song and the warm stone smell of Maya at night.

Aharah lay in the dark with the numbers fading from her mind — already losing them, the way dreams lose their edges at waking, the specifics dissolving into a residue of feeling. She could not hold the coordinates. She could not hold the image of the blue planet or the yellow star or the eight bodies in their slow procession. But she held the feeling — the vast, aching sense of distance, of something impossibly far that was also, somehow, the thing they had been looking for.

The thing they had always been looking for.

In her room across the hall, Anais slept. Her face was calm. Her hands were open on the blanket. The dream she had sent was gone from her, dispatched like a letter, its contents no longer hers to read. She slept the deep, unpunctured sleep of a child who had done her work for the day, and the twin moons cast their light through her window and touched her face, and the light was silver, and the silver was gentle, and the night held her the way the city held all of them — carefully, provisionally, with the knowledge that what was held could also be lost.

The three sisters slept.

The city of Maya turned beneath its moons and its barrier and its beautiful lie, and the sea moved in its channels, and the Marin bred in the deep, and the season counted down.
