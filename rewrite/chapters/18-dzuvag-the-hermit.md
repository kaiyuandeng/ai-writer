# Chapter 18: Dzuvag the Hermit

She weighed nothing.

Dzuvag knew this before he lifted her — knew it by the way the sand had not compressed beneath her body, by the shallow dent where her hip had rested against the Tagata Rock's base, by the fine red dust that had already begun to settle in the hollows of her collarbones. A body that weighed something displaced the world around it. This one had stopped displacing anything.

He crouched beside her. The twin suns were still low, Aleph painting the eastern dunes in white fire while Beth hung behind it like a coal. In this light, the girl's sunskin should have blazed. It didn't. The pigment had gone matte — a dull gold, tarnished, like a coin left in vinegar. Along her left side, from hip to shoulder, the sunskin was dead entirely: brown, scarred, the cells burned out. Her hair had gone brittle and grey at the ends, though the roots were still dark. Her ribs pressed against the training wrap like fingers under cloth.

Twelve years old. Maybe thirteen. Hard to tell with the Lyonessa gene rewriting the architecture. But young. Too young for what the desert had done to her.

Dzuvag slid one arm beneath her shoulders and the other beneath her knees and stood. She weighed nothing. He had carried manok carcasses heavier than this. Her head fell against his chest and the gem-key at her wrist pulsed — faint, irregular, the rhythm of a heart that had forgotten its meter.

He carried her east, toward the cave.

---

The cave was not a cave. It was a Vermillian cellar — the lowest chamber of a building that no longer existed above it, its walls of fired brick, its ceiling a barrel vault that had survived the burial of an entire civilization beneath the sand. Dzuvag had found it thirty years ago. He had cleared the sand from the entrance, shored the walls with scavenged timbers, lined the floor with woven grass mats. He had brought in water from the seep-spring a quarter mile north. He had hung dried manok meat from the ceiling beams and stacked fuel — dried brush, beetle husks, the desiccated roots of plants that burned slow and hot — in a corner.

It was not comfort. It was adequacy. Dzuvag did not believe in comfort. Comfort was a negotiation with the future, a bet that things would stay as they were. The desert made no such promises.

He laid the girl on the sleeping mat. She did not stir. He placed the back of his hand against her forehead: hot, dry, the skin papery. Dehydration beyond the point where the body sweated. The sunskin had kept her alive longer than she should have lasted — the photosynthetic cells converting sunlight to energy, feeding the blood sugar, maintaining organ function — but even the Lyonessa gene had limits. The cells along her left flank were dead. The rest were failing.

He went to the back of the cellar where he kept the beetles.

The vyvyan beetles lived in a clay jar sealed with wax. Summer beetles — he had collected them in the warm months, when their carapaces were green-gold and their bodies produced the healing paste instead of the killing powder. The distinction was survival itself: summer vyvyan healed burns, closed wounds, reversed the cellular damage of sun exposure. Winter vyvyan produced a fine yellow powder that scarred lungs and ate through skin like acid. The same creature. The same species. The season turned them, the way the season turned the desert from passable to lethal.

He crushed three beetles in a stone mortar. Their bodies cracked with a sound like knuckles popping and released a thick paste — translucent, faintly luminous, the color of new leaves. The smell hit him: eucalyptus and copper, sharp and medicinal, cutting through the cellar's dry-earth funk. He added water from the seep-spring. He worked the paste with the pestle until it was smooth.

The girl's training wrap came away in strips. Beneath it, the damage was thorough. Sunburn layered over sunburn, the skin cracked and peeling in sheets, the mottled sunskin along her left side raw and weeping where the sandstorm had scoured it. Her shoulder blades were sharp enough to cast shadows. The muscle between her ribs had wasted to cordage.

He applied the paste. Along the burns first — long strokes, the paste glowing faintly where it met damaged skin, the luminescence tracking the extent of the injury like a map drawn in light. Then the scoured sunskin on her left flank, where the paste pooled in the cracks and settled with a faint hiss, the sound of chemistry meeting biology. Then her hands, which were blistered and split at the knuckles.

The paste worked fast. Within minutes, the raw edges of the burns began to close — not healing exactly, but stabilizing, the cells remembering their shape. The faint glow dimmed as the paste was absorbed. The girl's breathing changed: deeper, slower, the rhythm of genuine sleep replacing the shallow panting of a body conserving its last reserves.

Dzuvag sat back on his heels and watched her breathe.

He had not spoken to another person in eleven years.

---

She came awake swinging.

Not the slow surfacing of ordinary waking — the explosive, violent emergence of a body that had learned to treat consciousness as combat. Her eyes opened and her hand found the sword that was no longer at her side and when it wasn't there she swung with what she had: a fist, tight and fast, aimed at his jaw.

Dzuvag caught her wrist. One hand. He didn't move from his seated position. He held her fist in the air between them the way a man holds a moth — loosely, without urgency, careful not to damage the thing.

She snarled. She twisted. She threw her other fist and he caught that too, and now he held both her wrists and she was half off the mat, her body trembling with the effort of fighting through the weakness, her matte-gold sunskin flushing darker with adrenaline.

"Let go of me."

"No."

She kicked. He shifted his knee and the kick struck the sleeping mat. She tried to bite. He extended his arms and held her at distance, her teeth snapping air.

"You have been unconscious for two days," Dzuvag said. "Your body has approximately the structural integrity of wet bread. If I release you, you will fall over. If you fall over, you will reopen the burns I spent considerable effort closing. I have a limited supply of beetle paste and an unlimited capacity for irritation. Choose wisely."

She stared at him. Her eyes were dark — not the hematite red-black of his, but a deep brown that the Lyonessa gene had ringed with amber. Furious. Lucid. The eyes of someone who had been fighting since before she could name the thing she fought.

"Who are you?" she said.

"I told you. Dzuvag."

"That's not an answer."

"It's a name. Names are the most honest answers there are." He released her wrists. She did not fall over, but the effort of staying upright cost her something visible — a tremor in her arms, a tightening at the corners of her mouth. "You, on the other hand, are the girl who collapsed against the Tagata Rock after walking forty-four days through the Vermillion without adequate water, without a guide, and without — I must note — any apparent sense of self-preservation. For which I am meant to be impressed."

"I didn't ask you to be impressed."

"Good. I'm not."

She looked around the cellar. The barrel-vaulted ceiling. The woven mats. The clay jar of beetles. The chain — coiled on a hook near the entrance, dark metal, its links interspersed with a weighted ball at one end and something curved and glinting at the other. Her eyes snagged on the chain and held.

"What is that?"

"Breakfast isn't ready yet," Dzuvag said, and stood. "You'll eat first. Then you'll ask questions."

---

She ate. Not gracefully — her hands shook around the bowl and she spilled broth down her chin and she chewed the dried manok strips with the single-minded ferocity of a body refueling — but she ate. Dzuvag watched from the entrance, where the morning light cut a diagonal across the cellar floor and the desert air carried the dry mineral smell of heated stone.

He was tall. Lean in the way the desert made everything lean — excess burned away, the body reduced to its essential architecture. His sunskin was deep umber, almost black, the pigment of someone who had spent decades under the twin suns without shelter. The scar along his jaw was old and silver and he did not touch it or favor it. His hands were large, the fingers long, the knuckles scarred in patterns that spoke of specific and repeated impact. He wore sand-colored wrappings that had been mended so many times the original fabric was indistinguishable from the patches.

Half-Hazzan, half-Vermillian. The blood of the conqueror and the conquered, mixed in a body the desert had simplified.

"How did you find me?" Aharah asked, between mouthfuls.

"The rock told me."

"Rocks don't talk."

"The Tagata Rock does. Not in words. In vibration. When someone touches the palm print, the stone resonates at a frequency that travels through the bedrock. I've been listening for it for thirty years." He paused. "You're the first to trigger it."

Aharah looked at the gem-key pulsing at her wrist. "The Oracle sent me to find the Tagata."

"And did you?"

"I found a rock."

"Ah." The faintest movement at the corner of his mouth. Not quite a smile. "Then you're ahead of most."

She set the bowl down. She looked at him with the directness that the desert had not yet burned out of her — the child's demand for truth that adulthood would teach her to soften. "Are you going to help me or not?"

"Help you do what?"

"Find the Tagata. Complete the vow. Go home."

Dzuvag was quiet for a long time. The light shifted on the cellar floor. A manok clicked past the entrance — its armored body catching the sun, iridescent green, its mandibles tapping stone.

"The Tagata is not a thing to be found," he said. "It's a state to be reached. No one can show you where it is because it doesn't have a where. It has a when. And the when is: after you've stopped looking."

"That's not helpful."

"No. It rarely is." He stood from his crouch and crossed to the hook where the chain hung. He lifted it down in one motion — the links cascading over his forearm, the weighted ball settling into his palm, the curved blade at the other end catching the light. "But this might be."

---

The chain-sickle was not a weapon Aharah recognized.

She had trained with blades — straight, curved, short, long. She had studied the caster, though she couldn't build one. She understood the geometry of striking: the arc, the edge, the relationship between mass and speed. But the chain-sickle belonged to a different geometry entirely.

The sickle itself was a curved blade, half the length of her forearm, its edge honed to a wire-thin line that the light slid off without catching. It was attached by a swivel joint to a chain — dark metal, each link forged tight, the chain perhaps three cubits long. At the chain's other end: a weighted ball, heavy, iron, its surface dimpled for grip.

Dzuvag held the ball in his right hand. He let the chain drape, the sickle resting against his left thigh. Then he moved.

The ball swung. The chain described an arc — not a simple circle but a figure-eight, the ball's weight pulling the chain taut, the links singing as they cut the air. The sickle rose at the chain's terminus and flew. It crossed ten paces of open desert, hooked around a dead thornbrush, and pulled. The bush tore from the sand. Dzuvag flicked his wrist and the chain reversed — the sickle unhooking, flying back, the ball catching the return momentum and settling into his palm as if it had never left.

The whole motion had taken two seconds. It had been silent except for the chain's hiss.

"A weapon of patience," Dzuvag said. "Not strength. The chain does the work. The ball provides the momentum. The sickle provides the intent. You do not swing it — you guide it. You do not aim it — you invite it. The chain-sickle does not cut what you tell it to cut. It cuts what you allow it to reach."

Aharah held out her hand. Dzuvag placed the ball in her palm. The weight was immediate and grounding — iron, dense, pulling her hand downward.

"Start with the ball," he said. "Learn its weight. Learn the chain's length. The sickle is the last thing you touch. The sickle is the conversation's final word."

She swung the ball. The chain whipped sideways and the sickle hit the sand at her feet.

Dzuvag's mouth did the not-quite-smile again. "Excellent. You've discovered gravity."

---

The days acquired rhythm.

Dawn: beetle paste on the healing burns, then water from the seep-spring, then movement. Dzuvag did not call it training. He called it learning to live. The desert, he said, was not an obstacle to be crossed but a system to be read. Everything in it was information. The angle of a dune's crest told you the wind's prevailing direction. The depth of a manok burrow told you the water table's level. The color of the sand — red, orange, dark ochre — told you what lay beneath: iron, calcium, the buried remnants of a civilization that had built with stone that stained the sand above it.

They hunted manoks. The armored insects were fast but predictable — they emerged at dusk from burrows they dug along the seep-lines, their mandibles clicking as they searched for the smaller beetles and larvae that lived in the sand's upper crust. The trick was not speed but position. You read the seep-lines. You found the burrow. You waited.

"Waiting is a skill," Dzuvag said. "Most people think patience is the absence of action. It's the opposite. Patience is action held in reserve. A coiled spring is patient. A sickle on a chain is patient. The moment before the strike contains the entire strike."

Aharah crouched beside him in the dusk light and watched a manok emerge. Its carapace was emerald, its legs articulated, its mandibles opening and closing with the mechanical regularity of a machine. She held the chain-sickle's ball in her right hand. The chain lay in loose coils on the sand.

"Not yet," Dzuvag said.

The manok turned. Exposed the gap between its head-plate and thorax — a stripe of pale chitin, unarmored.

"Now."

She swung. The ball pulled the chain taut. The sickle flew — not far, three paces — and struck the gap. The manok's legs folded. It was over before the sound of the chain's hiss faded from the air.

Dzuvag showed her how to dress it: remove the mandibles (tools, not food), crack the thorax (the meat was inside, dense and white, tasting of the minerals the manok ate), save the carapace (it held water when inverted, its chitin sealed against evaporation). Nothing was wasted. The desert did not permit waste.

"What's edible?" she asked, one evening, as they sat in the cellar eating manok meat roasted over a beetle-husk fire.

"Almost nothing. The manoks. The tubers that grow along the seep-lines — you'll know them by the grey leaves, low to the ground. The water from cracked Vermillian pipes, if you can find them. The dew that collects on stone at dawn."

"And what's toxic?"

"Almost everything else. The winter beetles. The thornbrush sap — it looks like water and it will stop your heart. The sand-kelp in the salt flats — you'll crave it when you're dehydrated, your body will tell you it's food. It isn't. It will dissolve the lining of your stomach in minutes." He tore a strip of manok meat with his teeth. "The desert is honest. It tells you exactly what it is. The danger is when you stop listening because you want it to be something else."

---

His story came out the way the desert gave up water — in seeps, in trickles, in moments when the pressure of holding it back exceeded the effort of release.

Vermilia had been a kingdom. Not large — a strip of green between the eastern mountains and the deep desert, fed by rivers that came down from the snowmelt. Its people were half-Hazzan by blood (the old Haz settlers had married into the Vermillian clans generations ago) and wholly Vermillian by identity. They built in red stone. They farmed the river flats. They developed a martial tradition — the Lian Hu school — that treated combat as geometry, the body as an instrument of precise and patient force.

Dzuvag had been the school's last protege. He had trained under the Lian Hu master from the age of six, learning the chain-sickle, the staff, the empty-hand forms that were not fighting styles but meditations on the body's relationship to space. He was fourteen when the Haz King came.

"He didn't burn Vermilia," Dzuvag said. They were walking the seep-line, checking the tuber traps he'd set. The twin suns were descending, their light stretching their shadows long and thin across the red sand. "Burning is honest. He turned the rivers. Diverted them — I don't know how. Some technology from the deep vaults, older than anyone could read. The rivers changed course overnight. Without water, the farms died in a week. Without farms, the people starved in a month. Without people, the sand came."

He pulled a tuber from the ground. Grey-leafed, the root bulbous and pale. He brushed the sand from it and bit into it raw, chewing with the unconcerned expression of someone eating something that was adequate and nothing more.

"The Haz King didn't destroy Vermilia. He let the desert do it. More efficient. Less blame. By the time anyone thought to fight, there was nothing left to fight for. The kingdom sank. Sand over stone. The spires are still there — you've seen them. The rest is gone."

"And you survived."

"I was already out here." He gestured at the desert with the tuber. "Training. The deep-desert retreat — forty days alone, the last test of the Lian Hu. I was fourteen. When I came back, Vermilia was gone. The school. The master. The river where I learned to swim." He chewed. Swallowed. "So I stayed."

"For thirty years?"

"Thirty-one."

Aharah was quiet. The seep-line stretched ahead, its damp sand dark against the red. A manok clicked past their feet, its carapace flashing green.

"Don't you hate him?" she said. "The Haz King."

Dzuvag stopped walking. He looked at her with the hematite eyes that the desert had not softened, and his expression was the closest thing to tenderness she had seen on that weathered face.

"Hatred is a chain-sickle with no blade," he said. "It only weights you down."

He handed her the tuber. She ate it. It tasted like dirt and mineral water and survival. They walked on.

---

The chain-sickle became an extension of her arm.

Not quickly. Not easily. The weapon resisted mastery the way the desert resisted comfort — it could be learned but not tamed, its physics cooperating with the wielder only when the wielder stopped trying to command them. The ball swung. The chain described its arcs. The sickle flew or it didn't, and the difference between flight and failure was not strength or speed but timing — the precise moment when the wrist's motion aligned with the chain's momentum and the sickle's weight, and the three became one motion.

She practiced in the mornings, before the suns climbed high enough to punish exertion. The ball in her palm. The chain singing. The sickle hooking stone, hooking thornbrush, hooking the wooden posts Dzuvag planted in the sand at varying distances. She missed. She hit her own shin. She tangled the chain around her forearm and had to unwind it with her teeth while Dzuvag watched from the cellar entrance with an expression of elaborate patience.

"You're thinking," he said, after she missed the post for the twentieth time.

"Of course I'm thinking."

"That's the problem. The chain doesn't think. The ball doesn't think. The sickle doesn't think. If you think, you're the only part of the weapon that's not working."

She threw. The sickle flew wide. The chain wrapped around nothing. The ball thudded into the sand.

"Again," Dzuvag said.

She threw. Wide.

"Again."

Wide.

"Again."

On the fortieth throw — or the fiftieth, she had stopped counting — she felt it. Not in her mind but in her wrist: a click, a settling, the ball's weight finding the chain's tension finding the sickle's edge. The three aligned. The sickle flew straight and true and hooked the post and held, and the chain went taut between her hand and the target like a line drawn in the air.

She looked at Dzuvag.

"Good," he said. "Now do it with your eyes closed."

She would. Not that day, not that week, but she would. The desert was patient and the chain-sickle was patient and Dzuvag, who had waited thirty-one years for the Tagata Rock to speak, was the most patient thing she had ever encountered. She learned the weapon the way she learned the desert: by stopping, by listening, by allowing the knowledge to arrive instead of chasing it.

Her sunskin healed. The beetle paste did its work and the damaged cells along her left side began to regenerate — slowly, unevenly, the gold returning in patches like a landscape emerging from fog. She gained weight. Her ribs receded. The brittle grey ends of her hair broke off and new growth came in dark, and the Lyonessa gene — fed by the manok meat and the seep-spring water and the twin suns' light — resumed its slow reconstruction of her body. She grew a full inch in the first two weeks. Her hands hardened further. Her night vision sharpened until the desert after dark was not darkness but a gradient of greys, and she moved through it the way the manoks did — low, sure, reading the sand with her feet.

Dzuvag watched the changes with the clinical interest of a man observing weather. "The gene is adapting," he said, one evening. "It's building you for this place. The Lyonessa sequence was designed for soldiers — urban fighters, wall-defenders. But you fed it desert. So it's building a desert creature."

"Is that bad?"

"It's unusual." He poked the fire with his staff. "The last Lyonessa I saw was forty years ago. She shone like a lantern and moved like water and she could take a bolt-shot to the chest and stand up. But she was a city thing. You—" he looked at her across the fire, and the flames lit the hematite of his eyes "—you are becoming something the gene hasn't built before."

The fire crackled. The desert breathed its low, sustained moan beyond the cellar entrance. Aharah looked at her hands — the hardened knuckles, the concentrated sunskin blazing gold in the firelight — and felt, beneath the healing burns and the growing muscle and the gene's quiet renovation, the thing she could not name. The thing the Tagata Rock had hummed when she pressed her palm against it. The vibration that said: *not yet, but soon.*

She turned the chain-sickle's ball in her palm and felt its weight, and the weight was not a burden but a promise.
