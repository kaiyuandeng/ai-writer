## Prose

"Yes—I am your Replacematon," a young man of about twenty-five said at the door.

His carved cheekbones, spiked black hair, and piercing brown eyes all bore down on the old man. He did not blink. Yet, despite his intensity, an oversized tan suit with too large shoulder pads made him look like a boy in wardrobe. He stood lazily, jutting out a hip, giving the impression that he was both intensely present and very lost at the same time.

"Jesus, how new are you? You still smell like goddam milk," Marcos di Assis said, tightening his corduroy robe and covering his nose.

"Eighteen hours and six minutes…" the young man answered.

"Seven minutes," he added.

Marcos sighed. He dropped his shoulders and scratched his head, and in doing so, his stomach popped out the front of his robe. He quickly covered himself up. "Jesus. I never thought I'd meet someone who was literally born yesterday," he said.

The young man didn't seem to hear him. He cranned his neck and looked at the rafters in the awning above.

"What'd you say your name was?" Marcos asked.

"I didn't say," the young man said, still looking up. In a corner where two beams met, a family of spiders had settled and were hauling in a net of flies. "My model is in the V9 line."

Marcos di Assis mulled over those words.

"V9… I'll think of a name for you later," Marcos said. "Well? What are you waiting for? Come in—You're just getting wet out there." He stepped aside, revealing a converted garage. The concrete driveway slopped down, leading to a narrow, unlit hallway and a set of elegant, steel staircase beside. The young man stepped in with the momentum of someone falling.

"Whoa! Careful there," Marcos said.

Once again, the young man seemed not to hear. He gazed around wide-eyed like a child. The driveway walls, remodeled in acid-etched bronze sheets now dulled to an emerald patina by time, bloomed with birds, bees, and flowers—the resplendent vision of a garden swollen at the peak of pregnancy.

"This art yours?" the young man asked.

"It is a grotesque," the old man scoffed.

The young man also bowed over slightly, pressing a hand to his right side and uttering low groans.

"Stop that!" Marcos yelped. "I know what you're doing. It's annoying, stop that."

"It's my duty to copy you as best as I can," the young man said.

"No it's not—," Marcos said. "You're here to do what I do. And I can tell you copying me is the fastest way to fail. Many have tried already…"

### The Violin Audition

The raw metallic sound of a violin permeated the living room as a bow sawed feverishly between two strings, attacking the music. The high atrium ceiling amplified the sound.

The young man touched the back of his neck. Hairs stood up. It was his first time having this sensation in his eighteen hours of living. Frission, he recalled.

"Violin Partita No. 2 in D Minor," the young man sighed. "But it is played so beautifully… with so much aggression… No. Courage. As if the violinist were playing it for the last time."

*[Valentine plays. Technically perfect. The Maestro weeps.]*

"Perfect technique. I don't think you were off a single milisecond," Marcos said.

"I let some notes linger an additional fifty to seventy-five nanoseconds to add a sense of roughness to the sound," the young man said. "To give it a human touch."

"It's boring. Technically perfect but bland and sterile—like dead cheese wrapped in cellophane. What you heard coming in? That's alive. That's got bacteria in it," Marcos said.

"But Maestro, you're crying," the young man said, furrowing his brow.

"If only we could learn this easily… to not have this talent wasted on a machine," Marcos said to himself under his breath.

"I'm sorry my talent makes you envious," the young man said earnestly without malice.

"You have no talent!" Marcos barked. "You have to cultivate talent. What you have is… is… an installation! It's a mockery!"

### Valentine's First Instinct

*[After the Maestro leaves, Valentine picks a second, "suboptimal" violin — pockmarked, warped, with a pinhole through the body. His analysis says it will sound terrible. He plays it anyway. Then his instinct tells him to switch hands — hold the violin in the right, bow with the left. Against all training data. He fights the impulse, then surrenders to it.]*

With sweat pouring from his brow and gritting his teeth, Valentine changed hands. He held the violin now in his right hand and the bow in his left, a most unusual position. Yet, somehow it felt right. He raised the violin to his cheek and leaned his jaw into it and it was as comfortable as a pillow. Quickly, a hundred renditions of the Partita ran through his mind. He prepared the bow, sniffed in, and launched into the performance.

Valentine became lost in the music.

Even though he was aware of every aspect of his playing, it felt as if he had stepped back; as if he were watching himself perform. His body swayed naturally. He was light on his feet. His left pinky somehow had a special affinity for bowing that pressed down on the grip and the screw just right and strings were growling, groaning… singing.

And this violin sounded better than the other, even though its analysis placed it as suboptimal. The simulation was wrong. The violin sounded richer and fuller. There was a magic that was lost to the simulation in how that pinhole—calculated as a weakness—stirred the texture of the sound.

*[Valentine then hears the live violin music from upstairs again — realizes it's not a recording. Someone is up there playing. He follows the sound up flights of stairs, through increasingly abstract paintings, down a long corridor, to a door ajar at the end. This is Vera's introduction: we hear her before we see her.]*

### Vera's Entrance

Valentine opened the door.

A young woman stood there. Platinum hair cut into an asymmetrical bob; a sharp face with eyes that stared intensely and unbrokenly back into his own. She wore old jeans frayed at the ankles and a homespun blouse with frilled collar and shoulders. A black, knitted sweater spun with gaps so large it was more net than sweater dropped just below her hipline. And she wore a pair of old, worn out ballet shoes.

"Who are you?" Valentine asked.

The young woman stepped backward, holding the bow like a saber between them.

## Editorial Notes (Author's Own, from Scrivener brackets)

- **Architecture:** "Reconcile the architecture. Either use the garage or have this be the front steps leading to the French Window." → Need a consistent blueprint of the house. The stairwell is crucial for the Frog & Well play later.
- **Maestro's character:** "Marcos is tough and V0 has memories of the old man battling through the playground. Making Marcos more believable makes the reveal of V0 more impactful later."
- **Valentine's bipolar mode:** The switch between childlike innocence and cold serpentine corporate mode should be highlighted as a feature of V9's personality — not a bug.
- **The violin as lifeline:** "Valentine should continue to practice every day even with Vera's distractions — and it could provide him with a lifeline when he gets to the City. I see him busking."
- **Vera's aesthetic:** "Make Vera's aesthetic more consistent. She looks like a hobo here with ballet shoes and later she changes into black. Maybe she starts with black hair and changes into platinum for the Frog play."
- **Save first tears:** "Let's save Valentine's first tears for after the Frog and the Well." → The crying after left-handed playing should be cut or muted; reserve the full emotional breakthrough for B3.
