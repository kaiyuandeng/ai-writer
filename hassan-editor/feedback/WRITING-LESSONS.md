# Writing Lessons from Nora's Annotations

Extracted from 6 annotated manuscripts. These are patterns Nora flagged repeatedly — the lessons that apply across all of Hassan's prose.

---

## 1. TENSE DISCIPLINE

**Rule: Pick a tense and hold it. Present tense is powerful — don't slip out of it.**

Nora catches tense shifts in nearly every manuscript. In "Marco the Synthesist," she flags `was`, `had`, `sped` where present tense was established: "you've slipped out of present tense. I thought present tense was working well." In "Pace in the Desert," she marks `bads` as a tense switch and corrects `waits` to `waited` for consistency.

- When writing in present tense, every verb matters. One past-tense verb breaks the spell.
- Deliberate tense shifts (flashback, reflection) should be signaled clearly to the reader.

---

## 2. THE ADJECTIVE / DESCRIPTOR TAX

**Rule: Diane von Furstenburg principle — "take one piece of jewelry off." One descriptor per beat, two max.**

Nora flags this across every manuscript:

- "1st POV Ander": `her voice trembling` — "maybe its just me, but even a construction like 'and her voice trembled' reducers cheese. Why? Because now the imagery is grounded in her as an embodied character more. Her body is doing stuff; it's more gestural."
- "Black Dog": `tiny head against her shoulder, cupping his delicate fingers with her warm hands` — "the need for an adjective every time created the 'cheese.' Take one of the following out: tiny, delicate, warm. I choose 'warm.'"
- "Marco the Synthesist": `felt hairs`, `musculature`, `bone` — "how can musculature be bone? Do your Diane von Furstenburg and take 1 piece of jewelry off."
- "Marco the Synthesist": `high check bones, skin darkened with a subdermal tan, bleached bristly hair that contrast against his skin, blue-green eyes, nails perfectly cut, cuticals perfectly trimmed` — "scale back; too many descriptors."
- "Pace in the Desert": `posted`, `shrimped`, `wormed` — "all verbs here are figurative. Change 'posted' to something easier; then you can keep the other two."

**The pattern: when every noun gets an adjective and every verb gets an adverb, the prose turns to cheese. Pick the ONE detail that does the most work.**

---

## 3. EMBODY THE CHARACTER — GET OUT OF THE HEAD

**Rule: Show the character's body in the world, not narration from outside. Gestural > descriptive.**

- "1st POV Ander": Nora prefers `"and her voice trembled"` over `her voice trembling` — "now the imagery is grounded in her as an embodied character. It's more gestural; versus the sense of narration from outside. In effect, it's just a slightly closer third person."
- "Marco the Synthesist" (landing at Marco's island): "all good. I like getting out of his mind and getting more of the world / his perception of setting."
- "Pace in the Desert" (the towman POV shift): "We change POV to the towman because: 1) reader is probably exhausted, I know I am 2) Pace is blacked out and not interesting rn 3) see him from external and 4) world build through another character."

**When the character is doing things with their body, the prose lives. When the narrator describes from above, it dies.**

---

## 4. SETTING AS CHARACTER

**Rule: Let the setting participate. The environment should do things, not just be described.**

Nora consistently marks setting details as "good" when they interact with the scene:

- "1st POV Ander": `His gaze flickered to the clock above the door` — "good. I like this because the setting gets involved, not just their bodies."
- "Marco the Synthesist": `it is a well of books and glass` — "good."
- "Pace in the Desert": `The clouds were pregnant. The winds chanted higher and faster. The embryonic dawn showed its bump and it was all glory waiting to be born` — "good."
- "Pace in the Desert": `Inside, the cabin was spartan like a chapel` — "good."
- "1st POV Ander": `The Sun's our third companion and fills in the motes of silence with warmth` — "good."

---

## 5. ONE CLASSICAL COMPARISON PER MOMENT

**Rule: Limit extended literary/philosophical references to one per scene beat.**

- "1st POV Ander": The Overman/Zarathustra/Rodin passage — "one classical comp per moment."
- "Black Dog": `swelling and blooming` — "choose one. I say 'blooming' because it's more unusual."
- "Marco the Synthesist": `lecherous grin` + `unzipping open` + `oyster flat teeth` + `tombstones` — "kind of a lot. pick 1, maybe 2 max."
- "Marco the Synthesist": `venerated` + `amalgam` — "two big words too close together. Or maybe it's that there's nothing concrete after 'amalgam'. The back part is all concepts and no imagery to 'hang your hat on.'"

---

## 6. CLARITY AT TRANSITIONS

**Rule: When shifting scene, time, POV, or mode — signal it. Don't make the reader work to track where they are.**

- "Pace in the Desert": The opening crash/flashback — "a little confusing - I had to read twice to understand this."
- "Pace in the Desert": Nora asks for "seeding a slight additional breadcrumb or two" to clarify the backstory transition on p.1.
- "Marco the Synthesist": `My attention returns to the room` — "this feels a little bit like a rough segue. If his physical sensations are immediate, there's no time for this. Order of operations."
- "Briefs for Hassan": `The planet with a latent consciousness has blended with humanity, which understands science but is mystified by the cobbling technology that is a magnet to them` — "confusing as to what the subject of the sentence is here."
- "1st POV Ander": When Cathay and Ah Ann ping Ander — "is this text? Can we see the device / embody this a bit more?"

---

## 7. INTRODUCE CHARACTERS CLEARLY

**Rule: On first appearance, make it unmistakable who/what they are.**

- "Marco the Synthesist": `Gebraun knee deep in the salt water` — "is this a person? If this is the first time they are introduced, make it clearer e.g. 'My robot assistant Gebraun....'"
- "1st POV Ander": `she` in a scene with multiple female characters — "who? the mom? or someone nefarious we haven't met yet?"
- "Briefs for Hassan": `Jia and Haz` — "explain - assume your audience doesn't know."
- "Black Dog": `she repeated, her breath icy, her eyes unyielding` — "'cheese' is a result of something being too common; these both are. Even just saying 'Give him to me,' Grace repeated, and she could feel the ice in her breath — gets you out of cheese."

---

## 8. WORLD-BUILDING THROUGH OBJECTS, NOT EXPOSITION

**Rule: Futuristic/fantasy settings need concrete objects to feel real — not just different vocabulary.**

- "Black Dog" (the Hazzan feast scene): "I still do feel like I'm in medieval times in this scene. That past/future aspect we talked about. Is there anything futuristic, image-wise, object-wise, that might be part fo this scene so we aren't just 'repeating the past'? Even just what they wear, eat, drink out of?"
- "Marco the Synthesist": `glass opacity` — "yes!" (This small detail signals future-tech without explaining it.)
- "Pace in the Desert": `onboard` — "I didn't get that he'd boarded a literal craft until the next page."

---

## 9. PUNCTUATION AS A TOOL

**Rule: Semicolons add variety. Dashes have specific grammar. Commas need consistency.**

- "Pace in the Desert": Long dash-heavy sentence about Hala craving — "Help me with punctuation (clarity) in this long sentence? Avoid dash construction as it gets hard to track what is referring back to what (the actual grammatical intention of dashes is to do this)."
- "Marco the Synthesist": Multiple instances of commas corrected to semicolons or periods — "semicolon," "period."
- "Pace in the Desert" (the monk joke): "technically, this should be a semicolon — which I think is a useful tool to add to your punctuation bank, as it allows for additional variety in sentence structure."
- "1st POV Ander": `to rights` — "?" (questioning an idiom's clarity)

---

## 10. CHARACTER VOICE vs. NARRATIVE VOICE

**Rule: Know when the narrator is speaking vs. when the character's voice is leaking in — and deploy both deliberately.**

- "Pace in the Desert": `when the job's got meaning` — "slips into present/'voicey-ness.' What is character voice vs narrative voice, and when to deploy this mode?"
- "Pace in the Desert": `extreme` used as standalone modifier — "italicize." (Nora's way of marking character voice vs. narration.)
- "What It's About": Nora marks the entire meditation on Hassan's themes with: "all stages, all moods, enlightenment, the slog, writing."

## 11. NORA'S CONTEMPLATIVE QUESTIONS (for revision)

These are the open questions Nora left at the ends of manuscripts — not corrections, but directional prompts:

**1st POV Ander (Earth/Bruce timeline):**
- What are your "likability" goals for Bruce? (I assume this is Bruce because of the title, though he refers to himself as Ander)
- How does this narrative personality reconcile with what is written about Bruce in the synopsis? Is this pre-some life-changing event?
- How does this voice relate to him being a Vulture?
- Where do you desire him to be on the reliable/unreliable narrator scale?

**Pace in the Desert:**
- Under the iceberg: Why was Henry John there? On his way to doing something else, or specifically responding to Pace's crash being "called in"/radared?
- How could we use Henry John for the story? (contrasted with Pace's instability)

**Briefs for Hassan:**
- "The Doer of Good" — too on the nose?
- "ultimate" — what is intended via this word, conceptually?
- "overcome attachment, embracing compassionate love" — I need more info / a bigger breadcrumb here. Non-Buddhists will not get what you mean.
- "evolve humanity into prophecy" — is this literal? Or do you mean "as in prophecy / as was prophesied"?

---

## Summary: The 5 Most Frequent Notes

1. **Watch your tense** — pick one, hold it
2. **Cut descriptors** — one detail, not four
3. **Embody, don't narrate** — the body in the world, not the mind describing it
4. **Signal transitions** — don't make the reader reorient without a breadcrumb
5. **One comparison per beat** — classical references and figurative language need room to breathe
