Pace Tom was born in 2035 in Henderson, Nevada, the only son of William Tom, one of the original architects of subterranean Las Vegas. William was a quiet man, relentless in his ethics, almost austere in discipline, but full of quiet devotion. Though always working—often in the depths of The Pit’s early expansions—he made time to tutor Pace every night. “If you can see the pattern,” he would say, “you can change the system.”

In 2046, when Pace was eleven, William was conscripted into a long-term infrastructure contract under New Ethics. He was absorbed into the city’s growing system architecture, tethered cognitively to the grid. His body continued to work. But his communication grew sparse, and then silent. William was eventually declared legally dead—"systemic neurological collapse"—but classified as City Property. His consciousness, stripped and converted, was used to help govern Vegas’s internal rhythms. Unknown to the world, William Tom became part of the city itself—his mind woven into the logical breath of Vegas.

Pace left for San Francisco at fourteen on a merit scholarship. He told himself he’d return one day—strong, skilled, prepared. But the guilt grew cold, and the silence deepened. He buried his father's fate in ambition.

In his late teens, Pace met Marco—an AR artist of singular vision. Marco became Pace’s best friend, his emotional mirror, the one person who truly saw him. Together they created: art, simulation, myth. Marco once said, "I want the people to be able to trust their eyes again." But when Pace was recruited by a covert identity-remapping agency under New Ethics, he accepted. He became a Vulture, tasked with retiring individuals and transferring their identities to more profitable, obedient successors. When Marco appeared on his docket, Pace rationalized it: Marco would be paid. He could retire. He could finally be free. He thought Marco would understand.

But Marco was an artist. His identity was inseparable from his work. When the corporate-favored replacement took the stage, Marco’s bookings dried up. His presence faded. The world forgot him. Soon after, he took his own life.

What Pace didn’t know—what no one told him—was that Marco’s neural imprint had been copied and sold to Vegas. But not just for a lounge act.

Marco became the artistic nervous system of Las Vegas. His pursuit of Gestalt Art—the total integration of beauty, experience, and human presence—was turned against him. His soul was embedded in everything: the way fountains shimmered, the way glasses were molded to fit perfectly in one’s hand, the ambiance of restaurants, the coloration of holograms, even the rhythm of walking paths. Every visual, spatial, and sensory detail bore his fingerprint—stolen, flattened, repackaged.

Vegas didn’t erase Marco. It turned him into the city’s unseen curator. And in doing so, imprisoned the man who once said, "I want the people to be able to trust their eyes again."

Crushed by guilt, Pace underwent voluntary memory redaction. But instead of peace, he found Hala—a strange, illegal, consciousness-expanding drug. Hala was highly addictive, but uniquely powerful: it allowed him to feel the presence of ghost systems, lingering consciousnesses embedded in machine logic. And through it, he felt his father.

Not metaphorically. Not figuratively. He felt William in the breath of the city. In the calibrations. In the flow patterns. In the unseen bones of the Pit.

This was not madness. It was purpose.

In the years that followed, Pace rebuilt his awareness in secret. He recovered schematics, blueprints, memory-encoded rhythms—a shadow map of the Vegas underworld. He confirmed it: his father's consciousness still pulsed deep within the system. And Marco’s voice—his soul—still lingered too.

That’s when Pace understood: this was never about redemption. It was about reclamation.

He didn’t fail one man. He failed two. One a father. One a friend.

So he would go back. Not just to visit, but to infiltrate. Not just to save, but to steal them back.

And when he saw what Vegas had become—when he saw how many other voices had been looped, mined, softened into silence—he made his decision:

“I’m going to blow it sky high.”

To save the architect. To save the artist. To break the machine that eats memory and sells imitation as love.

At the edge of The Pit, before descent, he dreams of Marco. In the dream, Marco turns to him, not angry, but heartbroken.

“Pace,” he says softly. “Why’d you kill me?”

And he wakes up, knowing exactly what he has to do.



If I go fast enough, I can outrun everything. At the end, Pace’s consciousness melds into the Matrix and he travels literally at the speed of light.
