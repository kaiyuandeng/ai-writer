<!$Scr_H::1><!$Scr_Ps::0>

1. Tell a good story. Think of the reader. Reader pleasure. Clarity over abstraction of metaphor unless it is very natural.
Be clear. Good writing (fiction or not fiction) is clear writing. Writerly writing comes up from ego, a need to show-off or self-express, whereas the crafting of good fiction is the challenge.
Set reading level around Isaacson, Crichton, and the Bible; make Hassan accessible. Gut-check, “Can Mike read this?” The topmost goal is to tell a story to the readers.
From Tolkien: “…I dislike allegory in all its manifestations and always have done so… I much prefer history, true or feigned… I think that many confuse ‘applicability’ with ‘allegory’; but the one resides in the freedom of the reader and the other in the purposed domination of the author… the prime motive was the desire of a tale-teller to try his hand at a really long story that would hold the attention of readers, amuse them, delight them, and at times maybe excite them or deeply move them. As a guide I had only my own feelings for what is appealing or moving, and for many the guide was inevitably often at fault… some who find it boring… others find it pleased… it is not possible in a long tale to please everybody at all points, but the most critical reader, me, find it: too short.”

2. Never be boring
“An author’s first job is to keep the reader turning the page.” Have fun, be fun, be funny, be attractive, be interesting. Balance high—low, horror—beauty, be sublime, use rhythm, tension, surprise, beauty, and truth; Utilize in media res. Prefer fast-paced: Don’t slow it down with subsidiary details. Don’t use cheap shock value — does it improve and align the story? Does it mix the familiar in uncanny combinations? Does it bang? And have FUN! If I’m not having fun the reader won’t have fun, they can feel the psychic imprint in the story. So what if I have to use one more word, if I’m having fun with it, I should do it.

3. Borrow, better yet steal, always evolve
Tropes are okay iff we deepen them into something new and relevant; Work smart, use the best tools, there is no obligation to do it on anyone else’s terms. This is my work and I will complete it to and for my satisfaction. “Black cat, white cat; As long as it catches the mouse.” In fact, Hassan necessary must have archetypes because it is built on all the past myths from before, so it’s not theft but homage. Honor what came before by making it better, “that’s how our species gets better, take inspiration and put it back through the flow of history.” Hassan is mythopoesis—creating myth.

4. The Theme is the North Star
The Theme is Love / Pain /Loneliness / Fear vs Faith / Belief & Purpose / Purpose & Meaning / Surrender vs Triumph: It is a story of Love in the time of the Pain Lord, on an old planet called Hassan; Come back to the theme when we are lost, ask “What is Love in this time?” “Who is the Pain Lord and how did he come to be?” “What is the history and setting of Hassan?”

5. Say the true thing; be lean
Risk sincerity; be earnest; self-actualizingly weird; dig deep into judgment and extract the universal truth; We’re not pontificating nor arbitrating, we are writing the best story we can. “A lot of my earlier works would have worked better if they had been simpler.” We are not here to win awards, we are here to win readers. Write lean, every beat drives the story, no florid overdescription. Not bloated, not dry — just honesty with economy: Lean. Use order of operations to pan the camera and be cinematic.  Use simplicity and subtlety to enhance emotions; Don’t try at all to show flare. Be natural, facile, effortless (in the final draft) and endeavor to work toward that state.

6. Be humble
This is not a political treatise and we never know when we’ll be stuck; Listen to the story, it’ll reveal itself plentifully — it has its own soul; Discover the story through the process as I discover the Self in the process of meditation; Not a manifesto — we are writing mythology. Listen to the story, “Use sound and ear to carry the story,” consult the Le Guin Five. Don’t use imagination to force the story — that is awkward and backward — the story will fuel our imagination to write itself; Don’t be falsely humble. Prefer momentum over style - catch the momentum of “things, the story” via Herzog

7. Keep falling in Love with the work itself
Work, play, rest, learn, date, explore, rest, intuit, plan, feel, work; “Metta will help you every time.” Tinker, chisel meticulously. Read out loud; Go for sound and beauty. Utilize ekphrasis as freeze-frames in still images.

8. Emotional depth and Intellectual Clarity are balanced
Parse the emotion, grok it, and present it clearly; Hassan must be honet, true, smart, & beautiful to have its intended impact. Has heart and mind. Repeat. It’s important.

9. Never over-explain
Trust the reader, feed crumbs of intrigue, let their minds wonder and fill in the world, the work is complete when readers participate. It’s about collaboration. It’s about respect.

10. Ask for help
“Atha yoga-anushasanam; Now the teachings of Yoga begins”; The atha moment in the Gita is when Arjuna asks Krishna for help; Put yourself out there, Hassan is more than strong enough to stand on its own; Stuck is the 4 of Pentacles, freedom is the Fool. Also, stuck? Just keep staring at it.

11. Respect the Le Guin 5
1) Sound of words 2) syntax and grammar 3) pattern of images 4) pattern of feeling

12. The Big Five: Character, Language, Plot, Theme, Setting
Scene not hitting? Chapter gone awry? Check that the Big Five are presented.
Character – The soul of the story. Emotional gravity. Arc. Choice. Conflict.
Language – The voice. Rhythm. Diction. Style. Prose that earns its keep.
Plot – The engine. Momentum. Cause-and-effect. Structure and reveals.
Theme – The compass. What it means. The questions beneath the events.
Setting – The body. World texture. History. Constraints. Mythos.

12. Black cat; White cat
Phase through the rules if they keep us back. Whatever works, whatever goes. Ball hard with heart. Language itself is up for renegotiation now.

13. If Hassan has anything, it is Heart. And Soul.
Let the losers cosplay over literature and literary fiction as a genre-in-denial. Hassan is about bold earnest and going into the depth of spiritual soul.

14. Finish.
Hassan must be finished.

Omniscient third POV:
We [Nora] talked about the omniscient narrator voice as having a camera that can zoom in and out of different characters heads, at times going more “close third person” on a variety of characters, while still understanding that the characters in these disparate opening chapters are eventually going to end up in the same chapter together. Therefore, the narrative voice and its style and mannerisms become what you called the “primer paint,” which I think is a good metaphor.

Baselines “vanilla” narrator without character color is: Mythic melancholy, lucid brutality, quiet yearning, fatal grace, faithful triumph

Mythic melancholy → Everything feels like it’s happening again, as if repeating across ages; a deep sadness inside everything, even when no one says it aloud.
Lucid brutality → Violence and decay are clear-eyed, unflinching, but not gory for the sake of it — it’s the natural entropy of all things.
Quiet yearning → A longing for redemption, connection, transcendence that persists under the surface, even when the world says "it's too late."
Fatal grace → Movements, moments, deaths — even collapse itself — has a strange, sorrowful beauty, as if decline and nobility are bound together.
Faithful triumph —> Hassan has heart. Hassan never gives up. Hopeful. Kind.

Keep action sequences to two or less paragraphs. Four to six for outstanding fights.


Italicize neologisms on first appearance only if their meaning isn’t immediately clear from context.
Do not italicize if the neologism:
Is slang or character voice (mancy, skull-picking, bad batch).
Functions like metaphor or poetic language in narration (hauntsmen).

Italicize on first use if the term:
Refers to a spiritual, philosophical, technological, or cultural system.
Carries layered meaning or in-world significance (Sankhara, resticide, chroma).

Drop italics after first use unless:
You are re-emphasizing symbolic meaning.
The word has dramatically changed in significance.
Do not explain neologisms directly; use sentence rhythm and context to suggest meaning.
Avoid italic clutter — let important terms stand out without over-formatting.

About em-dash: Create pause, emphasis, contrast, or interruption. When used well, it feels cinematic. But when overused, it draws too much attention to itself.
Use commas for gentle rhythm
Use periods for sharp clarity
Use colons for dramatic logic
Use rewriting when the dash is doing too much

Word frequency per chapter cannot be more than three. One is the standard. None is ideal.

Prefer momentum over style(?)
