**Pace Valentine Cycle – Near-Future / Cosmic-Noir Fragments**

**Story 1: The Telomere Plague (near-future detective setup)**

Scientists find the telomere base codes changing. Some people were having reactions and getting sudden and deadly cancer (change to cough or mysterious). The Detective reached out. He is Bruce—a Sherlock Holmes of the future.  
Bruce is human.  
It is 2039.  
AI is alive, it runs all of our days, most people are retired or training up a robot to replace them.  
Energy comes abundantly from the Sun. There are clouds of Space AI living in clusters of satellites that is beamed down to Earth.  
The calculations are done in Space now, so Earth is mostly a receiver. Then patches of people started getting cancer. The symptoms were different.  
Bruce finds out from the coroner of DNA cellular damage. He finds an eerie message encoded in the deceased’s telomeres, which are regular and fixed patterns of nucleotides.  
Where does this come from? What did those people have in common?  
They all slept with each other in the span of a few weeks.  
They were also doing it raw. The sperm wrote the DNA on the host female telomere. So the disease is passed by male to female hosts. Once a female is infected, she can infect new male hosts.  
The FBI did a test for other exposures. Kissing also and any swapping of DNA. So touching could also confer it. It is a very virulent but neutral pathogen that can evolve into a quick death.  
Pace is a Sabient, a human-produced AI intelligence, an artificial mind in a body created from human DNA.  
He could get infected, but he could reprint himself into a suit. Humans who have not been uploaded cannot.

**Story 2: Alley Interrogation (Pace Valentine confrontation)**

“My epitaph will probably read…”  
“World’s greatest detective,” Pace said. It was quiet and dank in the alley way. The shadowy man resided in the dark of a small bridge while Pace stood outside, his hands stuffed in his leather jacket. He had flipped the collars over his neck to cover himself from the brisk wind. His legs were almost shaking from the cold and the way the man in the shadow seemed criminal and ready to kill.  
“It’s not too late to give us the evidence, Mr. Valentine,” the thug said. “We want the encoded data from the human telomere virus.”  
“How did you find out about this?” Pace asked, raising his hands into the air.  
The man stepped out, gun in hand. He had a small bowler hat on with shades. His hand was on the gun and the other was in the pocket palming something. The man continued.  
“Mr. Valentine, the swathe of telomerase-related cancer has been known for some time. But it wasn’t until recently news went on the Net that your organization claimed there was an… extra-terrestrial explanation for the change.”  
“Do I have to say here?” Pace asked.  
“There is nowhere but here,” the man said and raised the gun.  
“Yes, we found that the encoding matched the algorithmic vibration of an artificial intelligence. Which one? We don’t know. But it looks like it’s trying to encode itself in humans.”  
“How did it do this?” the man asked urgently.  
“We don’t know,” Pace said. “So far, just stay away and don’t touch other humans.”  
The man retracted his gun and crossed his arms. “We’re meant to do this forever?” The man asked.  
“No… ten, twelve months?” Pace said.  
“Are you insane,” the man said.  
“Who are you?” Pace asked.  
“I’m with the FBI…” the man said but Pace interrupted him.  
“You couldn’t have sent an email?” Pace asked.  
“This is sensitive and urgent matter, the White House is getting a hold of this and…” The man said.  
“So you’re an agent from the White House,” Pace said.  
“We want to know if there’s a way to stop this?” the man asked.  
“Yes, isolate and let the infected ones die out,” Pace said. “But—we don’t have a good idea of who is infected or if they will stay put.”  
“What are the effects?”  
“We don’t know, it is a sentient virus,” Pace said. “It knows so it will try to carry onto another host before you can catch it.”  
“So the ones with cancer…”  
“Have cancer because we were watching them. The virus caught another ride and left us this. It’s insane if you think about it. But, if you think AI will just stay to the silicon plane, you are also out of your mind.” Pace answered.  
“Why are you so cloak and dagger for?” Pace said.  
The man uncovered his goggles. He had augmented eyes with a sheen on them. There was a connected piecer over his temple that let Pace know this man was a connected Sapient of another network.  
“I shouldn’t think this, but…” Pace said. “What if you and I also have it?”  
“How? There’s a test,” the man said.  
“No… recently we’ve been seeing the virus mimic telomerase DNA until it needs to mutate. So there is virtually no way to know. And, we don’t know how widespread it is. From the examples at the lab, a sneeze was enough. Loose skin cells if there were a lot, most transmissions will be over the air.”  
“There is a way to know,” the man said.  
“How?” Pace asked.  
“Shoot it dead,” the man said, “it’ll squirm when it’s dead.” He raised his gun and fired at Pace.  
Pace took a shot to the shoulder. He went down. He went for his gun but he was late on the draw. The man closed in and kicked it away.  
Pace groaned on the ground.  
“You couldn’t have…” he cursed, “any other way.”  
The man lowered his gun and quickly and deftly applied a healing gel from his kit. Pace stabilized.  
“I had to make sure,” the man said. “The agency doesn’t know who to trust. Those lunatics have banded together into the Human Conservancy…”

**Story 3: The Dawn Cult Case (private eye commission)**

A man comes in and offers me a job to tail his wife and her lover. He proposes that his wife has been turned by a cult. He sat there and I looked him in the eye. DNA cults were serious business and I didn’t want a fed arresting me. He didn’t look like a fed. But the best ones don’t. I eyed him some more.  
“Look—I’ll double it, but I need to get my wife out. Ever since she joined this cult, she’s been scaring the kids and the family,” the husband said.  
“What has she been doing?” I asked.  
“Well for one thing, she’s constantly murmuring to herself around the house. When I asked her about it, she told me it’s an alphabet and to not worry about it,” he said.  
“So, she likes to mutter. Creepy but not dangerous,” I said.  
“And the kids say she’s been changing,” he said, his voice lowering. The husband moved around uneasily on the sofa. A car passed by outside and we saw lines hidden by the privacy screens. “She’s been looking younger, taller,” he said, “better than she did when we started dating.”  
“Isn’t that good?” I asked.  
He shook his head.  
“She’s getting so much attention, I can barely hang out when we go outside,” he said.  
I nodded. This man was afraid his wife had outgrown him, and he’s right to worry. It seemed like every week there was some new therapy on the market that de-wrinkled and youthified the suburban housewives. But they never lasted long and this one didn’t seem any different.  
The man looked furtively around and rubbed his hands together reminiscent of a gerbil.  
“What is it you want to check out?” I asked.  
“Follow them into the cult club and see what they’re doing to my wife,” he said. “Maybe then we can get some proof and have the court lock her down.”  
I looked him up and down.  
“And there’s no other estate matter present?” I asked.  
He was taken aback. “What do you mean?”  
The bullshit detector started going off. He sounded too eager when he mentioned institutionalizing his wife.  
“Why lock her up?” I asked. “I don’t care but I need to know if I’m taking the case.”  
The man was hesitant. Then he relented.  
“Alright. There is a twenty million inheritance at stake along with a large tract of woodland in Montana,” he said.  
I nodded. This sounded like a big job. I could ask for more money.  
“Your family’s business is your business,” I said. “I’m just here to offer my service. No one can do neural extraction like I can; and I am very discreet.”  
The man beamed with a smile, relieved that he had found an ally.  
“However, because of the nature of the case I’ll need to charge more—” I said.  
“Of course, Mr. Valentine,” he said. “We’re ready to up the offer to two million. Get Claire and find evidence in the cult.”  
The amount was absurd by market standards, so there must be plenty of hidden obstacles. Nonetheless, two million blips for one job could get me off-map for twelve to fourteen months. The decision was easy.  
I took out my hand and shook his hand.  
“Mr. Dawn, you’ve just bought yourself a great asset,” I said.  
“Well I’m glad,” the man said. He collected his things and put on his artificer’s mask. His face adjusted into that of a Mediterranean man. He stepped outside of my office and was met by a chauffeur. I watched as the hovercraft took off. The crisp chilly night air of Neo San Francisco touched my skin and sculpted at my bone.

**Story 4: The Lyonessa’s Vigil (mythic guardian fragment)**

This was God’s message of winning inscribed on a rock tablet carried at her waist side. Her legs were caked up to her waist in thick, red mud. Her beast, a large horned lion, was equally caked with mud up to her shoulders and snout. The battle had been fierce, and after it blood had soaked the nearby river which flooded the land. Walking past it was wading through five days of mud and bodies.  
The Lyonessa, as she was called, lived near a patch of mountain land and beach with a bridge which she guards.  
The Bridge was the last connector between the land where her people lived and the land beyond of the Machine Mysterium. “Mechanical Magic,” they had also called it, the system that lived outside the land of human beings and how adaptive it was to survive.  
The Lyonessa carried two rifles loaded with shots, a spear, a shield, and a laser-sharpened tungsten blade sheathed against her thigh. She held two reins whilst riding the lion, although at a gallop, she could release the reins and take shots from the back of the galloping lion, hit her target, reload, and shoot again. She hunted for small game during the day, but for the most part, her energy came from bathing in the sun. Her skin was augmented with a photon-energizing pigment, so when her skin was exposed to the sunlight, it would take on a red to violet hue. Berries were abundant in the woods and swamps. And if she tired and wanted some meat, fish was abundant in the streams and so were fatty wild life. A duck was a savory meal and she loved hunting for them.  
She also enjoyed the solitude because the lion didn’t like the wetlands. Two nights ago was the last duck-hunt, she thought and wetted her lips, she’ll hunt this afternoon for a duck. Right now, she investigated a ringing siren.  
The dangling trapezoid of gongs and bells rang out through the forest. The Lyonessa crouched low over her lion as the pair stalked through the tall wetland grass, staying near to the rocky inland. The lion readied for a sprint. The Lyonessa shifted falling foliage with her spear to better see the view. It was clear, but something heavy had waded by. The ground was packed with its footprints. It was larger than a bear and had an extra pair of arms.  
The Lyonessa looked around at the trunks of the nearby trees and noticed nail marks an inch deep. These were the fang-like nails on its paws. She left the gong-bells ringing as her lion stalked the prey. The Lyonessa descended her mount and led with the spear and rifle held together. The trespassing beast had pushed two trunks apart in her stride, uprooting the healthy young trees.

**Story 5: World-Builder Genesis (Marcos Di Assisi vignette)**

Marcos Di Assisi took a drag of his Everpure cigar and exhaled deeply. An idea for the World Builder had come to him in the night. He got up and got into his chair. The atmosphere was quiet. A quiet piano started upon his waking and played gently in the background. He drew up his robe for the comfort around his collar. The image for the world engine flashed by in his dream again and when he stepped out, an immaculate idea had already splintered into fractured pieces. But he held onto some of those pieces.  
Marcos leaned over his desk with his elbows on the surface and for a moment didn’t speak. The sun was rising outside the tall slender window to his left. A world of mountains and trees revealed itself. He didn’t care much for it, although the concern of getting proper nutrients for his bones that have been sore lately did cross his mind. Focus.  
The World that came to his mind could let the players change the fabric of its reality.  
He stopped himself for a moment. What did that mean? Like those last words that fluttered out of a newly awakened as the dream slipped away, those words had made perfect sense just a minute ago.  
Then he remembered.  
There would be a world genie that held powers over changing the code of the world. That is real live code that runs the software. Which means, the player doesn’t own statistics. The player owns their own code of their world and the world is able to be rendered according to their code…  
He slowed down again. He had almost grasped the central idea. Marcos looked for a cup of water beside him. There was only a skim of water in the glass and it had been out for a day.  
Marcos went back to the central thought. “The new world will give players the ability to change its own physics. They will be able to manipulate mind and matter in this world…” The slogan still sounded too breezy. Then Marcos remembered the last image from his travel, a bellowing sand world that awaited for inhabitants and its own story. “And it would be set on a desert planet where surviving is tough.”
