Las Vegas in 2069 is no longer a city in the traditional sense—it is a precision-designed illusion, a fully operational engine of spectacle, data, and motion. Built atop the burnt bones of the Mojave and sustained by centuries of mythmaking, the city no longer reaches for the sky. Instead, it burrows downward. The Strip still exists, glowing with all the iconography of old Vegas, but it serves only as a surface layer—a shimmering crown atop a vast, multi-tiered civilization beneath.

Across the desert surrounding it, massive data centers stretch like microchip fabs, humming with the ceaseless work of computation—simulations, transactions, state surveillance, entire economies rendered into code. Earth’s cities run on fission now; true fusion remains the domain of Mars. Power is siphoned from colossal solar mirror fields, vast and blinding, arranged in geometric fields that shimmer like lakes. All of it fuels the illusion.

At the geographic and symbolic center of the city lies the Meadows, a climate-controlled haven engineered to perfection. There is no wind, no dust, no fluctuation. Flowers bloom on schedule. The temperature never rises or falls. This oasis exists for the rich, the famous, the preserved—for those who pay to never feel the desert.

Beneath this curated paradise lies the true Vegas: ninety-nine levels carved into the earth in concentric rings, spiraling into a monumental void known as the Pit. This is the real city. Vegas builds down, not up. Subsurface gantries on the city’s outer edges receive hundreds of orbital ships per day. Tourists are sorted and assigned tiers based on status and spend. Vast tunnels and high-speed loops ferry them between the docks and the central zone, where the experience begins.

The city’s most visible landmarks are its floating hotels—immense monoliths suspended magnetically above the skyline. These structures move in real time, adjusting their location based on population flow. When demand spikes, a hotel will descend, lock into the Pit, and open its base to receive travelers directly into the undercity. The mobility of these monoliths is essential. Vegas is alive, and it shifts its bones daily.

Every person in Vegas wears an artifice by default. Faces are smoothed, blemishes corrected, eyes brightened, gestures stylized. These filters aren’t digital overlays—they are atmospheric, projected into space by the Sphere Network. Originally built as entertainment domes, the Spheres now operate in unison to create a permanent augmented reality over the city. This projection—known as Māyā—casts dragons into the sky, renders buildings in shimmering gold, and reconfigures the very light in the air. The illusion consumes staggering amounts of power. But in Vegas, reality isn’t the point.

Access to the real city depends on your status. First-world tourists arrive in person, physically present. Second-tier visitors pilot android bodies, remote-controlled skinsuits. The third tier never leave their homes—they stream into Vegas through low-res VR feeds, experiencing only curated replicas of what happens underground. These visitors are invisible to the system. They do not count.

At the edge of the Strip stands Sentinel Tower, the local arm of New Ethics. It watches everything: eye tracking, biometrics, emotional telemetry, artificial enhancements. Every movement is logged. Every presence is priced. Every deviation is cataloged. In this city, data is currency—and surveillance is hospitality.

Vegas in 2069 is not decayed. It is perfected. A city not of dreams, but of dream-selling. A Pit where fantasy isn’t free—it’s licensed, lit, and leveled. And in the heart of it, beneath all the filters and facades, is one truth: no one enters Vegas as themselves.
