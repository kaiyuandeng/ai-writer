Marcos has the walk of a martial artist — spring-loaded, private, and confident.
His eyes are usually cast down or off to the side. Each step lands parallel to the next, and when he rests, he does so on one leg, the other bent at the knee, foot lounging on the floor like it’s posing for a portrait.
This is the man who wrote The Stellar Rose. Composed the symphony. Directed the opera. And finally sold the rights to Magic Incorporated — after which he disappeared to a man-made island off the coast of San Francisco called the Dragon Den.
Yes — his friends tried to talk him out of the name. He made the case that Fortress of Solitude wouldn’t be any better. They argued. They said he was comparing himself to Superman. He said Superman had superperson privileges. They said Dragon had too many racial connotations. Solitude was more vague, more tasteful.
Marcos, in a rare moment of personal collection, said they were right.
And still, when the news reported on his parties or burglaries, it was always framed as: “Marcos De Anza’s debaucherous bachelor pad — where he may have spliced and cloned a dragon.”
And not a dinosaur. A dragon. A serpent that breathes fire.
Officially and unofficially, that had a much cooler tone to it.
But privately, they still called it the Dragon Den. Or just: the Island.
