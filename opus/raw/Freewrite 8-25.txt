[Sword Fight Scene in the Jian Empire’s Western Capital]

In the walled garden of the Jian Empire’s Western Capital, where cherry blossoms drift over lotus ponds and ornate pavilions cast dappled shadows, Anais and Ahrah faced each other across a sparring circle. The training ground, nestled near the palace gates, hummed with the rustle of bamboo and the distant chime of temple bells, evoking the serene sprawl of a long-forgotten Golden Gate Park. Their light, thin foils gleamed faintly under the diffused light, designed to nick skin but not pierce deeply—perfect for training post-human girls whose rapid recovery grew sharper with every practiced wound.

“Nala’s back,” Anais said to Ahrah. Ahrah twirled her foil, testing its balance. They stood close, mirroring each other, separated by the chalked boundary of the sparring circle. Ahrah flicked her foil in precise arcs, the blade humming as it cut the air. She played with its motion, slicing downward and snapping it back with a twist of her wrist, like a yoyo returning to her hand. She struck again, controlled, deliberate cuts. Her eyes locked on the foil’s tip, noting the faint etchings on its surface. The soft earth muffled each step as she shifted her weight.

“Did you hear me? Nala—” Anais said.

“I heard you,” Ahrah replied. She glanced at her sister. Anais wore a light training skirt and a floral blouse, its hem adorned with pleated strips. “How are you going to move in that?”

Anais looked down at her skirt. “What, this? What do you mean?”

Ahrah tilted her head. “The frills,” she said. Anais glanced over her shoulder at the pleated strips sewn onto her blouse.

“My wings?” Anais said with a smile, her nonchalance meant to needle her sister.

“We’re wasting time, c’mon,” Ahrah said. She stepped back from the circle’s edge and lunged gently, sending a probing thrust toward Anais.

Anais parried the strike with a flick of her foil, redirecting it smoothly.

“What should we do?” Anais asked.

“Do?” Ahrah said. She pivoted, planting her right foot, and twisted her body in a fluid, effortless motion, whipping her foil in a swift arc. The blade sang as it sliced toward Anais, who stepped aside just in time, the tip grazing the air where she’d stood.

“Hey!” Anais said. “Light sparring! We’re warming up.”

“We are warming up,” Ahrah said. “You missed it.”

Anais puffed out her chest, planted her feet, and crouched lower, her foil raised. Ahrah drew another foil from her belt—a spare for training—and thrust it gently toward Anais. Anais countered with a smooth, light parry. Ahrah responded with a quick lunge, shallow but sharp, forcing Anais to step forward. Anais parried again, catching the blade a beat after it extended, and Ahrah deflected her counterstrike.

“Do you think she’s mastered Water Absorption?” Anais asked.

Ahrah shook her head. “Impossible,” she said. She thrust her foil again.

“I think she’s done it,” Anais replied.

“Why?” Ahrah asked. She feinted high, her foil darting upward.

Anais stepped back, raising her foil overhead in a graceful arc to deflect the strike. “She promised me she’d be back when it was done,” Anais said, returning a quick thrust.

Ahrah grimaced, holding her breath, and parried flat, her foil meeting Anais’s with a sharp clink. Anais anticipated the move, angling her blade upward to push Ahrah’s strike skyward. Ahrah stepped back, raising her foil overhead, loading her weight onto her back leg. Anais felt a surge of fear—Ahrah was preparing a powerful thrust. The garden seemed to still, the air heavy. Ahrah turned her body sideways, her foil raised high in her right hand like a poised javelin. She tracked Anais’s stance, her eyes narrowing. Anais shifted defensively to the circle’s edge. Ahrah picked her target—a precise spot near Anais’s feet, difficult to counter. She rose onto the balls of her feet, her focus absolute, the world narrowing to the glint of her foil’s tip.

A sudden gust swept through the garden, rustling the cherry blossoms and lifting Anais’s skirt. The clouds parted, and a beam of sunlight struck Ahrah’s eyes, blinding her momentarily. Her hearts skipped, her chest tightened. She lunged, a fraction too late.

The foil’s tip grazed Anais’s right cheek, drawing a thin line of blood. Anais cried out and dropped to her knees, clutching her face.

Ahrah let her foil fall and leaped across the circle. “I’m so sorry,” she said, rushing to Anais, who knelt on the soft earth, hand pressed to her cheek. Ahrah reached for her, asking, “Are you alright—”

“Don’t touch me!” Anais shouted, flinging out her arm. A sudden gust, as if summoned, roared through the garden and knocked Ahrah to the ground.
